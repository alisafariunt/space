🎯 Week 2 in Plain English

The Main Question: How do we know if different treatments (teaching methods, medications, etc.) actually produce different results?

The Answer: ANOVA (Analysis of Variance)

  • Step 1: Compare how different the GROUP AVERAGES are
  • Step 2: Compare that to how different people are WITHIN each group
  • Step 3: If between-group differences are much bigger than within-group differences → treatments work!

📐 Part 1: Completely Randomized Design (CRD) Fundamentals

1.1 What is a Completely Randomized Design?

A Completely Randomized Design (CRD) is the simplest experimental design for comparing the effects of multiple treatments. It has the following characteristics:

  • One Factor (Independent Variable): The design involves one treatment factor with two or more levels
  • Random Assignment: Participants are randomly assigned to treatment groups
  • Independent Observations: Each participant receives only one treatment
  • No Blocking: Unlike RBD or Latin Square, there are no nuisance variables controlled
Key Concept: # factors = # IVs = # "ways"

A one-way ANOVA has 1 factor (e.g., teaching method with 3 levels)
A two-way ANOVA has 2 factors (e.g., teaching method × time of day)
A three-way ANOVA has 3 factors (e.g., teaching method × time × location)

1.2 Design Notation

A CRD with p treatment levels is denoted as CR-p:

  • CR-2: 2 treatment levels (e.g., control vs. experimental)
  • CR-3: 3 treatment levels (e.g., three types of instruction)
  • CR-4: 4 treatment levels (e.g., four levels of food deprivation)

1.3 When to Use CRD vs. Other Designs

Design When to Use Advantages Disadvantages
CRD Homogeneous participants, no known nuisance variables Simple analysis, maximum error df, broadly representative Larger error variance if nuisance variables exist
RBD One nuisance variable to control (e.g., matched pairs) Reduces error variance, more powerful if blocking effective Requires identifying meaningful blocks, loses df
Latin Square Two nuisance variables to control simultaneously Controls two sources of variation efficiently Restrictive assumptions, limited flexibility, loses more df

From Kirk Exercise 2a (Page 71): The effects of three kinds of instruction (α₁, α₂, α₃) on first-grade students' tendency to help another child were investigated. Forty-two boys were randomly assigned to the three kinds of instructions with 14 boys in each group.

  • α₁: Indirect responsibility group
  • α₂: Direct responsibility group
  • α₃: Second direct responsibility group (with calls for help)

This is a CR-3 design because there is one factor (instruction type) with 3 levels, and boys are randomly assigned to groups.

🧮 Part 2: ANOVA Model for CRD

2.1 The Effects Model (Fixed Effects)

For a completely randomized design with p treatment levels and n observations per treatment, the effects model is:

Fixed Effects Model: 
Yij = μ + αj + εi(j)

where:
  Yij = observation i in treatment j
  μ = grand mean (overall population mean)
  αj = treatment effect for level j
  εi(j) = error effect for observation i in treatment j
  i = 1, ..., n (observations within treatment)
  j = 1, ..., p (treatment levels)

2.2 Partition of a Single Score

Every observation can be decomposed into three components:

Yij = Ȳ.. + (Ȳ.j - Ȳ..) + (Yij - Ȳ.j)

Score = Grand Mean + Treatment Effect + Error Effect
🎯 Simple Example: Imagine testing three teaching methods with this student's score:
  • Student's test score (Yij) = 85
  • Overall average across all methods (Ȳ..) = 75
  • Average for this teaching method (Ȳ.j) = 82

Breaking it down:

  • Grand Mean: 75 (baseline for everyone)
  • Treatment Effect: 82 - 75 = +7 (this method adds 7 points on average)
  • Error Effect: 85 - 82 = +3 (this student scored 3 points above their method's average)

Check: 75 + 7 + 3 = 85 ✓

Component Symbol Interpretation
Grand Mean Ȳ.. Mean of all observations across all treatments
Treatment Effect (Ȳ.j - Ȳ..) Deviation of treatment mean from grand mean
Error Effect (Yij - Ȳ.j) Deviation of observation from its treatment mean (unexplained variation)

2.3 Model Assumptions

The CR-p design requires four key assumptions about the error term εi(j):

  1. Normality: Data come from normally distributed populations within each treatment level
  2. Independence: Observations within and between cells are independent (or at least randomly assigned)
  3. Homogeneity of Variance: All treatment populations have equal variance (σ²ε is constant across j)
  4. Additivity: Treatment and error effects are additive
Important: These are the same assumptions as for regression models. The error term in ANOVA has the same characteristics as the error term in regression:
  • E(εi(j)) = 0
  • Var(εi(j)) = σ²ε (constant)
  • εi(j) ~ N(0, σ²ε)
  • εi(j) independent of εi'(j')

2.4 Fixed Effects vs. Random Effects

Aspect Fixed Effects Model Random Effects Model
Treatment levels All levels of interest are included Levels are random sample from larger population
αj interpretation Fixed constants: Σαj = 0 Random variables: E(αj) = 0, Var(αj) = σ²α
Inference About specific treatments tested About population of treatments
Example Three specific teaching methods we want to compare Three randomly selected teachers from a school district

📈 Part 3: Sums of Squares and ANOVA Table

3.1 Understanding Variation in ANOVA

The fundamental principle of ANOVA is partitioning the total variation into between-group and within-group components: 

SST = SSB + SSW

Total Variation = Between-group + Within-group
                  Variation       Variation
💡 Simple Analogy:

Imagine test scores from 3 classrooms (A, B, C):

  • SST (Total): How spread out are ALL students' scores? Some got 60, some got 95, etc.
  • SSB (Between): Are the classroom averages different? (Class A avg = 85, Class B avg = 75, Class C avg = 80)
  • SSW (Within): How spread out are scores WITHIN each classroom? (Even in Class A, some students got 70 while others got 95)

ANOVA asks: Is the variation BETWEEN classrooms (SSB) large compared to the variation WITHIN classrooms (SSW)?

3.2 Bracket Notation for Sums of Squares

Kirk uses bracket notation to simplify calculations. Three key terms are needed:

Notation Formula Meaning
[AS] ΣΣ Y²ij Sum of squared individual observations
[A] Σ(Σ Yij)² / n Square treatment totals, divide by n, then sum
[Y] (ΣΣ Yij)² / np Square grand total, divide by total sample size
Key Formulas:
  • SST = [AS] - [Y] (Total sum of squares)
  • SSB = [A] - [Y] (Between-group sum of squares)
  • SSW = [AS] - [A] (Within-group sum of squares)

These formulas work because: SST = SSB + SSW

3.3 Degrees of Freedom

  • dfTotal = np - 1 (total observations minus 1)
  • dfBetween = p - 1 (number of groups minus 1)
  • dfWithin = np - p = p(n - 1) (total observations minus number of groups)

3.4 Mean Squares

Mean squares are sums of squares divided by their degrees of freedom:

  • MSB = SSB / (p - 1)
  • MSW = SSW / [p(n - 1)]

3.5 Complete ANOVA Table Structure

Source SS df MS F
Between Groups SSB = [A] - [Y] p - 1 MSB = SSB/(p-1) F = MSB/MSW
Within Groups (Error) SSW = [AS] - [A] p(n - 1) MSW = SSW/[p(n-1)]
Total SST = [AS] - [Y] np - 1

3.6 SAS Code for One-Way ANOVA

/* One-Way ANOVA in SAS */
PROC GLM DATA=mydata;
    CLASS treatment;
    MODEL response = treatment;
    MEANS treatment / HOVTEST WELCH;
RUN;
QUIT;

3.7 R Code for One-Way ANOVA

# One-Way ANOVA in R
library(car)

# Fit the model
model <- lm(response ~ treatment, data = mydata)

# ANOVA table
anova(model)

# Levene's test for homogeneity of variance
leveneTest(response ~ treatment, data = mydata)

🎯 Part 4: Expected Mean Squares and F-Test Justification

4.1 Expected Values of Error Terms

Understanding the expected values of error terms is crucial for understanding the F-test:

  • E(εi(j)) = 0
  • E(Σεi(j)) = 0 (sum of errors in a group)
  • E(Σε²i(j)) = nσ²ε (sum of squared errors)
  • E(ΣΣε²i(j)) = npσ²ε (sum of all squared errors)

4.2 Expected Mean Squares (Fixed Effects Model)

For Fixed Effects CR-p Design: 
E(MSB) = σ²ε + n·Σα²j/(p-1)

E(MSW) = σ²ε

E(F) ≈ E(MSB)/E(MSW) = [σ²ε + n·Σα²j/(p-1)] / σ²ε

Key Insight: If H₀ is true (all αj = 0), then E(F) ≈ 1.

4.3 Expected Mean Squares (Random Effects Model)

For Random Effects CR-p Design: 
E(MSB) = σ²ε + nσ²α

E(MSW) = σ²ε

E(F) ≈ E(MSB)/E(MSW) = (σ²ε + nσ²α) / σ²ε

Key Insight: If H₀ is true (σ²α = 0), then E(F) ≈ 1.

Important Observation: For the CR-p design, both fixed and random effects models lead to the same F-ratio structure. This will NOT always be true for more complex designs!

4.4 Why the F-Test Works

The F-statistic tests whether MSB is significantly larger than MSW:

  1. MSW always estimates σ²ε (the within-group variance)
  2. MSB estimates σ²ε PLUS treatment effects
    • If treatments have no effect: E(MSB) = σ²ε, so F ≈ 1
    • If treatments differ: E(MSB) > σ²ε, so F > 1
  3. Large F values indicate treatments have different effects
🔍 Intuitive Explanation:

Think of ANOVA like this:

  • Within-group variation (MSW): How much do people differ within the same treatment? This is just random noise.
  • Between-group variation (MSB): How much do the group averages differ from each other?

The Logic:

  • If treatments do nothing → group means differ only due to random chance → MSB ≈ MSW → F ≈ 1
  • If treatments have real effects → group means differ MORE than random chance → MSB > MSW → F > 1

Example: Testing 3 pain medications:

  • MSW = 4 (people taking the same med still differ by about 4 points due to individual variation)
  • MSB = 12 (the three medication averages differ by much more than 4 points)
  • F = 12/4 = 3 (the between-group differences are 3× larger than within-group noise → medications likely have different effects!)

4.5 Interactive Q&A Examples

Q1: For any typical significance level, if a computed value of the F statistic for a one-way ANOVA is less than or equal to one, we would:
  1. Always reject the null hypothesis
  2. Always fail to reject the null hypothesis ✓
  3. Either reject or fail to reject depending on degrees of freedom
Answer: (2) Since E(F) ≈ 1 when H₀ is true, F ≤ 1 provides no evidence against H₀.
Q2: The expected mean square due to the treatment in a one-way ANOVA consists of:
  1. Variation due to the treatment levels
  2. Variation due to the treatment levels AND the error term ✓
  3. Variation only due to the error term
Answer: (2) E(MSB) = σ²ε + [treatment effect term]

🇮🇷 آماره F و معنی آن

آماره F نسبت واریانس بین گروهی به واریانس درون گروهی است.

  • اگر تیمارها تأثیری نداشته باشند: F تقریباً برابر 1 خواهد بود
  • اگر تیمارها متفاوت باشند: F بزرگتر از 1 خواهد بود
  • MSB همیشه شامل σ²ε به علاوه اثر تیمار است
  • MSW فقط واریانس درون گروهی (σ²ε) را برآورد می‌کند

وقتی F ≤ 1 باشد، هیچ شواهدی علیه فرض صفر وجود ندارد.

🔄 Part 5: Regression Representation of ANOVA

5.1 One-Way ANOVA as Regression with Dummy Variables

A one-way ANOVA with one factor is equivalent to a regression model with one qualitative variable (represented by dummy variables).

💡 The Big Picture:

ANOVA and regression are actually the SAME thing! Here's how:

  • ANOVA: Compare means across groups (e.g., 3 teaching methods)
  • Regression: Use dummy variables (0/1) to represent groups
    • Method A: x₁=1, x₂=0
    • Method B: x₁=0, x₂=1
    • Method C: x₁=0, x₂=0 (reference group)

Why this matters: You can run ANOVA using regression software! The F-test is the same, p-values are the same.

Q: A standard "One-Way ANOVA" with one factor is equivalent to:
  1. A regression model in which there is one dummy variable
  2. A regression model in which the dependent variable is a dummy variable
  3. A regression model in which there is one qualitative variable ✓

5.2 The Appraiser Problem: Full Example

Scenario: Four appraisers (A, B, C, D) each assess 10 properties. We want to test whether appraisers differ systematically in their assessments.

This is an RB-10 design (Randomized Block with 10 blocks, where blocks are properties).

Regression Model Specification:

E(y) = β₀ + β₁x₁ + β₂x₂ + β₃x₃ + β₄x₄ + β₅x₅ + ... + β₁₂x₁₂
       \_____Treatment______/   \________Blocks (Properties)________/
         (Appraisers)

where:
  x₁ = 1 if appraiser A, 0 otherwise
  x₂ = 1 if appraiser B, 0 otherwise
  x₃ = 1 if appraiser C, 0 otherwise
  (Appraiser D is reference category)

  x₄ = 1 if property 1, 0 otherwise
  x₅ = 1 if property 2, 0 otherwise
  ...
  x₁₂ = 1 if property 9, 0 otherwise
  (Property 10 is reference category)

5.3 Interpretation of β Coefficients

Coefficient Interpretation
β₀ μD + μ10 (mean for Appraiser D on Property 10)
β₁ μA - μD (difference between Appraiser A and D)
β₂ μB - μD (difference between Appraiser B and D)
β₃ μC - μD (difference between Appraiser C and D)
β₄ μ1 - μ10 (difference between Property 1 and 10)
β₅, ..., β₁₂ μ2 - μ10, ..., μ9 - μ10 (other property differences)

5.4 Hypothesis Testing in Regression Form

To test differences among appraisers:

H₀: μ_A = μ_B = μ_C = μ_D

Equivalent to:

H₀: β₁ = β₂ = β₃ = 0
H_A: β_i ≠ 0 for some i ∈ {1, 2, 3}

To test differences among properties:

H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ = ... = μ₉ = μ₁₀

Equivalent to:

H₀: β₄ = β₅ = ... = β₁₂ = 0
H_A: β_i ≠ 0 for some i ∈ {4, 5, ..., 12}

5.5 Why This Matters

Key Insight: Understanding the connection between ANOVA and regression helps you:
  • Interpret regression output when dealing with categorical predictors
  • Understand how software codes categorical variables
  • Extend ANOVA to more complex models (ANCOVA, mixed models)
  • Use regression software for ANOVA problems

⚖️ Part 6: Homogeneity of Variance (HOV) Testing

6.1 Why Is HOV Important?

ANOVA assumes all treatment populations have equal variance: σ²₁ = σ²₂ = ... = σ²p

H₀: σ²₁ = σ²₂ = ... = σ²p (homogeneity of variance)

🎯 What Does "Equal Variance" Mean?

Imagine testing 3 weight-loss programs:

  • Program A: Everyone loses 5-7 lbs (variance = 1 lb²)
  • Program B: People lose 0-15 lbs (variance = 25 lb²)
  • Program C: Everyone loses 5-8 lbs (variance = 1.5 lb²)

Problem: Program B has much higher variance (more spread). ANOVA assumes all programs have similar spreads.

Why it matters: If variances differ a lot, ANOVA's p-values become unreliable!

Why This Matters:
  • ANOVA is robust to moderate violations when group sizes are equal
  • With unequal n, violations can seriously affect Type I error rates
  • Larger variances in smaller groups → inflated Type I error
  • Larger variances in larger groups → reduced power

6.2 Rule of Thumb

Simple Rule: If the ratio of largest to smallest group variances exceeds 4, use an alternative procedure. 
Fmax = s²largest / s²smallest

If Fmax > 4: Consider alternatives (Welch test, transformations)

The more unequal the group sizes, the more important it is to follow this rule.

6.3 Hartley's Fmax Test

Generalizes the F-test for two variances to p variances:

Fmax = s²largest / s²smallest

Critical values depend on:

  • Number of groups (p)
  • Degrees of freedom per group (n - 1)
  • Significance level (α)
Caution: Hartley's test is sensitive to non-normality. If data are clearly non-normal, use Levene or Brown-Forsythe tests instead.

6.4 Levene's Test

Procedure:

  1. Replace each observation Yij with |Yij - Ȳj| (absolute deviation from group mean)
  2. Run a one-way ANOVA on these transformed values
  3. If ANOVA is significant, reject homogeneity of variance
Advantages:
  • Less sensitive to non-normality than Hartley's test
  • Works with unequal sample sizes
  • Available in most statistical software

6.5 Brown-Forsythe Test

Modification of Levene's test that uses deviations from the median instead of the mean:

  1. Replace each observation with |Yij - Mj| where Mj is the group median
  2. Run a one-way ANOVA on these values

Advantage: Even more robust to non-normality than Levene's test.

6.6 Welch's Test for ANOVA Means

When HOV is violated, use Welch's adjusted F-test instead of standard ANOVA:

W* = Σwj(Ȳj - μ̂)² / (k-1)
     ────────────────────────────────
     1 + [2(k-2)/(k²-1)]Σhj

where:
  wj = nj/s²j (weight inversely proportional to variance)
  μ̂ = ΣwjȲj / Σwj (weighted grand mean)
  hj = (1 - wj/W)² / (nj - 1)
  f = k² - 1 / [3Σhj] (adjusted degrees of freedom)

Reject H₀ if: W* exceeds Fα,k-1,f

6.7 SAS and SPSS Output

SAS Code:

PROC GLM DATA=mydata;
    CLASS treatment;
    MODEL response = treatment;
    MEANS treatment / HOVTEST WELCH;
RUN;

Interpreting Output:

Test Null Hypothesis When to Use
Levene Variances are equal If p > .05: retain H₀, use standard ANOVA
If p < .05: reject H₀, use Welch or Brown-Forsythe
Welch Means are equal (unequal variances OK) Use when Levene test is significant
Brown-Forsythe Means are equal (unequal variances OK) Alternative to Welch, slightly more robust

Example interpretation: If Levene statistic = 6.540 with p = .012, we reject homogeneity of variance and should use Welch's test (or Brown-Forsythe) instead of standard ANOVA.

🔀 Part 7: Data Transformations

🤔 Why Transform Data?

Sometimes your data violates ANOVA assumptions (non-normal, unequal variances). Instead of giving up, you can transform the data to fix the problem!

Simple Example:

  • Original data: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 100 (very skewed, large variance)
  • After square root: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 10 (less skewed, more equal spread)

Key idea: Transform → Run ANOVA → Interpret results (in transformed scale or convert back)

7.1 The Power Transformation Family

Any transformation of the form y = xp is a member of the power transformation family:

p value Transformation Name When to Use
p = 2 y = x² Square Rarely used (increases skewness)
p = 1 y = x No transformation Data already meet assumptions
p = 0.5 y = √x Square root Count data, means ∝ variances
p = 0 y = ln(x) Logarithmic Positively skewed data, means ∝ std deviations
p = -0.5 y = 1/√x Inverse square root Severely skewed data
p = -1 y = 1/x Reciprocal Reaction time data, mean² ∝ std deviation
p = -2 y = 1/x² Reciprocal square Extreme skewness

7.2 Rules of Thumb for Selecting Transformations

Square Root Transformation (√x)

  • Sometimes used for count data
  • May be helpful if means are proportional to the variances

Logarithmic Transformation (ln x)

  • Sometimes used for reaction time data or positively skewed data
  • May be helpful if means are proportional to the standard deviations

Reciprocal Transformation (1/x)

  • Sometimes used for reaction time data
  • May be helpful if the square of the means are proportional to the standard deviations

7.3 Plotting Variances to Determine Transformation

Procedure:

  1. Plot the variance of each group (y-axis) by the group mean (x-axis)
  2. Draw a least squares line through the points
  3. Find the slope β
  4. Use p = 1 - β to determine the transformation
  5. Round p to the nearest value from the transformation family
Example:
  • If slope β = 0, then p = 1 - 0 = 1 → No transformation needed
  • If slope β = 0.5, then p = 1 - 0.5 = 0.5 → Square root transformation
  • If slope β = 1, then p = 1 - 1 = 0 → Log transformation
  • If slope β = 2, then p = 1 - 2 = -1 → Reciprocal transformation

7.4 Smallest Range Criteria

Alternative method: Try multiple transformations and select the one that produces the smallest ratio of largest range to smallest range.

Transformation Treatment 1 Treatment 2 Treatment 3 Range Ratio
Original (y) Range = 4 Range = 5 Range = 6 6/4 = 1.50
Log (ln y) Range = 1.609 Range = 1.253 Range = 0.693 1.609/0.693 = 2.23
√y Range = 1.236 Range = 1.232 Range = 0.974 1.236/0.974 = 1.27 ✓ BEST
1/y Range = 0.800 Range = 0.357 Range = 0.083 0.800/0.083 = 9.65

7.5 Important Considerations

Caution with Transformations:
  • Interpretation changes: Conclusions apply to transformed data, not original scale
  • May distort meaning: Log of salary is not the same as salary
  • Not always applicable: Some transformations require positive data
  • Results may differ: Significant on one scale may not be on another

📊 Part 8: Nonparametric Alternatives

8.1 When to Use Nonparametric Tests

🛤️ Quick Decision Guide: 
START: Comparing 3+ groups

Step 1: Check Variances (Levene test)
├─ Equal variances?
│  ├─ YES → Step 2
│  └─ NO → Use Welch's ANOVA

Step 2: Check Normality (Shapiro-Wilk, Q-Q plot)
├─ Normal data?
│  ├─ YES → Use Standard ANOVA ✅
│  └─ NO → Step 3

Step 3: Try Transformation
├─ Transformation fixes it?
│  ├─ YES → ANOVA on transformed data
│  └─ NO → Use Kruskal-Wallis test (nonparametric)
Q: A non-parametric ANOVA should primarily be used when which conditions are violated?
  1. Normality ✓
  2. Independence of observations
  3. Homogeneity of variance (use Welch instead)
  4. Any of the above
Answer: (1) Nonparametric tests are primarily for normality violations. For HOV violations, use Welch or transformations. Independence violations require different approaches.

8.2 Kruskal-Wallis Test

The Kruskal-Wallis test is the nonparametric alternative to one-way ANOVA:

  • Multi-group equivalent of the Mann-Whitney U test
  • Also called "ANOVA by ranks"
  • Data must be at least ordinal scale

Procedure:

  1. Rank all observations in the entire data set (smallest = 1)
  2. Perform an ANOVA on the rank scores for each group
  3. Test statistic approximately follows χ² distribution

Assumptions:

  • No normality assumption - main advantage!
  • Shape assumption: Distribution shape must be equal for all groups (weaker version of HOV)
  • No parameter estimates: Cannot construct confidence intervals

8.3 Other Nonparametric Tests

Test Design Parametric Equivalent
Wilcoxon sign-rank Paired samples (2 groups) Paired t-test
Mann-Whitney U (Wilcoxon sum-rank) Independent samples (2 groups) Independent t-test
Kruskal-Wallis Independent samples (3+ groups) One-way ANOVA
Friedman test Repeated measures (3+ groups) Repeated-measures ANOVA

8.4 Guidelines for Choosing Alternative Tests

Method When Appropriate Advantages Disadvantages
Parametric ANOVA Normal/symmetrical data, equal variances, no outliers Most powerful when assumptions met, most familiar Wrong results when assumptions not met
Welch F* Normal/symmetrical data, no outliers (unequal variances OK) Fewer assumptions, more powerful in typical data Less familiar
Transformations Transformed data: normal/symmetrical, homogeneous variances, no outliers Permits use of familiar parametric tests May distort meaning, can't always apply, conclusions apply to transformed scale
Kruskal-Wallis Similar shape distributions (weak HOV assumption) Does not distort data, can use ordinal data Loses information, may be less powerful, less familiar

DATA Treat2;
    SET mydata;
    resp = Treat2;
RUN;

DATA Treat3;
    SET mydata;
    resp = Treat3;
RUN;

/* Combine and add grouping variable */
DATA myAnovaData;
    SET Treat1 Treat2 Treat3;
    IF _N_ <= 27 THEN Level = 3;
    IF _N_ <= 18 THEN Level = 2;
    IF _N_ <= 9 THEN Level = 1;
    KEEP resp Level;
RUN;

Step 3: Run One-Way ANOVA with HOV Tests

/* One-way ANOVA with Levene and Welch tests */
PROC GLM DATA=myAnovaData;
    CLASS Level;
    MODEL resp = Level;
    MEANS Level / HOVTEST WELCH;
RUN;
QUIT;

10.1 Kirk Page 72 Exercise 2a - Three Types of Instruction

Problem: The effects of three kinds of instruction on first-grade students' tendency to help another child were investigated. Forty-two boys were randomly assigned to three instruction types (14 per group).

Solution:

  • (i) Design: CR-3 (Completely Randomized with 3 treatment levels)
  • (ii) Hypotheses: H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ = μ₃
  • (iii) Model: Yij = μ + αj + εi(j), i=1,...,14, j=1,2,3

10.2 Kirk Page 72 Exercise 2c - Mongolian Gerbils

Problem: Effects of isolation at 90, 120, 150, and 180 days of age on subsequent combative behavior of Mongolian gerbils were investigated. Eighty gerbils randomly assigned to four isolation conditions (20 each).

Solution:

  • (i) Design: CR-4
  • (ii) Hypotheses: H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ = μ₃ = μ₄
  • (iii) Model: Yij = μ + αj + εi(j), i=1,...,20, j=1,2,3,4

10.3 Kirk Page 72 Exercise 2d - Conforming-Compulsive Scale

Problem: Scores on the Conforming-Compulsive scale were used to form blocks of three boys with similar scores. The three boys in each block were randomly assigned to three treatment conditions.

  • (ii) Hypotheses: H₀: μ.1 = μ.2 = μ.3
  • (iii) Model: Yij = μ + αj + πi + εij, i=1,...,14, j=1,2,3

10.4 Kirk Page 72 Exercise 2e - French vs. English Canadian Dreams

Solution:

  • (i) Design: Paired t-test for dependent samples (RB-2 design)
  • (ii) Hypotheses: H₀: μ.1 ≤ μ.2 (directional hypothesis)
  • (iii) Model: Yij = μ + αj + πi + εij, i=1,...,50, j=1,2

Note: Directional hypothesis requires t-test, not F-test.

10.5 Kirk Page 72 Exercise 2f - Human Faces with Emotions

Solution:

  • (i) Design: CRF-6,2 (Completely Randomized Factorial: 6 emotions × 2 sides)
  • (ii) Hypotheses:
    • H₀: μ1. = μ2. = ... = μ6. (emotion main effect)
    • H₀: μ.1 ≤ μ.2 (side main effect, directional)
  • (iii) Model: Yijk = μ + αj + βk + (αβ)jk + εi(jk), i=1,...,50, j=1,...,6, k=1,2

10.6 Kirk Page 73 Exercise 9 - Interaction Example

Problem: Data on running time (seconds) in a straight-alley maze obtained in a CRF-33 design (3 magnitudes of reinforcement × 3 hours of deprivation).

Small (α₁) Medium (α₂) Large (α₃)
10 hrs (b₁) Ȳ = 9 Ȳ = 7 Ȳ = 6
15 hrs (b₂) Ȳ = 8 Ȳ = 7 Ȳ = 5
20 hrs (b₃) Ȳ = 5 Ȳ = 5 Ȳ = 5

Verbal Description of Interaction:

For the third level of deprivation (20 hours), there is no effect due to reinforcement - all means are equal at 5 seconds. However, for the two lower levels of deprivation (10 and 15 hours), running time is negatively related to magnitude of reinforcement - larger reinforcement leads to faster running times (lower seconds).

This is a clear interaction because the effect of reinforcement depends on the level of deprivation.

10.7 Kirk Page 118 Exercise 5 - Summation Rules

Purpose: Illustrate how Σ(Yi - μ)² can be partitioned.

Key Result:

Σ(Yi - μ)² = Σ(Yi - Ȳ + Ȳ - μ)²
            = Σ(Yi - Ȳ)² + 2(Ȳ - μ)Σ(Yi - Ȳ) + n(Ȳ - μ)²
            = Σ(Yi - Ȳ)² + n(Ȳ - μ)²   [middle term = 0]

This demonstrates the fundamental ANOVA partition at the population level.

10.8 Kirk Page 120 Exercise 16 - Fixed vs. Random Effects

Solutions:

  • (a) Random samples of White and Black employed mothers: FIXED effects (specific groups of interest)
  • (b) 20 Wistar rats with randomly selected injection times: RANDOM effects (times are random sample)
  • (c) Effects of 1, 2, 3, or 4 social science courses: FIXED effects (specific course counts)
  • (d) Children classified as severely, moderately, mildly, or nonhandicapped: FIXED effects (specific categories)
  • (e) Eight randomly selected doses of testosterone: RANDOM effects (doses are random sample)

✅ Part 11: Key Concepts and Takeaways

11.1 Critical Concepts Checklist

  • Understand when F ≤ 1 means always fail to reject H₀ (no evidence against null)
  • Expected MS for treatment (MSB) includes BOTH σ²ε AND treatment effect
  • Variables must be INDEPENDENT for sum of variances to equal variance of sum
  • One-way ANOVA is equivalent to regression with one QUALITATIVE variable (dummy coded)
  • Nonparametric tests are primarily for NORMALITY violations (not HOV)
  • Use Welch/Brown-Forsythe tests when HOV assumption is violated
  • Fixed effects: specific treatment levels of interest included in study
  • Random effects: treatment levels are random sample from larger population
  • SSE for RB/LS is always ≤ SSE for CRD (if blocking is effective)
  • CRD provides maximum error df but may have larger error variance

11.2 Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Don't:
  • Confuse MSB with MSW - they estimate different things!
  • Use Hartley's test with non-normal data
  • Apply transformations without considering interpretation changes
  • Forget that ANOVA assumptions are identical to regression assumptions
  • Think nonparametric tests are for HOV violations (use Welch instead)
  • Ignore the connection between ANOVA and regression with dummy variables
✓ Do:
  • Always check assumptions before interpreting ANOVA results
  • Use Levene or Brown-Forsythe tests to assess HOV
  • Consider the practical significance of transformations on interpretation
  • Understand that F-ratio works because E(MSB) includes treatment effects
  • Remember that blocking removes variation IF nuisance variable is effective

11.3 Formula Summary

Concept Formula

SST Partition SST = SSB + SSW
F-ratio F = MSB / MSW
