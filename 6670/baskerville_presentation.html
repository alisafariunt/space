📚 Baskerville, Kaul & Storey (2015)

Genres of Inquiry in Design-Science Research: Justification and Evaluation of Knowledge Production
MIS Quarterly, 39(3), pp. 541-564  |  Presenter: Kim

1️⃣ What Problem Does This Paper Solve?

🗣️ What I would say: After Hevner 2004 and Gregor & Hevner 2013, people knew DSR is real research and they knew how to classify their contribution. But there was still a problem: DSR projects are messy. In a single project, you might design an artifact, then do an experiment to test it, then do a case study to deploy it, then go back and redesign. Each of those steps uses a different kind of research logic.

Baskerville et al. call this a "methodological hodgepodge." The problem: when a reviewer reads your DSR paper, which criteria should they use to evaluate it? Design criteria? Scientific criteria? Both? It depends on which part of the project you are in at that moment.

Their solution: the concept of "Genres of Inquiry." Each genre has its own rules for what counts as a good contribution and how to evaluate it.

🗣️ What I would say: Cross the two dualities and you get four genres. This is the core of the paper. When you are doing a DSR project, at any given moment you are in one of these four genres:

4️⃣ Knowledge Moments — How Genres Flow

🗣️ What I would say: Here is the key insight: a DSR project does NOT stay in one genre. It moves through multiple genres as you work. Each transition is called a "knowledge moment."

Think of it like this: you start with a general idea (ND), build a specific system (ID), test it in a real context (IS), and then extract general lessons (NS). Or you might go in a different order. The point is that at each moment, the criteria for evaluating your work change.

💡 Example — CyberGate (Abbasi & Chen, 2008):

  • Step 1 (Genre ND): They created meta-requirements and design principles for text analysis in computer-mediated communication.
  • Step 2 (Genre ID): They built the actual CyberGate system — a working prototype.
  • Step 3 (Genre IS): They tested it on the Enron email database to see how it performs in a specific context.

💡 Example — VEM (Viability Engineering Method):

  • Step 1 (Genre ND): Created meta-models for modeling information flows.
  • Step 2 (Genre ID): Applied it in banking and healthcare contexts.
  • Step 3 (Genre NS): Generalized scientific knowledge from those applications.

🏆 Why this matters: If a reviewer judges your prototype (Genre ID) using the standards for general theory (Genre NS), they will say "this is not generalizable!" and reject you. But that is unfair because you were not trying to generalize at that moment. Knowing which genre you are in protects you from being judged by the wrong criteria.

5️⃣ Justification vs. Evaluation

🗣️ What I would say: Baskerville et al. make an important distinction between two types of "proof" in DSR:

ConceptWhat It IsWhenExample
Justification The logic and arguments — why your design should work. Your theoretical grounding. Before and during building "We use Predictive Processing Theory because agents need to predict and update. This is why norm-based coordination should work."
Evaluation The empirical evidence — does your artifact actually work? Performance data. After building "We tested FastSR on 3 datasets. F1@3 = 73. PRC = 72.61. It outperforms all baselines."

🗣️ Key point: Both are needed, but they serve different purposes. Justification tells you why it should work. Evaluation tells you whether it actually works. A paper with strong justification but weak evaluation (like Grisold et al.) is still valuable — it just needs future work to test it. A paper with strong evaluation but no justification is just engineering without theory.

6️⃣ How Each Genre Gets Justified and Evaluated

🗣️ What I would say: Each genre has different standards for justification and evaluation. This is the practical payoff of the paper:

GenreHow to JustifyHow to Evaluate
ND
(General Design)		 Ground your design principles in kernel theories (Ω). Explain WHY these principles should work. Are the principles internally consistent? Are they grounded in solid theory? Do they cover the design space?
ID
(Specific Design)		 Show your artifact meets the requirements. Explain design choices. Does the artifact work? Performance benchmarks, user testing, comparison to alternatives.
NS
(General Science)		 Use established scientific methods. Hypothetico-deductive logic. Statistical tests, replication, generalizability across contexts.
IS
(Specific Science)		 Rich description of context. Explain why this case matters. Credibility of findings, depth of analysis, transferability to similar contexts.

7️⃣ How This Connects to Hevner 2004 and Gregor & Hevner 2013

🗣️ What I would say: These three papers build on each other:

  • Hevner 2004: "DSR is real research. Here are 7 guidelines." → Established legitimacy.
  • Gregor & Hevner 2013: "Here is how to classify and present your DSR contribution." → Gave us the 2×2 matrix and 3 levels.
  • Baskerville et al. 2015: "A DSR project moves through different phases. Each phase has different rules." → Gave us genres and knowledge moments.

Together: Hevner says what DSR is. Gregor & Hevner say how to position it. Baskerville says how to evaluate each part of it.

8️⃣ Applying to Grisold et al. (2025)

🗣️ If someone asks me to classify Grisold using genres:

  • Genre ND (main): They created general design principles for norm-based coordination. Four principles, ten subprinciples. This is their core contribution — general, abstract, design-oriented.
  • Genre ID (partial): They illustrated the principles with a warehouse scenario (drones, robots, humans). But they did not build a real system.
  • Genre IS (weak): No real case study. The scenario is illustrative, not empirical.
  • Genre NS (not yet): No general scientific theory tested. That is future work.

Limitation: The paper stays mostly in Genre ND. To be stronger, future work should move to ID (build it) and IS (test it in a real setting).

9️⃣ Applying to Liu et al. (2025)

🗣️ If someone asks me to classify Liu et al. using genres:

  • Genre ID (strong): They built a specific artifact — the FastSR model. Architecture described in detail. This is the core.
  • Genre IS (strong): Tested on multiple datasets (WD, SD, CD). Benchmarked against 15+ models. Also did qualitative evaluation with practitioners.
  • Genre ND (implicit): The architecture itself suggests design principles for few-shot learning in low-label environments, but they do not explicitly extract general principles.
  • Genre NS (partial): Some general claims about expert-augmented FSL, but not formally theorized.

Strength: Strong ID + IS. They built it AND tested it properly. Limitation: Could have moved toward ND by extracting explicit design principles.

📣 One-sentence summary for class: "Baskerville et al. say a DSR project is not one kind of research — it moves through different genres. At each moment, you need to know whether you are designing or studying, and whether you are being general or specific. That determines how to justify and evaluate your work."
