Paper 1: The Sociotechnical Axis of Cohesion for the IS Discipline

Sarker, S., Chatterjee, S., Xiao, X., & Elbanna, A. (2019). The sociotechnical axis of cohesion for the IS discipline: Its historical legacy and its continued relevance. MIS Quarterly, 43(3), 695-719.
0. Citation

MIS Quarterly theory paper examining the identity and coherence of the IS discipline.

1. Goal

To argue that IS research has lost its sociotechnical character and to propose ways to reclaim this core identity.

2. Audience

IS researchers and the broader IS community concerned about the discipline's identity and future direction.

3. Problem

IS was built on the idea that both social and technical parts matter equally and should be studied together. But this is no longer happening. The authors reviewed 991 papers from MISQ and ISR between 2000-2016.

"About 56% of research is mostly social (Type I), only 7% is mostly technical (Type VI), and only 13% actually examines how social and technical interact (Type IV). Also, 91% of studies focus only on instrumental outcomes (efficiency) and ignore humanistic outcomes (ethics, well-being)."

Three Dangers of Losing the Axis

  1. Loss of distinctiveness: What makes IS different from computer science or organizational behavior?
  2. Internal division: The field breaks into pieces that don't talk to each other
  3. Ethical failure: Ignoring how technology affects human well-being
4. Research Questions

Implicit: Has IS research maintained its sociotechnical character? What types of social-technical relationships appear in published research?

5. Resolution

What is an "Axis of Cohesion"?

The authors borrow Andrew Abbott's concept of "axis of cohesion" from sociology. An axis of cohesion is a shared frame that holds a discipline together. It provides three things:

  • Common language: Words and concepts everyone in the field understands
  • Research orientation: A shared way of thinking about problems
  • Communal knowledge: Core ideas that everyone builds upon

For IS, the sociotechnical perspective should serve as this axis. It means studying both technical artifacts AND the social context (individuals, groups, organizations) together, treating neither as more important than the other.

Historical Context: Where Did Sociotechnical Come From?

The sociotechnical perspective has roots in the Tavistock Institute studies of British coal mines in the 1950s. Researchers noticed that introducing new mining technology failed when they ignored how workers were organized socially. The lesson: technology and social structure must be designed together.

IS as a field adopted this perspective from its founding. Early IS scholars like Enid Mumford, Rob Kling, and Claudio Ciborra emphasized that information systems are not just technical tools but are embedded in social practices. However, over time, this balance has been lost.

The Six Types of IS Research (Detailed)

Type Description How Social-Technical Relate MISQ % ISR %
Type I Predominantly Social IT is just context, barely mentioned. "Research on behavior that happens to involve IT." 56.5% 54.4%
Type II Social Imperative Technology is outcome of social processes. Organizations shape technology. 11.4% 12.2%
Type III Additive Antecedents Social AND Technical are separate inputs → outcomes. No interaction between them. 2.9% 2.7%
Type IV Interplay (IDEAL!) Social ↔ Technical interact dynamically. True sociotechnical research! 13.0% 10.3%
Type V Technical Imperative Technology drives social outcomes. "Soft" technological determinism. 9.9% 13.7%
Type VI Predominantly Technical Focus on IT design with minimal social consideration. Design science without users. 6.3% 6.7%

The Problem: Research Clusters at Extremes!

About 63% of IS research is at Type I or Type VI (the extremes). Only 13% is Type IV (true sociotechnical interplay). This means most IS research is either "social research that mentions IT" or "technical research that ignores users."

Type IV Subtypes (The Ideal Sociotechnical Research)

Type IV research shows true interaction between social and technical. Authors identify four subtypes:

Subtype How Social-Technical Interact Example
Reciprocal Social influences technical, which then influences social back Users modify system, system changes user behavior
Fit/Interaction Social × Technical interaction term in statistical models Technology effect depends on organizational context
Sociomaterial Social and material are inseparable, "entangled" Orlikowski's studies of work practices with technology
Inscription Social values embedded in technical design Algorithms encode designer assumptions

Three Core Observations from the Literature Review

  1. Uneven emphasis: Research does not balance social and technical. Most IS research is social-dominant (Type I). Very little is technical-dominant (Type VI). The middle (Type IV) is rare.
  2. Limited relationship types: When social and technical are both present, the relationship is usually just "fit" or "joint optimization." Authors argue for accepting more varied relationships: contextual, inscribed, imbricated, disharmonious, role-reversal, etc.
  3. Instrumental focus (91%): Almost all research focuses on efficiency, productivity, performance. Only 9% considers humanistic outcomes like well-being, equality, freedom, or ethics.

Three Recommendations

Recommendation 1: Social-Technical Continuum

Don't require every study to be in the middle. Instead, accept research anywhere on the continuum from predominantly social (Type I) to predominantly technical (Type VI). But:

  • Researchers should know where their work sits
  • Avoid clustering only at the extremes
  • Use different evaluation criteria for different types

Recommendation 2: Accept Varied Relationships

Don't assume social and technical must be in "fit," "harmony," or "joint optimization." Also accept:

  • Contextual: Technology's effect depends on social context
  • Inscribed: Social values built into technology design
  • Imbricated: Social and technical overlap like roof tiles (Leonardi)
  • Disharmonious: Technology and social structure in conflict
  • Role-reversal: Technology becomes social actor, humans become resources

Recommendation 3: Synergistic Humanistic-Instrumental Outcomes

Link efficiency goals with ethics and well-being goals. They can support each other in a virtuous cycle:

Humanistic outcomes (well-being, fairness) → Employee engagement → Better performance → More resources for humanistic outcomes

Example: Amartya Sen's capabilities approach suggests that human flourishing leads to economic development, not just the other way around. "Ethics is good business."

6. Contribution

Constructs and Framework: The six-type classification of research approaches and the axis of cohesion concept applied to IS.

7. Guidelines
  • Be aware of where your research sits on the social-technical continuum
  • Consider humanistic outcomes alongside instrumental ones
  • Explore different ways social and technical elements can interact
  • Contribute to the field's coherence by engaging with both aspects
8. Paper Structure
  • Introduction: The identity crisis in IS
  • Historical review: How IS became sociotechnical
  • Empirical analysis: Classification of 991 papers
  • Framework: The axis of cohesion concept
  • Recommendations: Three proposals for the future
9. Inspired Research Question

How can research on trust in AI systems bridge instrumental outcomes (efficiency, accuracy) with humanistic outcomes (fairness, well-being, autonomy)?

Paper 2: Theorizing the Digital Object

Faulkner, P., & Runde, J. (2019). Theorizing the digital object. MIS Quarterly, 43(4), 1279-1302.
0. Citation

MIS Quarterly theory paper addressing the fundamental nature of digital objects.

1. Goal

To provide a theory that explains what digital objects actually ARE, addressing their complexity and how their identity depends on social context.

2. Audience

IS researchers who want a deeper theoretical understanding of the IT artifacts they study.

3. Problem

IS research lacks good theories about what digital objects fundamentally are. We talk about digital technology all the time, but we don't really understand their basic nature.

"Orlikowski and Iacono (2001) showed that IS research under-theorizes the IT artifact. Follow-up studies (up to 2015) show this hasn't changed much. Most IS research either ignores what IT artifacts really are, treats them as simple tools, or reduces them to proxy measures. Only about 12-21% use the 'ensemble view' that properly considers technology complexity."
4. Research Questions

Implicit: What is the ontological nature of digital objects? How can we theorize their complexity and context-dependence?

5. Resolution

The authors build a complete theory of digital objects from first principles. This is "blue ocean" theorizing that avoids importing imprecise concepts from other fields.

Foundational Concepts

Concept Definition Examples
Continuant Something that endures through time, fully present at each moment A smartphone, a file, a person
Occurrent Something that takes place in time, unfolds over a period A process, an event, a transaction
Object A continuant that is structured (composed of distinct parts organized in some way) A server, a software application

Material vs. Nonmaterial Objects

Type Key Property Mode of Being Examples
Material Has spatial attributes (shape, volume, mass, location) Physical Scanners, smartphones, servers, hard drives
Nonmaterial Lacks spatial attributes Nonphysical Software, files, protocols, procedures
Hybrid Contains both material and nonmaterial components Material overall Laptop running an app, smartphone with installed software

Key Insight: Nonmaterial Objects Have Intrinsic Properties!

Unlike sociomateriality views that deny discrete things with intrinsic properties, this theory affirms that digital objects have inherent properties that matter organizationally. For example:

  • Nonmaterial objects don't degrade with use (unlike physical things)
  • They can be copied infinitely at near-zero cost (non-rivalry)
  • The same file can exist in many places at once
  • They don't wear out - no "lemons problem" for used digital goods

Syntactic Objects

A special type of nonmaterial object consisting of symbols arranged in well-formed expressions according to the rules of some language. Examples include:

  • News articles (symbols = words)
  • Source code (symbols = programming language syntax)
  • Musical notation (symbols = notes)
  • Mathematical formulas (symbols = numbers and operators)

Bitstrings: The Foundation of Digital Computing

A bitstring is a syntactic object made of 0s and 1s, structured according to a file format that makes it readable by computer hardware. Almost all information on silicon-based computers is encoded in bitstrings.

Type What It Encodes Examples
Program Files Logical operations (instructions) Operating systems, applications, games
Data Files Data (information content) Documents, images, videos, audio

The Bearer Theory (Major Innovation!)

Core Idea

Nonmaterial objects must be inscribed on, contained in, or borne by other objects to be accessed. The "bearer" is the object that carries the nonmaterial object.

Two Types of Bearers

Bearer Type What It Is Examples
Material Bearer Physical object that carries a nonmaterial object Screen displaying text, hard drive storing files, paper with printed document
Nonmaterial Bearer Syntactic object that carries another nonmaterial object Bitstring bearing an instruction set, DOCX file bearing a text document

The Innovation: Bitstrings as Nonmaterial Bearers

This is the key theoretical contribution. Bitstrings can serve as bearers of other nonmaterial objects, enabling multiple layers of bearers. This is unique to digital technology!

Translational Actions

Moving between bearer layers requires translational actions:

  • Encoding: Creating a bitstring that bears a nonmaterial object
  • Compressing: Creating a ZIP from files
  • Storing: Writing to a material bearer (hard drive)
  • Retrieving: Reading from a material bearer
  • Displaying: Rendering on screen
  • Printing: Creating a paper copy

Computation

Computation is the real-time algorithmic manipulation of information in bitstrings. Key points:

  • Computation has an existential relationship with digital objects (bidirectional dependency)
  • Execution of a program file creates a process of computation
  • Distributed computing: Multiple interconnected processes across locations and platforms

Social Positioning: How Digital Objects Get Identity

Digital objects acquire social identity through being positioned in social systems. This is how the same physical/nonmaterial thing can have different meanings in different contexts.

Layer Object Type Bears
1 Testing protocol (the actual rules) Nonmaterial —
2 English text expressing the protocol Syntactic (nonmaterial bearer) Protocol
3 DOCX file encoding the text Bitstring (nonmaterial bearer) Text
4 ZIP file containing the DOCX Bitstring (nonmaterial bearer) DOCX
5 Hard drive storing the ZIP Material bearer ZIP
Concept Definition Example
Social Position Status within a system that locates an entity as a component MRI scanner position in a hospital
Social Identity What the thing "is" in social terms "This is our MRI machine"
System Function What the position occupant is expected to contribute Produce body images for diagnosis

Key insight: Positions are:

  • Relational: Internally related to other positions (MRI technician ↔ MRI scanner)
  • Performed: Must be enacted through practices to exist
  • Reproduced or transformed: Through ongoing practices over time

Practical Example: Secondary Markets for Downloaded Bitstrings

The authors use the bearer theory to analyze a real legal dispute: Can you resell a "used" MP3 file?

Capitol Records v. ReDigi (US, 2013)

Background: ReDigi created a "Cloud Locker" where users could upload and sell their iTunes purchases. Capitol Records sued.

Ruling: Unlawful. Uploading to Cloud Locker creates a new material bearer (server copy) = copyright violation. The original "first sale" was for one copy, not the right to make new copies.

UsedSoft v. Oracle (EU, 2012)

Context: UsedSoft resold Oracle software licenses that customers no longer needed.

Ruling: Lawful. Creating a new material bearer is acceptable reproduction if it's necessary for use and the original is deleted. "Exhaustion of distribution right" applies to downloaded software.

Why the difference? The US court focused on the new material bearer being a copy. The EU court focused on the economic value transfer.

Implications for RBV, KBV, and SDL

The bearer theory has implications for major management theories:

  • Resource-Based View: Knowledge resources can be embedded in layers: knowledge in text in DOCX in ZIP in server. Each layer has different properties (accessibility, transferability).
  • Knowledge-Based View: "Resource liquefaction" (decoupling info from physical form) requires understanding bearer layers and translational actions.
  • Service-Dominant Logic: Expand "operand resources" to include nonmaterial objects like software, files, and protocols. Embedding patterns can be complex (operant in operand, operand in operand, etc.).

Bridge Between Sociomateriality and Imbrication

The authors position their theory as a bridge between two competing views:

View Core Claim This Theory's Position
Sociomateriality Denies discrete things with intrinsic properties; world = relations Accepts relationality and performativity in social positioning
Imbrication Affirms artifacts with fixed (even if temporary) intrinsic properties Accepts that digital objects have intrinsic properties that matter

Result: Digital objects have both intrinsic properties (nonmateriality, layered bearers) AND socially constructed identity (social positioning).

6. Contribution

Constructs and Underlying Mechanism: The bearer theory and the material/nonmaterial distinction provide new conceptual tools for understanding digital objects.

7. Guidelines
  • When studying digital technology, consider both material and nonmaterial aspects
  • Trace the chain of bearers when analyzing digital artifacts
  • Consider how social positioning gives digital objects their identity
  • Remember that digital object identity can change as social practices change
8. Paper Structure
  • Introduction: The under-theorized IT artifact problem
  • Ontological foundations: Objects, occurrents, material, nonmaterial
  • Syntactic objects and bitstrings: The building blocks
  • Bearer theory: How nonmaterial objects are carried
  • Social positioning: How identity is socially constructed
9. Inspired Research Question

Paper 3: The Next Generation of Research on IS Use

Baird, A., & Maruping, L. M. (2021). The next generation of research on IS use: A theoretical framework of delegation to and from agentic IS artifacts. MIS Quarterly, 45(1), 315-341.
0. Citation

MIS Quarterly theory paper introducing delegation as a new lens for human-AI relationships.

1. Goal

To introduce DELEGATION as a new lens to understand human-AI relationships, replacing the outdated "IS use" paradigm.

2. Audience

IS researchers studying technology adoption, use, and human-computer interaction, especially those interested in AI and autonomous systems.

3. Problem

IS use research has always assumed humans are in control and technology is a passive tool. But now we have "agentic IS artifacts" that can make decisions, learn, and act on their own. Three problems with current thinking:

  1. Human-centric language: "Users use systems." But what happens when AI uses the human?
  2. Focus on perceptions: UTAUT cares about what users think, not what the artifact can do
  3. Wrong unit of analysis: Current research looks at individuals, but with agentic IS, the DYAD (human + AI pair) is what matters
4. Research Questions

Implicit: How should we conceptualize human relationships with AI systems that can act autonomously?

5. Resolution

What is Agency?

Drawing from Russell & Norvig (2010): An agent is "something that perceives and acts in an environment." This definition applies to both humans and AI systems.

What are Agentic IS Artifacts?

Agentic IS artifacts are rational software-based agents that can:

  • Perceive their environment through sensors/inputs
  • Act autonomously without direct human intervention
  • Accept rights for task execution
  • Accept responsibilities for preferred outcomes
  • Make rational decisions to achieve best expected outcome

Three Key Properties of Agentic IS Artifacts

Property Definition Example
Situated Receives sensor input, performs actions that change environment Smart speaker listening for voice commands
Autonomous Acts without direct human intervention, controls own actions and state Autonomous vehicle navigating traffic
Flexible Responsive, proactive, and social Chatbot handling varied customer queries

Flexibility has three components:

  • Responsive: Perceives and responds timely to environmental changes
  • Proactive: Exhibits goal-directed behavior, takes initiative
  • Social: Interacts with other agents (both AI and human)

Four Archetypes of Agentic IS Artifacts (Detailed)

Arranged on a continuum from limited decision-making to expansive decision-making:

Archetype How It Works Decision Scope Examples
1. Reflexive (Reactive) Acts reflexively to stimuli per predefined models. No real "reasoning." Limited Sensing/alerting agents, basic voice assistants responding to queries, motion sensors
2. Supervisory (Control System) Evaluates deviations from norm, guides decision-making or takes corrective action Moderate Behavior modification apps, GPS guidance, smart thermostats, fitness trackers
3. Anticipatory (Proactive) Proactively anticipates needs/wants using model-based "reasoning" Substantial Social media curation, automatic album creation, AR name reminders, email auto-complete
4. Prescriptive (Autonomous) Acts as substitute for human decision-making by prescribing or taking actions Expansive Chatbots, autonomous vehicles, robo-advisors, medical diagnosis AI, legal AI

Key Insight: Prescriptive Archetypes Raise Trust Issues!

As we move toward prescriptive archetypes, the AI takes over more decision-making. This raises critical questions:

  • How do we evaluate AI's capability before delegating?
  • What happens when the AI makes a mistake?
  • Who is responsible for outcomes?

The IS Delegation Framework (Core Theory)

Definition of Delegation

Delegation = transferring rights and responsibilities for task execution and outcomes from one agent to another.

Three core elements:

  1. Presence of at least two agents
  2. Task or outcome that brings parties together
  3. Rights and responsibilities are transferred

Agent Attributes (Detailed)

Attribute Human Agent Agentic IS Artifact
Endowments Knowledge, experience, abstract thinking, creativity, emotion, intuition Data storage, processing power, speed, tirelessness, consistency, scale
Preferences Personal goals, decision model, values, risk tolerance Optimization objective, decision rules, what it's designed to achieve
Roles Delegator (gives task) OR Proxy (receives task) — can switch! Delegator (gives task) OR Proxy (receives task) — can switch!

Paradigm Shift: Bidirectional Delegation!

Unlike IS Use (human → system), delegation can be bidirectional:

  • Human → AI: User asks chatbot to book a flight
  • AI → Human: Manager bot assigns code review to developer

The AI can delegate tasks back to humans when it determines humans are better suited!

Task Attributes

Attribute Definition Implications for Delegation
Action Requirements Cognitive, digital, or physical AI better at cognitive/digital; humans still needed for physical
Complexity Uncertainty, interdependence, dynamics Higher complexity → more appraisal needed, more coordination
Decomposability Ability to subdivide into subtasks Decomposable tasks enable partial delegation

Situational Attributes

Attribute Definition Example
Incentives Economic or social motivations Payment, reputation, career advancement
Complexity Stability, observability, controllability of environment Stable warehouse vs. unpredictable traffic

Three Foundational Delegation Mechanisms (Detailed)

Mechanism 1: Appraisal

Agents evaluate each other and the delegation situation before deciding to delegate.

  • What it involves: Assessing other agent's capabilities, reliability, accuracy
  • Trust is central: Trust = positive evaluation of other agent's endowments and preferences
  • Cost-benefit analysis: Benefits of delegating vs. costs of potential failure
  • Situational: Different stakes require different levels of scrutiny

Example: Surgeon evaluating whether to trust AI's surgical guidance

Mechanism 2: Distribution

How rights and responsibilities are allocated between agents.

  • What is retained: Which rights does the delegator keep?
  • What is transferred: Which rights go to the proxy?
  • Contracting: Explicit or implicit agreements
  • Structure: Formal vs. informal delegation structures
  • Monitoring: How much oversight is maintained

Example: Defining what the chatbot can and cannot do without human approval

Mechanism 3: Coordination

How agents work together after initial delegation.

  • Monitoring: Tracking progress and performance
  • Updating: Adjusting delegation as situation changes
  • Feedback loops: Learning from outcomes to improve future delegation
  • Dynamic adaptation: Teams, tasks, and goals evolve over time

Example: Code review bot adjusting future assignments based on reviewer performance

Example 1: Surgical Support AI

The paper illustrates the framework with a surgical support AI example:

Element Application
Task Cognitive (determine optimal sequence) + Physical (execute surgery)
Delegation Surgeon delegates cognitive portion (sequence determination) to AI
Mechanism Appraisal is primary — surgeon must trust AI before following guidance
Process Surgeon compares own preferred approach with AI recommendations over time
Evolution Confidence builds through repeated successful interactions → more delegation

Example 2: Open Source Code Review Bots

The Kubernetes project uses bots to manage code review assignments:

Element Application
Task Assign code for review, identify appropriate reviewer, track completion
Delegation Bot (delegator) assigns review to developer (proxy) — AI→Human!
Mechanism Distribution (who gets assigned) + Coordination (tracking completion)
Bot's decision model Optimize for speed (who accepts quickly) or quality (who does best reviews)
Feedback loops Bot learns from past reviewer performance to improve future assignments

Multi-Agent Perspectives (Future Research)

The paper identifies three directions for extending the framework:

1. Triadic Perspective: The Designer

The AI designer has been ignored but matters enormously:

  • Designer's values are encoded in AI's decision model
  • AI may serve designer interests (e.g., advertisers) over user interests
  • If user suspects AI loyalty is to designer, delegation may be impeded
  • Key question: Whose interests does the AI really serve?

2. Agentic IS Platforms

Multiple AI agents working together on platforms:

  • Autonomous vehicle = platform with navigation, entertainment, safety AIs
  • IoT ecosystems = many devices with different capabilities
  • Third-party agents on platforms may have conflicting interests
  • Cooperation AND competition within platforms
  • Key question: How do dependencies between agents affect reliability?

3. Recipients and Ethics

The person affected by delegation (recipient) is often ignored:

  • Patient being operated on by surgeon + AI
  • Whose interests are prioritized?
  • Transparency about AI decision logic
  • Corporate secrecy may hide ethically dubious actions (Burrell 2016, Zuboff 2019)
  • Key question: How will ethics play a role in delegation appraisal?

Q1: What is the "axis of cohesion" and why do the authors argue it's important for IS?

The "axis of cohesion" is a concept borrowed from sociologist Andrew Abbott, referring to a shared intellectual frame that provides a discipline with common language, research orientation, and communal knowledge (Sarker et al., 2019). For IS, Sarker and colleagues argue this axis should be the sociotechnical perspective, which considers both technical artifacts and social contexts together, privileging neither.

The axis of cohesion matters for three critical reasons. First, it provides disciplinary distinctiveness. Without a unique perspective, IS risks having its territory claimed by other fields like computer science or management (Sarker et al., 2019). Second, it enables coherent expansion. As IS grows into new areas, a shared axis prevents fragmentation into disconnected silos that cannot communicate. Third, it guards against ethical abdication. By maintaining focus on both humanistic outcomes (well-being, freedom) and instrumental outcomes (efficiency), IS avoids contributing to a dehumanized society as imagined in dystopian literature like Orwell's 1984 (Sarker et al., 2019).

The authors trace the sociotechnical tradition to the Tavistock Institute's coal mining studies in 1950s Britain, demonstrating its deep roots and continued relevance for IS identity.

Reference: Sarker, S., Chatterjee, S., Xiao, X., & Elbanna, A. (2019). The sociotechnical axis of cohesion for the IS discipline. MIS Quarterly, 43(3), 695-719.

Q2: Why is Type IV (social-technical interaction) so rare at only 13%?

Sarker et al. (2019) found that only 13% of papers in their review of 991 articles from MISQ and ISR (2000-2016) demonstrated Type IV research, where social and technical elements genuinely interact and mutually shape each other. This rarity reflects several structural problems in IS research.

First, methodological challenges make true sociotechnical research difficult. Studying dynamic interplay between technology and social systems requires longitudinal designs, mixed methods, and theoretical frameworks that can accommodate mutual causation rather than simple independent-dependent variable relationships (Sarker et al., 2019).

Second, incentive structures may discourage Type IV research. Studies at the extremes (Type I and Type VI) are often easier to execute and publish because they follow established templates from reference disciplines, whether organizational behavior or computer science.

Third, disciplinary training often emphasizes one side. Researchers trained in behavioral traditions may default to Type I work, while those from technical backgrounds gravitate toward Type VI. The integration required for Type IV demands expertise in both domains (Sarker et al., 2019).

This imbalance matters because Type IV research is precisely what makes IS distinctive. Without it, IS becomes either sociology with technology context or engineering without user consideration.

Reference: Sarker, S., et al. (2019). The sociotechnical axis of cohesion for the IS discipline. MIS Quarterly, 43(3), 695-719.

Q3: What is the difference between material and nonmaterial bearers?

According to Faulkner and Runde (2019), nonmaterial objects like software, protocols, and digital files cannot exist independently and must be "borne" by other objects to be accessed. The theory distinguishes two types of bearers.

Material bearers are physical objects with spatial attributes (mass, volume, location) that carry nonmaterial objects. Examples include hard drives storing files, screens displaying text, paper holding printed documents, and human memory retaining procedures. Material bearers exist in physical space and are subject to degradation, damage, and location constraints (Faulkner & Runde, 2019).

Nonmaterial bearers are syntactic objects, particularly bitstrings, that carry other nonmaterial objects. A DOCX file (bitstring) bears an English text, which bears a testing protocol. The key innovation is recognizing that bitstrings can bear other syntactic objects, enabling unlimited layering: protocol → text → DOCX → ZIP → hard drive (Faulkner & Runde, 2019).

This distinction has profound implications. The same nonmaterial object can have multiple simultaneous material bearers in different locations. Each bearer layer has distinct properties, so the text is not the same as the DOCX bearing it. Translational actions (encoding, compressing, storing) are required to move between layers.

Reference: Faulkner, P., & Runde, J. (2019). Theorizing the digital object. MIS Quarterly, 43(4), 1279-1302.

Q4: How does the bearer theory help us understand the nature of AI systems?

Faulkner and Runde's (2019) bearer theory provides a rigorous ontological foundation for understanding AI systems as hybrid objects combining material and nonmaterial components.

AI systems involve multiple bearer layers. The AI's model (nonmaterial) is borne by bitstrings (nonmaterial bearer), which are borne by storage hardware (material bearer). Training data, algorithms, and outputs each exist at different bearer layers with distinct properties. This layered understanding helps explain why AI "knowledge" can be instantiated across multiple physical systems simultaneously, yet remains the same knowledge (Faulkner & Runde, 2019).

The theory also illuminates computation as the real-time algorithmic manipulation of information in bitstrings. For AI, computation is not merely processing but an existential relationship where the AI model depends on computational processes to function, while computation requires the model to have something to process (Faulkner & Runde, 2019).

Furthermore, social positioning explains how AI acquires its identity and function within organizational systems. An AI doesn't inherently have a "role"; it acquires social identity through positioning in institutional contexts, just like human actors. This connects to Baird and Maruping's (2021) work on agentic artifacts and their place in sociotechnical systems.

References: Faulkner, P., & Runde, J. (2019). Theorizing the digital object. MIS Quarterly, 43(4), 1279-1302; Baird, A., & Maruping, L. M. (2021). The next generation of research on IS use. MIS Quarterly, 45(1), 527-536.

Q5: What are the four archetypes of agentic IS artifacts?

Baird and Maruping (2021) identify four archetypes of agentic IS artifacts arranged along a continuum from limited to expansive decision-making scope.

1. Reflexive (Reactive): These artifacts act reflexively to stimuli according to predefined behavioral models. They involve no real "reasoning." Examples include motion sensors, basic voice assistants responding to queries, and simple alerting systems. Decision scope is limited to triggering predetermined responses (Baird & Maruping, 2021).

2. Supervisory (Control System): These artifacts evaluate deviations from norms and guide decision-making or take corrective action. Examples include behavior modification apps, GPS navigation that recalculates routes, and smart thermostats. They have moderate decision scope involving monitoring and adjustment (Baird & Maruping, 2021).

3. Anticipatory (Proactive): These artifacts proactively anticipate user needs using model-based "reasoning" without being explicitly asked. Examples include social media feed curation, automatic photo albums, and predictive text completion. They have substantial decision scope involving prediction and proactive intervention (Baird & Maruping, 2021).

4. Prescriptive (Autonomous): These artifacts act as substitutes for human decision-making by prescribing or taking autonomous actions. Examples include chatbots, autonomous vehicles, robo-advisors, and medical diagnosis AI. This archetype raises the most significant trust and delegation concerns due to its expansive decision scope.

Reference: Baird, A., & Maruping, L. M. (2021). The next generation of research on IS use. MIS Quarterly, 45(1), 527-536.

Q6: How is delegation different from traditional "IS use"?

Baird and Maruping (2021) argue that traditional IS use theories are inadequate for agentic AI systems, and propose delegation as the core organizing concept instead.

Traditional use theories like TAM, UTAUT, and Task-Technology Fit assume humans are always in control, applying technology as a tool. The terminology of "use" implies a human actor manipulating a passive instrument. These theories focus heavily on user perceptions (perceived usefulness, ease of use) rather than artifact capabilities. The unit of analysis is the individual user (Baird & Maruping, 2021).

Delegation fundamentally differs by recognizing that rights and responsibilities can be transferred between agents, whether human or AI. Key distinctions include: (1) Roles are interchangeable, either party can be delegator or proxy; (2) Bidirectional flow, AI can delegate to humans (like code review bots assigning tasks); (3) Tasks can be ambiguous and dynamic, not just well-defined operations; (4) Unit of analysis is the dyad, the human-AI pair rather than just the human (Baird & Maruping, 2021).

This matters for AI research because it accommodates scenarios where AI systems operate autonomously, make decisions, and even direct human action, which use-centered theories cannot adequately theorize.

Reference: Baird, A., & Maruping, L. M. (2021). The next generation of research on IS use. MIS Quarterly, 45(1), 527-536.

Q7: Which Gartner trends do you think will have the most impact on IS research?

Based on Gartner's Top 10 Strategic Technology Trends for 2026, three trends appear particularly impactful for IS research.

Multiagent Systems (1445% increase in Gartner inquiries) represents the most significant development. As autonomous AI agents collaborate and compete within integrated platforms, IS researchers must extend delegation theory beyond human-AI dyads to multi-agent ecosystems. Questions of trust, coordination, and accountability become exponentially more complex when multiple AI agents interact (Gartner, 2025).

Physical AI brings previously abstract AI concepts into tangible form (robots, autonomous vehicles, smart infrastructure). When AI mistakes have real-world physical consequences, trust and risk perception research takes on new urgency. The stakes of delegation decisions increase dramatically when failures can cause physical harm (Gartner, 2025).

AI Security addresses vulnerabilities in AI systems that go beyond traditional cybersecurity. Prompt injection, model manipulation, and adversarial attacks require new theoretical frameworks for understanding technological trust. This connects directly to Baird and Maruping's (2021) appraisal mechanism, where agents must evaluate each other's reliability (Gartner, 2025).

These trends suggest IS research must rapidly develop theories for multi-agent coordination, embodied AI trust, and security in delegation contexts.

References: Gartner (2025). Top 10 Strategic Technology Trends for 2026; Baird, A., & Maruping, L. M. (2021). MIS Quarterly.

Q8: How might multiagent systems change the way we think about trust in AI?

Multiagent systems fundamentally transform trust considerations by introducing inter-agent trust alongside human-AI trust. Traditional trust research focuses on whether humans should trust AI recommendations. In multiagent systems, researchers must also consider whether AI agents should trust each other (Gartner, 2025).

Baird and Maruping's (2021) delegation framework suggests several implications. First, appraisal becomes multilayered. Humans must evaluate not just individual AI agents but the entire agent ecosystem, including how agents evaluate each other. Second, coordination complexity increases. When agents delegate tasks among themselves before presenting results to humans, the human may not understand the provenance of recommendations (Baird & Maruping, 2021).

Third, the triadic perspective becomes inadequate. Baird and Maruping note that AI designers embed values into systems. With multiple agents from multiple designers interacting, value conflicts may emerge that are opaque to users. Fourth, accountability diffuses. When an AI recommendation fails, identifying the responsible agent in a multiagent chain becomes challenging (Baird & Maruping, 2021).

Future trust research must develop frameworks for evaluating multiagent ecosystems holistically, not just individual agents. This requires extending trust theory beyond dyadic relationships to network-level analysis.

References: Gartner (2025). Top 10 Strategic Technology Trends for 2026; Baird, A., & Maruping, L. M. (2021). MIS Quarterly, 45(1), 527-536.

📋 Additional Exam Questions

Q9: What is the sociotechnical perspective and why does Sarker argue it matters?

The sociotechnical perspective considers technical artifacts AND individuals/collectives in social contexts together, privileging neither (Sarker et al., 2019). It originated at the Tavistock Institute in 1950s Britain through studies of coal mining, where researchers discovered that optimizing technology alone without considering social systems led to failure.

Sarker argues this perspective matters because it provides IS with disciplinary distinctiveness. The perspective espouses focus on both instrumental outcomes (efficiency, productivity) and humanistic outcomes (well-being, equality, freedom). Without this dual focus, IS risks either becoming pure engineering that ignores human consequences or pure social science that fails to theorize technology deeply (Sarker et al., 2019).

The perspective should serve as the "axis of cohesion" providing a shared frame for the discipline. Without it, IS faces erosion (other fields claiming IS territory), fragmentation (silos that cannot communicate), and ethical abdication (contributing to dehumanized societies).

Reference: Sarker, S., et al. (2019). The sociotechnical axis of cohesion for the IS discipline. MIS Quarterly, 43(3), 695-719.

Q10: What is a bitstring and why does it matter?

A bitstring is a syntactic object made of 0s and 1s structured according to a file format, readable by computer hardware (Faulkner & Runde, 2019). Virtually all information on silicon-based computers is encoded in bitstrings.

Bitstrings come in two types: Program files encode logical operations (operating systems, applications, games), while Data files encode data (documents, images, videos). The structure of each bitstring is determined by its file format.

Bitstrings matter for ontological theory because they can serve as nonmaterial bearers of other nonmaterial objects. A DOCX bitstring bears text, which bears meaning. This enables unlimited layering of bearers, a defining feature of digital technology that distinguishes it from analog information storage. Understanding bitstrings helps explain properties of digital objects like perfect reproducibility, zero marginal cost of copying, and simultaneous existence in multiple locations (Faulkner & Runde, 2019).

Reference: Faulkner, P., & Runde, J. (2019). Theorizing the digital object. MIS Quarterly, 43(4), 1279-1302.

Q11: What are the 3 foundational mechanisms of IS delegation?

Baird and Maruping (2021) identify three foundational mechanisms through which delegation operates:

1. Appraisal: Agents evaluate each other and the situation before delegation occurs. This includes assessing the other party's endowments (abilities, resources), preferences (goals, values), and roles. Trust is part of appraisal: positive evaluation of the other party's capabilities and intentions. Cost-benefit analysis also occurs here (Baird & Maruping, 2021).

2. Distribution: How rights and responsibilities are allocated between parties. This includes decisions about what rights are retained versus transferred, what level of oversight the delegator maintains, and how accountability is structured. Distribution can range from full delegation to heavily monitored partial delegation (Baird & Maruping, 2021).

3. Coordination: How agents work together after delegation occurs. This includes monitoring mechanisms, feedback loops, updating arrangements as circumstances change, and adapting to unexpected situations. Effective coordination requires continued communication and mutual adjustment (Baird & Maruping, 2021).

Reference: Baird, A., & Maruping, L. M. (2021). The next generation of research on IS use. MIS Quarterly, 45(1), 527-536.

Q12: How did US and EU rule differently on secondary markets for downloaded bitstrings?

Faulkner and Runde (2019) use contrasting legal decisions to illustrate how bearer theory has practical implications for digital commerce.

US (Capitol Records v. ReDigi, 2013): The court ruled that when a user uploads a music file to a cloud locker for resale, they necessarily create a new material bearer (new copy on new server). This constitutes copyright violation regardless of whether the original is deleted. Therefore, secondary market resale of downloaded music is unlawful in the US.

EU (UsedSoft v. Oracle, 2012): The court ruled that creating a new material bearer is acceptable lawful reproduction if it is necessary for use and the original is deleted. The First Sale Doctrine applies to digital goods just as to physical goods. Therefore, secondary market resale of downloaded software is lawful in the EU.

The core philosophical difference: Is creating a new bearer inherently an illegal reproduction, or is it acceptable if functionally equivalent to transferring a physical object? Bearer theory helps articulate exactly what happens in these transactions (Faulkner & Runde, 2019).

Reference: Faulkner, P., & Runde, J. (2019). Theorizing the digital object. MIS Quarterly, 43(4), 1279-1302.

۱. بازتابی (Reflexive) واکنش به محرک‌ها طبق مدل‌های از پیش تعریف‌شده سنسور، دستیار صوتی ساده
۲. نظارتی (Supervisory) انحراف از نرم را ارزیابی، راهنمایی یا اصلاح می‌کند ترموستات هوشمند، GPS
۳. پیش‌بینانه (Anticipatory) نیازها را پیش‌بینی می‌کند با "استدلال" مبتنی بر مدل فیلتر شبکه اجتماعی، آلبوم خودکار
۴. تجویزی (Prescriptive) جایگزین تصمیم‌گیری انسان می‌شود! chatbot، خودروی خودران، مشاور مالی AI

تعریف تفویض

تفویض = انتقال حقوق و مسئولیت‌ها برای اجرای کار از یک agent به دیگری

سه عنصر اساسی:

  1. حضور حداقل دو agent
  2. کار یا نتیجه‌ای که طرفین را گرد هم می‌آورد
  3. حقوق و مسئولیت‌ها منتقل می‌شوند

تفاوت IS Use و IS Delegation

IS Use (قدیمی) IS Delegation (جدید)
انسان همیشه در کنترل است نقش‌ها قابل تعویض هستند (انسان یا AI می‌تواند delegator باشد)
سیستم وابسته به کاربر است سیستم می‌تواند خودمختار باشد
کارها مشخص و محدود هستند کارها می‌توانند مبهم، نامطمئن، پویا باشند
انسان همیشه برتر است هر agent ممکن است "بهتر" باشد
یک‌طرفه: انسان → سیستم دوطرفه: انسان ↔ سیستم

⭐ نکته کلیدی: تفویض دوطرفه!

AI می‌تواند به انسان تفویض کند! مثال: ربات مدیر در Kubernetes که کار review کد را به توسعه‌دهنده می‌دهد.

سه مکانیزم تفویض

  1. ارزیابی (Appraisal): agent ها یکدیگر و شرایط را ارزیابی می‌کنند. آیا می‌توانم اعتماد کنم؟ هزینه-فایده چیست؟
  2. توزیع (Distribution): چه حقوقی نگه داشته می‌شود؟ چه حقوقی منتقل می‌شود؟ چقدر نظارت؟
  3. هماهنگی (Coordination): پس از تفویض چگونه با هم کار می‌کنند؟ نظارت، به‌روزرسانی، بازخورد.

مثال‌های کاربردی از مقاله

  • Surgical Support AI: جراح بخش شناختی (تعیین ترتیب) را به AI تفویض می‌کند. Appraisal مهم‌ترین مکانیزم است.
  • Kubernetes Code Review Bot: ربات (delegator) کار review را به توسعه‌دهنده (proxy) تفویض می‌کند. AI → انسان!

سه دیدگاه Multi-Agent (تحقیقات آینده)

  1. Triadic (سه‌گانه): طراح AI هم مهم است! ارزش‌های طراح در مدل تصمیم AI encode شده. AI ممکن است به نفع طراح (مثلاً تبلیغ‌دهنده) عمل کند نه کاربر.
  2. Agentic Platforms: چندین AI روی یک پلتفرم. مثال: خودروی خودران = ناوبری + سرگرمی + ایمنی. همکاری و رقابت همزمان!
  3. Recipients & Ethics: شخص تحت تأثیر (مثلاً بیمار) اغلب نادیده گرفته می‌شود. شفافیت منطق AI مهم است.
💡 نکات کلیدی Baird & Maruping:
  • Unit of Analysis: Human-Agentic IS Artifact Dyad (جفت انسان-AI)
  • Agent Attributes: Endowments (دارایی‌ها)، Preferences (ترجیحات)، Roles (نقش‌ها)
  • Task Attributes: Action Requirements، Complexity، Decomposability
  • ادبیات ترکیب‌شده: Advice Literature + Algorithm Perception + Delegation Decision-Making
  • اعتماد = بخشی از Appraisal: ارزیابی مثبت endowments و preferences طرف مقابل

🎯 گارتنر ۲۰۲۶: ترندهای فناوری

سه تم اصلی

  1. The Architect (معمار): AI-Native Development، AI Supercomputing، Confidential Computing
  2. The Synthesist (ترکیب‌کننده): Multiagent Systems، Domain-Specific Models، Physical AI
  3. The Vanguard (پیشتاز): Preemptive Cybersecurity، Digital Provenance، AI Security

ارتباط با تحقیقات IS:

  • Multiagent Systems: ۱۴۴۵٪ افزایش سوالات! چندین AI با هم کار می‌کنند. سوالات اعتماد و تفویض بین agent ها مطرح می‌شود.
  • Domain-Specific Models: AI تخصصی ممکن است بیشتر قابل اعتماد باشد چون برای یک حوزه خاص طراحی شده.
  • Physical AI: وقتی AI در دنیای فیزیکی عمل می‌کند، اعتماد حیاتی‌تر است چون اشتباهات پیامد واقعی دارند.
💡 نکات کلیدی Gartner:
  • ۴۰٪: اپلیکیشن‌های سازمانی تا ۲۰۳۰ با پلتفرم‌های AI-native ساخته می‌شوند
  • Geopatriation: برگرداندن داده‌ها به مکان‌های جغرافیایی خاص برای compliance
  • Digital Provenance: تأیید منشأ و سلامت نرم‌افزار، داده و رسانه
