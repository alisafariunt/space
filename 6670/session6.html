🎯 Core Question - سوال اصلی

English: Can we develop a brief, self-administered instrument that measures the degree to which an adult with normal intelligence has the traits associated with the autistic spectrum?

فارسی: آیا می‌توانیم یک ابزار کوتاه و خودگزارش‌دهی بسازیم که میزان ویژگی‌های مرتبط با طیف اوتیسم را در بزرگسالان با هوش عادی اندازه‌گیری کند؟

📖 Background & Motivation

Autism and Asperger Syndrome (AS) are conditions that exist on a spectrum. Clinically, they are diagnosed only when traits are severe enough to cause problems in daily life. But Baron-Cohen and colleagues argue that autistic traits are also present to varying degrees in the general population — the idea being that autism is not just a binary (you have it or you don't), but rather a continuous dimension.

Before this paper, there was no short, self-report instrument for measuring where someone falls on this spectrum. The authors aimed to fill that gap by creating the Autism-Spectrum Quotient (AQ).

🧪 The AQ-50 Instrument

The AQ is a 50-item self-report questionnaire. Each item is scored 1 point if the response is in the "autistic" direction, giving a total score from 0 to 50. Higher scores = more autistic traits.

Five Subscales (10 items each):
Subscale What It Measures Example Item Direction
Social Skill Preference for social interaction "I prefer to do things with others rather than on my own"
Attention Switching Ability to shift focus flexibly "I frequently get so absorbed that I lose sight of other things"
Attention to Detail Focus on fine details and patterns "I tend to notice details that others do not"
Communication Ease with social communication "I find it easy to 'read between the lines'"
Imagination Capacity for imaginative thinking "I find it easy to play games with children that involve pretending"

📊 Study Design & Participants

Group N Description Mean AQ Score
Group 1 (AS/HFA) 58 Adults diagnosed with Asperger Syndrome or High-Functioning Autism 35.8
Group 2 (Controls) 174 Randomly selected from general population 16.4
Group 3 (Students) 840 Cambridge University students across disciplines 17.6

🔑 Key Findings - یافته‌های کلیدی

✅ Finding 1: The AQ Differentiates Groups

The AS/HFA group scored significantly higher (M = 35.8) than controls (M = 16.4) and students (M = 17.6). A cutoff score of 32+ correctly identified 79.3% of the AS/HFA group while only 2% of controls scored that high.

✅ Finding 2: Sex Differences

Males scored higher than females across all three groups — consistent with the idea that autism is an extreme form of the "male brain." At the AQ score of 20+, twice as many males (40%) as females (21%) in the control group reached this level.

✅ Finding 3: Scientists Score Higher

Among Cambridge students, those studying mathematics and physical sciences scored significantly higher than those in humanities and social sciences. Within sciences, mathematics had the highest mean AQ (21.9), followed by computer science, and engineering.

⚠️ Finding 4: Validation with Clinical Interviews

Of 11 students scoring 32+, 7 met criteria for HFA or AS based on clinical interview. However, none complained of unhappiness — in a university setting, their preferences (e.g., not socializing, pursuing narrow interests in math/computing) were "not considered odd, and was even valued."

💡 Why This Paper Matters for IS Research

  • Foundation for measurement: The AQ provides a validated instrument that later IS research (like Jia, 2025) uses to study autistic traits in IT professionals
  • Scientists ≈ higher AQ: The finding that math/CS students score higher on the AQ directly connects to the question "Are IS people different?"
  • Spectrum concept: Introduces the idea that traits exist on a continuum, not a binary diagnosis — this is central to the neurodiversity perspective
  • Context matters: The finding that high-scoring individuals thrived in university settings shows that person-environment fit matters for wellbeing

🧠 Connection to the "Extreme Male Brain" Theory

Baron-Cohen's broader theoretical framework argues that the autistic brain is an extreme version of the typical male cognitive profile:

Cognitive Profile Description Relevance
Typical Female Higher empathizing (E > S) Better at reading emotions and social cues
Typical Male Higher systemizing (S > E) Better at understanding rule-based systems
Autistic Extreme systemizing (S >> E) Excellent at systems but struggles with social reading

🔑 Why This Matters for the IT Workforce

If IT work naturally attracts high-systemizers, and high-systemizing correlates with higher AQ scores, then the IT profession may naturally concentrate individuals with elevated autistic traits. This is exactly what Jia (2025) later confirms empirically.

⚠️ Limitations to Note

  • The AQ is a screening tool, not a diagnostic instrument
  • All participants were from the UK — cultural generalizability is unclear
  • Self-report instruments have inherent biases (social desirability, self-awareness)
  • The student sample is from Cambridge University — a very specific population

📖 Theoretical Foundations

1. Empathizing-Systemizing (E-S) Theory (Baron-Cohen)

This theory says individuals differ on two dimensions:

  • Empathizing (E): The ability to identify and respond to another person's emotions and mental states
  • Systemizing (S): The drive to analyze, understand, and build rule-based systems

People with high autistic traits tend to be hyper-systemizers and hypo-empathizers. IT professionals, by nature of their work with complex systems, may naturally attract those with this cognitive profile.

2. Theory of Mind (ToM)

ToM is the ability to read other people's mental states — their beliefs, intentions, and emotions. The paper measures this using the "Reading the Mind in the Eyes" Test (also by Baron-Cohen): participants identify emotions from photographs of just the eye region of faces.

3. Emotional Labor (Hochschild, 1983)

Emotional labor is the effort of managing one's emotional expressions to meet workplace expectations. Surface acting — faking emotions you don't feel — is the most harmful form and is linked to exhaustion. Jia extends this: for IT professionals with weaker ToM, social interactions themselves become a form of literal emotional labor.

4. Social Compensation Hypothesis

Even when autistic individuals achieve similar accuracy in social tasks, they do so through effortful compensatory processing rather than intuition. This effort uses "cognitive resources not otherwise used by neurotypical individuals for mentalizing" (Livingston et al., 2019) — and this extra effort is exhausting over time.

🔬 Three-Study Design

Study 1: Establishing the Phenomenon (N = 32 IT professionals)

Purpose: Do IT professionals score higher on the AQ-50?

  • Mean AQ-50 score = 21.56 (significantly higher than the general population norm, p < .001, medium effect size: Cohen's d=.69)
  • 28.1% met the clinical cutoff (≥ 26) vs. only 3.05% in the general population (p < .001)
  • Four participants self-identified as autistic (none formally diagnosed)
  • The company was in the U.S. Midwest, not Silicon Valley — making this finding more notable
Study 2: Explaining Social Difficulties via ToM (N = 222 students)

Purpose: Do IT students have lower Theory of Mind capacity?

  • Compared 143 IT majors vs. 79 business management majors using the Eyes Test
  • Accuracy: No significant difference (IT = .704, MGT = .710, n.s.)
  • Response Time: IT students took significantly longer (RTIT = 7.29s vs. RTMGT = 5.23s, p < .001, large effect size: Δ = 1.26)
  • Interpretation: IT students can read emotions just as accurately, but it requires significantly more cognitive effort — this is the social compensation hypothesis in action
  • Gender effect was nonsignificant — women who self-select into IT show similar patterns as IT men
Study 3: Consequences for Emotional Labor & Mental Health (N = 249 IT professionals)

Purpose: What are the downstream effects of autistic traits?

  • SEM model tested with LISREL 9.3 — satisfactory model fit
  • Social challenge → Emotional labor (β = .342, p < .005) ✅ H4
  • Emotional labor → Work exhaustion (p < .005) ✅ H5
  • Work exhaustion → Turnover intention (p < .005) ✅ H6
  • Work exhaustion → Wellbeing (negative, p < .005) ✅ H7
  • Social challenge → Wellbeing (direct negative path, p < .005) ✅ H8a

🧠 Mental Health Findings (Study 3)

  • Anxiety: 15.67% of IT professionals met clinical cutoff (GAD-7 ≥ 10) — about 2.3x the general population rate
  • Depression: 17.67% of IT professionals met clinical cutoff (PHQ-9 ≥ 10) — also about 2.3x the general population rate
  • IT professionals were significantly underdiagnosed of autism (H2b supported)
  • Autism diagnoses in their immediate families were 2.3x higher than the population norm (H2c supported)

🔬 Methodology Deep Dive

Study Design Sample Key Measures Analysis
Study 1 One-sample comparison 32 IT professionals at Midwestern firm AQ-50 (Baron-Cohen), self-reported diagnosis One-sample t-test, chi-square against population norms
Study 2 Between-groups quasi-experiment 143 IT vs. 79 business students Reading the Mind in the Eyes Test (accuracy + RT) ANCOVA controlling for age/sex, Cohen's d for effect size
Study 3 Cross-sectional survey + SEM 249 IT professionals from Qualtrics panel AQ-10, surface acting, work exhaustion, turnover, GAD-7, PHQ-9 LISREL 9.3, CFA + full SEM

⚠️ Important Methodological Notes

  • Study 1 used AQ-50 (the full Baron-Cohen instrument), but Study 3 used AQ-10 (a shortened version) — this is a practical trade-off for survey length
  • The Eyes Test in Study 2 measured both accuracy AND response time — this dual measurement was essential for discovering the social compensation effect
  • Study 3 used validated clinical scales (GAD-7 for anxiety, PHQ-9 for depression) — these are the same tools used by doctors, making the mental health findings clinically meaningful
  • The social communication challenge construct was measured through 4 AQ-10 items related to communication difficulty, not the full AQ

💡 Key Contributions - مشارکت‌های کلیدی

  • Trait-based approach to emotional labor: Traditionally, emotional labor is studied in boundary-spanning service roles (e.g., nurses, call center workers). Jia shows that for IT professionals, emotional labor comes from who they are (dispositional traits), not just what they're asked to do
  • ToM as mechanism: First IS study to use a cognitive performance task (Eyes Test) to show that IT professionals' social difficulties have a socio-cognitive basis, not just a skills gap
  • Mental health alarm: IT professionals are 2.3x more likely to meet clinical cutoffs for anxiety and depression — this goes beyond typical "burnout" research
  • "Schneider's law": "The people make the place" — dispositional factors (autistic traits) partly explain why the IT profession has a particular culture
  • Gender finding: Women who self-select into IT have similar autistic trait profiles as IT men — gender differences in the general population may disappear within IT

🏢 Practical Implications

⚠️ Critical Insight: Social Skills Training May Not Work

ToM has a 28% heritability (Warrier et al., 2018). The paper argues that asking IT professionals to "gain more social skills" is like asking them to change their brain wiring. Short-term training (e.g., a large tech firm training new hires to make eye contact) doesn't address the underlying neurocognitive causes and may even increase stigma and pressure to camouflage.

  • Person-job fit: Use ToM-based assessments to place high-empathy IT professionals in client-facing roles and allow others to focus on technical work
  • Remote work: Flexible work arrangements can reduce the social demands that drive emotional labor for high-autistic-trait individuals
  • Neurodiversity programs: Corporate programs focusing only on diagnosed individuals miss the many IT professionals with elevated (but subclinical) autistic traits
  • Wellbeing priority: IT managers should actively manage social demands and prioritize employee mental health

⚠️ Limitations

  • Study 1 has a small sample (N = 32) — limits generalizability
  • The Eyes Test uses static photographs — real-life social interactions are dynamic and more demanding
  • Cross-sectional data in Study 3 cannot establish causality
  • IT professionals are examined at the group level — within-group differences (e.g., analyst vs. programmer) are not explored
  • Did not ask if respondents were on medication for anxiety/depression — prevalence may be underestimated

🎤 Questions for Dr. Jia — Guest Speaker

Deep questions to ask during class, with short expected answers to prepare your mind.

Q1: In Study 2, IT students got the same accuracy on the Eyes Test but took 40% longer. You call this "social compensation." My question is — in real life, people don't wait 7 seconds for you to read their face. So does this mean the Eyes Test actually underestimates how hard social interaction is for IT people?

💡 Expected direction of answer

Yes, most likely. The Eyes Test uses static photos with no time limit — real conversations are fast and dynamic. The paper itself says "in dynamic social settings… the seemingly benign weakness could become much more amplified." So the large RT effect size (d = 1.26) is probably a conservative estimate of the real-world difficulty.

Q2: You mention that a big tech company trains new hires to make eye contact, and you basically say this doesn't work because it doesn't fix the underlying ToM issue. But isn't there a practical problem here? Organizations need IT people to communicate with business. If training doesn't work, what should they do instead?

💡 Expected direction of answer

The paper suggests a few alternatives: (1) Use person-job fit — assign IT people with higher ToM to client-facing roles, (2) Reduce unnecessary social demands through remote work, async communication, fewer meetings, (3) Rather than forcing "soft skills," foster awareness and self-acceptance about neurodiversity. The key shift is: change the environment, not the person.

Q3: Your paper says remote work can help reduce emotional labor for IT professionals. But some would argue remote work just hides the problem — people still need to collaborate on Zoom calls and read emotions through screens. Do you think remote work is a real solution, or just a temporary fix?

💡 Expected direction of answer

Remote work reduces the frequency and intensity of social demands, even if it doesn't eliminate them. Async communication (email, Slack) is much easier for someone with ToM weakness because you have time to process — like the Eyes Test with no time limit. But video calls still require real-time face reading. So it's a partial accommodation, not a full solution.

Q4: You found that 15.67% of IT professionals had clinical anxiety and 17.67% had clinical depression — 2.3× the general population! But you also note that you didn't ask whether they were on medication. If some are medicated, the real numbers could be even worse. How big of a concern is this for you?

💡 Expected direction of answer

It's a real limitation the paper acknowledges. If respondents were on medication and still scored below the cutoff, they'd be counted as "none/minimal" severity. So the 15-17% figure is likely an underestimate. This makes the finding even more alarming and reinforces the urgency of addressing mental health in IT workplaces.

Q5: The CIO case study you cite really stuck with me — the newly promoted CIO who almost got fired because he couldn't read the business executives. You frame this as a ToM problem. But could it also be that the organization failed by promoting someone without checking if they had the social skills for that role? Isn't this more of a management failure than a neurodiversity issue?

💡 Expected direction of answer

Both! The paper argues for person-job fit — organizations should use ToM-based assessments instead of traditional self-reports for social skill evaluation. But yes, the org also failed by assuming a great technician equals a great leader. This connects to the broader point: ToM weakness doesn't mean someone is a bad employee — it means they might be in the wrong role.

Q6: Your paper introduces a "disposition-centric paradigm" for emotional labor — basically saying it's not just about the job situation, it's about who the person is. But doesn't this risk blaming the individual? Like — "you're anxious because of your traits, not because of bad management." How do you keep this from becoming a way to excuse bad work environments?

💡 Expected direction of answer

Great concern. The paper is careful to say mental health challenges are "not an intrinsic feature of high autistic traits" — all three mechanisms (socio-cognitive, behavioral, physiological) are activated in social environments. So the disposition-centric view complements the situation-centric view — both matter. The point is not to blame traits but to understand that the same environment affects people differently based on their neurocognitive profile.

Q7: You treat IT professionals as one group, but you admit there's big within-group variation. A cybersecurity analyst who works alone is very different from a business analyst who talks to clients all day. Do you think future research should look at autistic traits by IT job role? Would you expect, say, developers to score higher than IT consultants?

💡 Expected direction of answer

Absolutely — the paper flags this as a limitation and future direction. Holland's vocational interest theory predicts that within IT, people still self-select. More socially demanding roles (consultant, PM) might attract people with better ToM. Roles with less social demand (backend dev, security) might attract higher autistic traits. The dispersion in Study 2 RT already hints at this heterogeneity.

Q8: This paper connects three very different fields — autism research, emotional labor, and IS workforce research. That's really unusual for a MISQ paper. Can you share what the review process was like? Did reviewers push back on bringing clinical concepts like autism into IS research?

💡 Expected direction of answer

This is a more personal question that shows genuine curiosity. Given that the paper acknowledges reviewers' "insightful" comments (including one who called emotional labor a "core feature" of IT work), the review likely involved significant debate. The paper went through ICIS and SIGMIS-CPR conferences before MISQ — suggesting a long development journey. Ask this toward the end of the session for a more personal conversation.

⚡ Quick-fire Questions (short & sharp)

Q9: 28.1% of your IT sample hit the AQ-50 clinical cutoff, but only 0.8% had an actual diagnosis. Is the AQ-50 over-flagging, or is the gap really about underdiagnosis?

Q10: MTurk workers scored higher on autistic traits than your IT professionals (Table 12). Doesn't that weaken the "IT people are special" argument?

🔍 Core Question

What makes one intrinsically interested in IT? Building on the Empathizing-Systemizing (E-S) theory, the paper shows that autistic tendency — not gender — is the real driver of intrinsic interest in technology.

📚 Key Concepts from the Paper

🧠 The E-S Theory

Baron-Cohen's (2002) Empathizing-Systemizing theory says the mind has two major dimensions:

  • Empathizing: The drive to identify another person's emotions and thoughts, and respond with appropriate emotions. Allows predicting people.
  • Systemizing: The drive to analyze, control, and build rule-based systems by understanding input-operation-output relationships. Allows predicting systems.

People high in autistic tendency exhibit hyper-systemizing (along with impaired empathizing). This is why they are drawn to computers — "Unlike people, computers follow strict laws, and are closed systems — all the variables are well-defined within the system, are knowable, predictable and, in principle, controllable" (Baron-Cohen, 2002, p. 252).

📊 Autistic Tendency as a Continuum

A central clarification: the paper uses "autistic tendency" (not "autism") to emphasize that:

  • It is a continuous variable, not a binary diagnosis
  • Autism "blends seamlessly into the general population" (Baron-Cohen, 2009)
  • Everyone can be "a bit autistic" (Happé & Frith, 2020), though mostly at subclinical levels
  • Employers don't want people with a label — they want people with specific attributes (attention to detail, analytical skills)

🎯 PIIT — Personal Innovativeness with IT

Dependent variable: PIIT is "an intrinsic willingness to try out new technologies" (Agarwal & Prasad, 1998). It captures one's general orientation toward technology, not a specific technology adoption decision. This is important because most adoption studies treat intrinsic motivators as exogenous — but why are some people more interested in IT? This paper offers one answer: autistic tendency.

🔬 Study 1: U.S. Sample (N = 419)

Method: Online survey via Amazon Mechanical Turk (MTurk). Diverse U.S. sample: 186 men (44.4%), 233 women (55.6%).

Instruments:

  • AQ-9 (Jia et al., 2019): Two factors — Attention to Detail (propensity to capture info thoroughly) and Social Communication (level of impairment in social interactions)
  • SQ-7 (Jia et al., 2019): Two factors — Technicity (drive to process technical information) and Structure (drive to discover the structure of objects)
  • PIIT (Agarwal & Prasad, 1998): 3 items measuring intrinsic IT interest

Analysis: PLS-SEM (SmartPLS 3.2.7) + ANOVA for gender difference tests.

⚠️ The Gender Illusion

Without controlling for autistic tendency: Men showed significantly higher PIIT than women (PIITMen = 4.81, PIITWomen = 3.98, F = 32.8, p < 0.001).

With autistic tendency in the model: Gender → PIIT becomes completely nonsignificant (β = -0.061, n.s.).

This means what looks like a gender difference is actually an individual-level difference in autistic tendency masquerading as gender.

🗣️ Ask in class today!

This finding really surprised me. For years researchers assumed men are naturally more interested in IT. But Jia et al. showed that when you control for autistic tendency, the gender effect completely disappears (F=32.8 becomes n.s.)! So what looked like a gender difference was actually an individual-level difference in autistic traits pretending to be gender. In India, gender was never significant even without controlling. This means the gender gap is a sociocultural artifact, not biological.

Question for class: Could this finding challenge the entire gender difference literature in IT? For example, Venkatesh & Morris (2000) found men accept technology more than women. Was autistic tendency a confound there too?

An Examination of Prestigious Stigma: The Case of the Technology Geek

📝 Citation

Moore, J. E. & Love, M. S. (2011). An Examination of Prestigious Stigma: The Case of the Technology Geek. International Journal of Social and Organizational Dynamics in IT, 1(2), 1–25.

پیپر سوم

این پیپر چه می‌گوید؟ این یک پیپر تئوریک (نه تجربی) است که مفهوم جدیدی به نام «انگ اعتبارمند» (Prestigious Stigma) را معرفی می‌کند. ایده اصلی این است که «گیک فناوری» همزمان هم انگ اجتماعی دارد (یعنی از نظر اجتماعی عجیب و غریب تلقی می‌شود) و هم اعتبار دارد (چون تخصص فنی‌اش لازم و ارزشمند است).

تفاوت با انگ معمولی: در تئوری گافمن (Goffman, 1963)، فرد انگ‌خورده معمولاً سعی می‌کند انگ خود را پنهان کند (Passing). اما گیک فناوری برعکس عمل می‌کند: او عمداً تفاوتش را بیشتر نشان می‌دهد (Reverse Passing) — مثلاً با استفاده از اصطلاحات فنی پیچیده، لباس غیرعادی، و آخرین گجت‌ها.

چرا این اتفاق می‌افتد؟ چون تخصص فنی به گیک یک «سپر اعتبار» (Status Shield) می‌دهد — یعنی می‌تواند هنجارهای اجتماعی را نادیده بگیرد و باز هم شغلش را حفظ کند، چون سازمان به مهارت‌هایش نیاز دارد.

نتیجه: این رفتار هم مزایا دارد (اجتناب از کارهای ناخوشایند، تقویت عزت نفس) و هم معایب (رشد حرفه‌ای محدود، کاهش اشتراک دانش در سازمان). هفت گزاره (P1-P7) این پویایی‌ها را توصیف می‌کنند.

ارتباط با پیپر Jia: Moore & Love فکر می‌کنند مشکل ارتباطی گیک بیشتر «نخواستن» (unwillingness) است. اما Jia نشان می‌دهد که بخشی از آن «نتوانستن» (inability) است — یک تفاوت عصبی-شناختی واقعی.

🎯 Core Question - سوال اصلی

English: How does the combination of prestige and stigma shape the technology geek's behavior in organizational interactions?

فارسی: ترکیب اعتبار و انگ اجتماعی چگونه رفتار «گیک فناوری» را در تعاملات سازمانی شکل می‌دهد؟

📖 Theoretical Foundation: Goffman's Stigma Theory

Erving Goffman (1963) defined stigma as an attribute that discredits someone, reducing them from a "whole and usual person" to a "tainted, discounted one." A stigmatized person has a gap between their virtual social identity (what society expects) and their actual social identity (who they really are).

Key Concepts from Goffman:
Concept Definition Application to Tech Geek
Mixed Interaction Contact between stigmatized and "normals" Meetings between IT geeks and business people
Tension Management The stigmatized person's effort to smooth interactions Geeks are expected to but often decline to do this
Passing Hiding the stigma to appear "normal" Geeks often choose not to pass
Stigma Symbols Signs that draw attention to the stigma Tech gadgets, non-standard dress, jargon

🔑 Core Innovation: The Concept of "Prestigious Stigma"

Moore & Love's key insight is that some stigmas come paired with prestige. The technology geek stereotype includes both negative elements (socially awkward, poor communicator) and positive ones (technical genius, indispensable). This combination creates a unique dynamic:

Traditional Stigma vs. Prestigious Stigma

Dimension Traditional Stigma Prestigious Stigma (Tech Geek)
Source of differentness Only negative attributes Negative social + positive technical attributes
Power balance Low power — normals dominate Has expert power — can push back
Tension management Stigmatized person manages tension Geek rejects tension management
Passing behavior Tries to hide stigma Does NOT try to pass
Identity display Uses "disidentifiers" to distance from stigma Uses "identifiers" to embrace stigma

🆕 Novel Concepts Introduced

1. Reverse Passing

The opposite of Goffman's "passing." Instead of hiding their differentness, the prestigiously stigmatized technology geek actively makes their differentness more noticeable. Examples:

  • Carrying the latest tech gadgets
  • Wearing clothes outside the organization's dress code
  • Using technical jargon deliberately
2. Identifiers (opposite of Goffman's "disidentifiers")

Conscious displays that enforce one's stigmatized identity and further separate oneself from normals. A key form is speech divergence — deliberately using acronyms and technical language that excludes non-technical people.

3. Status Shield

The geek's technical expertise gives them a status shield — the power and freedom to ignore social norms, skip meetings, refuse to document, and still keep their position because of their indispensable skills.

📖 Key Supporting Theories

Willingness to Communicate (WTC)

McCroskey & Richmond's (1990) WTC theory is central to the paper. WTC is a person's tendency to initiate communication when free to do so. Moore & Love argue that the technology geek has low WTC — driven by a combination of:

  • Communication Apprehension (CA): Anxiety about interacting with others — the geek doubts their social ability
  • Self-perceived communication competence: Low confidence in social skills
  • Disinterest: A genuine lack of motivation to communicate with "normals" about non-technical topics
Self-Handicapping (Jones & Berglas, 1978)

Self-handicapping means creating obstacles to your own success so that failure can be attributed to the obstacle, not to lack of ability. The geek's refusal to engage socially serves as a self-handicapping strategy:

🔑 How Self-Handicapping Works for the Geek

  • If the geek fails socially → "I wasn't trying anyway" (protects self-esteem)
  • If the geek succeeds socially → "I'm so talented I didn't even need to try" (enhances self-esteem)
  • Either way, the geek's core identity as a technical expert remains intact

💡 Why This Paper Matters

  • Extends stigma theory: Goffman never considered the case where the stigmatized person also has prestige — this creates entirely different interaction dynamics
  • Both sides lose: The model shows that while geeks gain short-term advantages from embracing their stigma, they also hurt their own career development and harm organizational knowledge sharing
  • The "wise" concept: Goffman described "the wise" as normals who are symapthetic to the stigmatized — this could be leveraged in organizations to bridge geek-normal interactions
  • Trust connection: Technical expertise alone has "almost no relationship to trust" (Krackhardt & Hanson, 1993) — so the geek's technical prestige does not automatically translate to collaborative relationships

⚠️ Limitations

  • This is a conceptual paper — all propositions are untested
  • The "technology geek" stereotype may be dated in some contexts (it was published in 2011)
  • Does not differentiate between inability and unwillingness to communicate — Jia (2025) later provides evidence that it's partially inability (ToM deficits)
  • Gender is not explicitly addressed in the model
  • Cultural context may affect how prestigious the tech geek stigma is

📖 The Story (in simple words)

These four papers, when you read them together, tell one complete story. Let me walk you through it.

It all starts with a question that IS researchers have asked for a long time: Are people who work in IT somehow different from other people? We all have some idea that "tech people" are a certain way. They are good with systems but maybe not great with people. But is that just a stereotype, or is there something real behind it?

📏 Paper 1: Baron-Cohen et al. (2001) — The Tool

Before anyone could study this question properly, we needed a way to measure these traits. Baron-Cohen and his team built the AQ-50, a 50-question test that measures how many autistic traits a person has. The key idea is that autism is not just "you have it or you don't." It is a spectrum. Everyone is somewhere on it. Some people have more autistic traits, some have fewer.

They tested it on different groups and found something interesting: students in math and computer science scored higher than students in other fields. This was the first sign that people who are drawn to technical fields might have more of these traits.

Why it matters: Without this tool, the next three papers could not exist. It gave researchers a way to actually measure what was before just a feeling.

🧲 Paper 2: Jia, Steelman & Jia (2022) — The Attraction

OK, so if tech people score higher on autistic traits, the next question is: why are these people drawn to IT in the first place?

Jia et al. used the Empathizing-Systemizing (E-S) theory to answer this. Some people are natural "systemizers." They like rules, patterns, and figuring out how things work. Technology is basically a big system. So it makes sense that people with a strong systemizing drive, which is linked to autistic tendency, would be naturally interested in IT.

But here is the really interesting finding: when you control for autistic tendency, the gender gap in IT interest disappears. In the U.S., men seemed more interested in IT than women. But Jia et al. showed this was not really about gender. It was about autistic tendency. Since men on average have slightly higher autistic tendency, it looked like a gender difference, but it was actually an individual-level trait difference.

Why it matters: This changes how we think about diversity in IT. The real driver is not "male vs. female" but "how strong is your systemizing drive."

🏷️ Paper 3: Moore & Love (2011) — The Identity

So we know these "different" people are drawn to IT. But what happens when they get there? Moore and Love built a theory about this.

They introduced the idea of prestigious stigma. This is a new concept. Usually, stigma is just bad. If people see you as different, that hurts you. But tech people have something special: everyone needs their skills. So the "tech geek" has both stigma (people think you are socially awkward) and prestige (people know they need you and your expertise).

This creates some unique behaviors. Instead of trying to hide their differences (what Goffman called "passing"), tech geeks sometimes do the opposite. They do "reverse passing", they make their geekiness even more visible. They use jargon on purpose, they dress differently, they avoid social events. Why? Because their expertise gives them a "status shield" that protects them from the usual social consequences.

Moore and Love say this happens because geeks are unwilling to communicate in "normal" ways. They choose not to.

Why it matters: This is a theoretical paper. It gives us a way to think about the unique social position of IT professionals. But it assumes the communication problem is a choice.

💔 Paper 4: Jia (2025) — The Consequences

This is the newest paper and it brings everything together with hard data.

Jia ran three studies and found that IT professionals really do have higher autistic traits than the general population. But the big contribution is showing what this actually costs them.

Because they have weaker Theory of Mind (ToM), which is the ability to read what other people are thinking and feeling, they need much more mental effort to handle social situations at work. Jia calls this "social compensation." They can still do it, but it is exhausting. It is like writing with your non-dominant hand. You can do it, but it takes a lot more energy.

This extra effort leads to emotional labor (surface acting, faking emotions), which in turn leads to real mental health problems. IT professionals in the study were 2.3 times more likely to meet the clinical cutoff for anxiety and depression.

Here is the key disagreement with Moore and Love: Moore said geeks won't communicate ("unwillingness"). Jia showed that part of it is they can't communicate easily ("inability"). ToM is 28% heritable, so it is not something you can just train away. This means social skills training might not be the answer. Instead, we need to change the work environment, things like remote work options, written communication instead of meetings, and smaller team sizes.

Why it matters: This paper moves the conversation from "interesting finding" to "this is a real problem that affects real people's health." And it challenges the common solution (just teach them social skills) by showing the problem has a biological basis.

⚡ The Big Debate: Can't vs. Won't

If I had to pick the single most important thing these papers disagree about, it is this:

Moore & Love (2011) Jia (2025)
Why do IT pros struggle socially? They choose not to (unwillingness). They have a status shield, so why bother? They find it hard to (inability). ToM deficits mean social interaction takes extra effort.
What should we do about it? Teach them to communicate, change their behavior. Change the environment. Social skills training does not fix a brain wiring difference.
The truth? Probably both. Some of it is inability (Jia's evidence is strong). But the status shield also makes it easier to just not try. They interact and make it worse.

مور و لاو می‌گن مشکل ارتباطی گیک‌ها بخاطر نخواستنه. انتخاب می‌کنن که ارتباط برقرار نکنن.

چرا مهمه: این یه مقاله نظریه‌ایه. یه چارچوب برای فکر کردن به جایگاه اجتماعی خاص متخصصان IT می‌ده. ولی فرض می‌کنه مشکل ارتباطی یه انتخابه.

📝 My Research Questions

Paper Topic: Bidirectional Trust-Delegation Framework for Agentic IS

My paper is about how trust works as a gatekeeper for delegation in human-AI relationships. When we talk about agentic IS, we mean AI systems that can act on their own, make decisions, and even decide when to ask the human for help. My main argument is that trust in this context is not just one-way (human trusts AI), it is actually bidirectional. The AI also builds something like trust about the human, learning when to escalate and when to act alone. This idea came from my discussions with Dr. Sidorova, who pointed out that "you cannot delegate until you trust" and that in agentic IS "trust is a two-way street."

Research Questions

RQ1: How does trust function as a gating mechanism for the allocation of delegation rights in human-AI agentic relationships?

RQ2: How does bidirectional trust (both human-to-AI and AI-to-human functional trust) develop over time, and how does it shape the scope of delegation?

RQ3: What are the conditions under which trust calibration failures lead to delegation misallocation (misuse, disuse, or escalation failures) in agentic IS?

1. Which conversation are you joining?

I am joining the conversation about how humans interact with autonomous AI systems, specifically the discussion around delegation and trust in agentic IS. This conversation has been growing recently because AI is not just a tool anymore. It is becoming an agent that can make its own decisions, and that changes how we think about control and trust.

Baird and Maruping (2021) started an important part of this conversation when they introduced the human-to-AI delegation framework. They argued that as AI systems gain more capabilities, we need a theory of how humans allocate decision rights to these systems. Their framework talks about "endowments" (what the AI can do) and "dispositions" (what the human wants), but it does not really address the role of trust. This is the gap that my work tries to fill.

At a broader level, I am also joining the trust literature conversation in IS. Researchers like Mayer et al. (1995) and McKnight et al. (2011) have been studying trust for decades, but mostly in human-to-human or human-to-technology settings where the technology is just a tool. When AI becomes agentic, the old models might not be enough. Glikson and Woolley (2020) did a good review of trust in AI, and they noticed that most studies treat trust as going in one direction, from the human to the AI. But if the AI can decide things on its own, maybe we need to think about trust going both ways.

There is also the delegation literature outside of IS. Gur and Bjornskov (2017) showed at the macro level that social trust actually causes delegation. They used data from 122 countries and found causal evidence that when people trust more, they delegate more. This is interesting because it confirms at a big scale what we suspect happens at individual level too. You do not delegate to someone or something you dont trust.

So basically, I am at the intersection of three conversations: the agentic IS conversation (what happens when AI acts on its own), the trust-in-AI conversation (how do we trust machines), and the delegation theory conversation (when and why do people give up control). My paper tries to bring these together into one framework.

2. What is the theoretical tension/research gap, and why is it important?

The main tension in my research comes from a simple observation: you cannot delegate until you trust, but the delegation framework in IS does not have trust in it. Baird and Maruping (2021) built a nice framework for how humans delegate to AI, talking about recommendation rights, decision rights, and execution rights. But trust is missing from their model. They focus on what the AI can do and what the human wants, but not on whether the human actually trusts the AI enough to hand over those rights. This is a real problem because in practice people do not delegate to AI just because the AI is capable. They delegate when they feel comfortable enough to do so.

The second part of the tension goes deeper, and this is where my contribution gets more original. In agentic IS, delegation is not just one-way. When an AI agent is autonomous enough to make decisions, it also has to "decide" when to ask the human for help. This is what I call reverse delegation or escalation. The AI needs some kind of model about the human, something like "does this human give good feedback?" or "is this human reliable when I ask them?" This looks a lot like trust going in the other direction, from AI to human. Existing frameworks completely miss this reverse direction.

Why does this matter? Because if we only think about trust going one way, we cannot explain some real problems that happen with AI systems. For example, why do some human-AI teams fall into a bad cycle where the human micromanages the AI, so the AI never learns properly, which makes the human trust it even less? Or the opposite, where good collaboration leads to more trust which leads to more delegation which leads to even better collaboration? These are what I call trust spirals, and they only make sense if you think about trust as bidirectional.

Also, trust calibration matters a lot here. Lee and See (2004) showed that when human trust does not match the actual AI capability, bad things happen. You get "misuse" (trusting too much and delegating things the AI cannot do well) or "disuse" (not trusting enough and not using AI even when it would help). But nobody has really looked at calibration from the AI side. What happens when the AI has a wrong model of the human? It might escalate too much (bothering the human with every small decision) or too little (making decisions it should have asked about). I think this is important gap in the literature.

The industry evidence also supports why this is urgent. Capgemini (2025) found that trust is the leading barrier to adopting agentic AI in organizations. So this is not just an academic exercise. Companies are dealing with this problem right now.

3. Which literature streams are you building on?

My paper builds on at least two major literature streams, and I would say there is a third one that plays supporting role.

The first stream is trust theory, particularly trust in technology and AI. This includes the foundational work by Mayer et al. (1995) who defined trust as willingness to be vulnerable based on ability, benevolence, and integrity of the trustee. For the technology side, McKnight et al. (2011) adapted trust constructs to information technology. But the most important papers for my work are the ones that deal with how trust develops over time and how it gets calibrated. Lewicki and Bunker (1996) proposed that trust develops in stages, from calculus-based trust (where you verify everything) to knowledge-based trust (where you predict behavior because you know the other party) to identification-based trust (where you share values). Lee and See (2004) added the calibration idea showing that trust needs to match the actual trustworthiness of the system for things to work properly. Hoff and Bashir (2015) brought all this together with their three-layer model of trust in automation (dispositional, situational, and learned trust). I am taking these models and applying them in both directions, which is new.

The second stream is delegation theory in IS, especially human-to-AI delegation. The key paper here is Baird and Maruping (2021) who proposed a framework for how humans delegate decision rights to AI. They talk about different types of rights (recommendation, decision, execution) and how AI capabilities ("endowments") and human goals ("dispositions") determine what gets delegated. More recently, Konsynski et al. (2024) and Liu et al. (2025) have continued this conversation in MISQ, looking at how AI agents interact with humans in organizational settings. Outside IS, the economics literature on delegation is also relevant. Gur and Bjornskov (2017) established causal link between trust and delegation using instrumental variables, showing that trust is not just correlated with delegation but actually causes it. This gives macro-level support for my theoretical argument.

The supporting stream is CASA theory and agentic IS. CASA theory, from Nass et al. (1994), says that people naturally treat computers as social actors. I use this to justify why it makes sense to talk about AI having "trust" in the human. It is not that the AI literally feels trust, but it develops functional analogs of trust, behavioral patterns that look like trust and serve similar function. This is what makes the bidirectional framework theoretically defensible. Without CASA, someone could say "AI cannot trust, so your reverse direction makes no sense." But if we accept that humans treat AI as social actors, and AI develops learned models of human behavior, then bidirectional trust is a reasonable theoretical construct.

What I found interesting while going through the course readings is that some of the papers we read in earlier weeks also connect to this. For example, Gregor (2006) from Week 3 talks about different types of theory, and my paper fits her Type IV (explanation and prediction) since I am trying to explain how trust and delegation interact and predict outcomes. Baird (2021) from Week 1 on how to write theory papers was also useful because he talks about finding "theoretical tension" rather than just gap-spotting, and that is exactly what I did. My tension is not just "nobody studied this" but rather "the current framework has a contradiction because it ignores the mechanism (trust) that enables the process (delegation)."

🔭 Possible Extensions of This Week's Papers

  1. Longitudinal study of emotional labor: Jia (2025) is cross-sectional. A longitudinal study could track IT professionals over years to see if emotional labor accumulates or if people adapt.
  2. Remote work as natural experiment: COVID-19 forced remote work. Did IT professionals with high autistic traits experience less emotional labor during remote work? This would directly test the accommodation hypothesis.
  3. AI as social mediator: Could AI tools (like Copilot, ChatGPT) reduce the emotional labor of IT-business communication? IT professionals could use AI to translate technical language into business language, reducing the "speech divergence" Moore & Love describe.
  4. Testing prestigious stigma propositions: Moore & Love's 7 propositions are untested. A mixed-methods study (surveys + interviews) could empirically test whether tech geeks actually engage in reverse passing and benefit from status shields.
  5. Within-IT comparison: Jia flags this as a limitation. Comparing autistic traits across IT sub-roles (developer vs. business analyst vs. PM vs. security) could show that within-group variation is as important as between-group differences.
  6. Cultural replication: Jia et al. (2022) compared U.S. and India. What about other cultures? In some cultures (e.g., East Asia), the "geek" stereotype may not carry prestige at all.

📖 Key Terms to Know

📚 Vocabulary Check

  • AQ-50: 50-item Autism-Spectrum Quotient questionnaire (Baron-Cohen et al.)
  • Theory of Mind (ToM): The ability to infer others' mental states
  • Eyes Test: "Reading the Mind in the Eyes" — a ToM assessment task
  • E-S Theory: Empathizing-Systemizing — people vary in empathizing vs. systemizing drives
  • PIIT: Personal Innovativeness with IT — intrinsic willingness to try new technology (Agarwal & Prasad, 1998)
  • Surface Acting: Faking emotional expressions to meet work expectations
  • Social Compensation: Achieving the same social outcome but with much more effort
  • Camouflaging: Hiding autistic traits to appear neurotypical — exhausting over time
  • Prestigious Stigma: A stigma that simultaneously carries prestige (Moore & Love)
  • Mixed Interaction: Contact between stigmatized and non-stigmatized individuals (Goffman)
  • Reverse Passing: Actively making one's differentness MORE noticeable
  • Identifiers: Behaviors that cement one's stigmatized identity (opposite of disidentifiers)
  • Status Shield: The protection from social norms that prestige provides
  • Allostatic Load: The physiological toll of chronic stress on the body
  • Willingness to Communicate (WTC): An individual's tendency to initiate communication
  • Fundamental Attribution Error: Mistaking individual-level differences (autistic tendency) for group-level differences (gender)
