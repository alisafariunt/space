1️⃣ Paper 1: IS Success - Quest for Independent Variables

Petter, S., DeLone, W., & McLean, E. R. (2013). Information systems success: The quest for the independent variables. Journal of Management Information Systems, 29(4), 7-61.
Research Question

What are the important antecedents (independent variables) of IS success across six success dimensions?

The Updated D&M IS Success Model

📊 Six Success Dimensions

  1. System Quality - Technical performance, reliability, ease of use
  2. Information Quality - Accuracy, completeness, relevance, timeliness
  3. Service Quality - Support, responsiveness (added in 2003 update)
  4. Use / Intention to Use - Actual usage behavior
  5. User Satisfaction - Overall contentment with the system
  6. Net Benefits - Individual and organizational impact

Five Categories of Antecedents (Leavitt's Diamond)
Category Description Examples
Technology System characteristics, features, capabilities System functionality, data quality, interface design
Task Nature of work being performed Task complexity, task variety, task interdependence
People User characteristics and capabilities Computer self-efficacy, experience, training
Structure Organizational context Management support, organizational climate
Project Implementation approach User participation, project management quality
Key Findings

💡 Strong Relationships Found

  • System Quality → User Satisfaction (well-supported)
  • Information Quality → User Satisfaction (well-supported)
  • Service Quality is critical (emerged as important dimension)
  • Use ↔ User Satisfaction (closely interrelated)

⚠️ Research Gaps Identified

  • Limited research on indirect relationships between antecedents and success
  • Most studies focus on direct effects only
  • Need more research on Service Quality antecedents
  • Organizational Impact less studied than Individual Impact
Implications for Research & Practice

For Researchers:

  • Use this framework to identify which antecedents matter most for different success dimensions
  • Consider indirect pathways (e.g., Technology → System Quality → User Satisfaction)
  • Study Service Quality more - it's understudied but important

For Practitioners:

  • Focus on all six success dimensions, not just Use
  • Invest in Service Quality (support, training) alongside System and Information Quality
  • Recognize feedback loops - success breeds more use, which breeds more success

Research Questions
  1. What are pertinent affective concepts in the ICT context?
  2. In what ways are these affective concepts similar to or different from each other?
  3. How do these affective concepts relate to each other?

Dimension Description Values

💡 Why ARM Matters

  • Systematic taxonomy for classifying affective concepts in ICT research

Navigability Metrics (Data-Driven Approach)

📊 Three Components of Navigability

Component Measures Interpretation
Power R(U) Probability of finding target content Higher = more likely to find what you need
Efficiency Q(U) Minimizing clicks to reach target Higher = fewer clicks needed
Directness L(U) Ease of deciding next move Higher = clearer navigation choices

Overall Navigability: Nav(U) = Harmonic mean of R(U), Q(U), L(U)

Experimental Design
Aspect Details
Setting Two university websites (Site A vs. Site B)
Participants University students
Tasks 12 experimental tasks (finding locations, services, information)
Navigability Difference Site A: 0.62 vs. Site B: 0.54 (14.7% difference)
Measures Cognitive load (survey), Performance (time, accuracy), Satisfaction (survey)
Key Findings

✅ Supported Hypotheses

  • Better navigability → Lower cognitive load
  • Lower cognitive load → Better performance outcomes
  • Better performance → Higher user satisfaction
  • User familiarity moderates the navigability-cognitive load relationship

🔍 Mediation Mechanism

The relationship between navigability and satisfaction is fully mediated by:

  1. Cognitive Load (thinking effort required)
  2. Performance Outcomes (task completion success/speed)

This means: Good navigation doesn't directly make users happy. Instead, it reduces mental effort → improves task performance → leads to satisfaction.

Practical Implications

For Website Designers:

  • Use data-driven metrics (web logs) to measure navigability, not just opinions
  • Focus on reducing cognitive load - make navigation decisions easy
  • Test with users who have varying familiarity levels

For Researchers:

  • Don't assume direct effects - look for mediating mechanisms
  • Consider both objective metrics (performance) and subjective perceptions (cognitive load)
  • User characteristics (familiarity) moderate usability effects

📊 Liu et al. (2025) - Human-AI Delegation Dynamics

Liu, J., Yue, W. T., Leung, A. C. M., & Zhang, X. (2025). Find the Good, Seek the Unity: A Hidden Markov Model of Human-AI Delegation Dynamics. MIS Quarterly, 49(3), 1185-1204.

🎯 1. Introduction: The Research Problem

The Big Picture

The Challenge: As AI becomes part of enterprise decision-making, we need to understand how humans collaborate with AI systems over time. Most research takes a static view, but human-AI relationships are actually dynamic - they evolve through continuous interaction.

Real-World Example: Walmart's AI inventory system doesn't just run automatically - employees constantly provide feedback to refine the AI. This ongoing interaction shapes how much employees trust and delegate to the AI system.

The Gap: Existing IS literature assumes predefined rules and static adoption decisions. This misses the unique dynamics where humans and AI constantly gather data, adapt behaviors, and learn from each other.

Research Questions

  • RQ1: How does managers' willingness to delegate tasks to AI systems evolve over time?
  • RQ2: What role does performance feedback play in shaping delegation dynamics?
  • RQ3: Do different managers develop different delegation patterns, and why?

📚 2. Theoretical Foundation: Three Pillars

Pillar 1: Agentic Systems Delegation Framework (ADF)

From: Baird & Maruping (2021)

Key Idea: Delegation is a continuous feedback loop, not a one-time decision.

  • Willingness to Delegate: Based on perceived AI capability and goal attainment
  • Delegation Decision: Observable action - what tasks to give to AI
  • Delegation Outcome: Performance results that shape future perceptions
  • Feedback Loop: Outcomes → Perceived Capability → Willingness → Decision → Outcomes

Pillar 2: Instance-Based Learning Theory (IBLT)

From: Gonzalez et al. (2003)

Key Idea: Humans make decisions in dynamic environments by relying on past experiences.

  • Learning Process: Retrieve similar past experiences to inform current choices
  • Adaptation: Refine knowledge base by incorporating feedback from action outcomes
  • Connection to ADF: Explains HOW humans update their perceived AI capability - through accumulated experience

Pillar 3: Hidden Markov Model (HMM) - The Analytical Tool

Why HMM? Perfect for modeling hidden (willingness) and observable (decisions) states

  • Observable: Delegation decisions (% of tasks delegated)
  • Unobservable: Delegation willingness (attitude toward AI)
  • Dynamic: States change over time based on performance feedback
  • Markov Property: Future states depend only on current state (past is embedded in current)

🏪 3. Research Context: The Real-World Setting

The Company & AI System

  • Industry: Large retail company (1,000+ stores) selling perishable bakery and dairy products
  • Challenge: High demand volatility + short shelf-life = complex replenishment decisions
  • AI Solution: Sequence-to-sequence deep learning algorithm embedded in ERP system
  • AI Input: External data (weather, holidays) + Internal data (sales, inventory)
  • AI Output: Automated daily replenishment decisions (~70 per day per store)

Human-in-the-Loop Design

  • Manager Role: Monitor AI decisions and override when appropriate
  • Override Range: 0% to 100% of AI decisions
  • Override Requirement: Brief comment explaining reason for not delegating
  • AI Transparency: Managers don't understand inner algorithms but observe sales performance

Performance Feedback: Three KPIs (Weekly Assessment Meetings)

  1. Team vs. Human (TH): Human-AI collab performance vs. human working alone (pre-AI baseline)
  2. Team vs. AI (TA): Team performance vs. pure AI without human intervention
  3. Team vs. Team (TT): Store's team performance vs. regional average of other teams

Study Data

  • Period: January 2017 - June 2018
  • Sample: 875 store managers who used AI for 30+ weeks
  • Data Collected: Daily delegation decisions, sales outcomes, environmental conditions

⚙️ 4. Methodology: How HMM Works in This Study

HMM Components: λ = (π, Q, A)

Element 1: Initial State Distribution (π)

  • Probability distribution of manager's willingness state in period t=1
  • Assumption: Uniform distribution among all latent states

Element 2: State Transition Matrix (Q)

  • Probability of transitioning from state j to state k from time t to t+1
  • Equation 1 - Latent Propensity: Zijt = βijRit + uijk + ξi
  • Where Rit = vector of performance indicators (TH, TA, TT)
  • βij = state-dependent coefficients
  • uijk = inherent transition threshold
  • ξi = manager-specific random effect
  • Equation 2 - Transition Probability (Ordered Logit): qjkt = P(St+1=k | St=j)

Element 3: Emission Probability (A)

  • Probability of observing delegation decision given current state
  • Equation 4 - Delegation Decision (Gaussian): P(Oit | λ, Sit=j) = exp(-(Oit - (ρijWt + ηij + δi))2)
  • Where Wt = control variables (implementation age, bad weather, holidays)
  • ηij = state-dependent emission intercept
  • ρij = state-dependent effect of controls
  • δi = manager-specific heterogeneity

Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Find optimal parameters λ* by maximizing the probability of observing the sequence of delegation decisions Oi

Finding 1: State-Dependent Delegation Decisions

Discovery: Managers fall into three distinct willingness states with different delegation behaviors

Statistical Test: State 3 is significantly higher than State 2 (diff=0.1118, p <0.05) and State 1 (diff=0.2054, p<0.01)

Time Trends by State (Table 3 - Emission Factors)

  • Low-Willingness (L): Implementation Age = -0.3028*** → Delegate fewer tasks over time (both short & long term)
  • Medium (M): Implementation Age = -0.1730*** → Initially delegate fewer, smaller downtrend
  • High-Willingness (H): Implementation Age² = 0.1231** → U-shaped: Initially delegate fewer, but increase in long term

Environmental Conditions Impact

  • Bad Weather effect on High-Willingness: -0.0560** (delegate less during bad weather)
  • Holiday effect on High-Willingness: -0.0958** (delegate less during holidays)
  • Low-Willingness: No significant effect (not sensitive to environmental complexity)

Interpretation: High-willingness managers are more careful in assessing AI capability under complex/rare conditions that AI may not handle well due to limited training data.

Finding 2: Intrinsic "Stickiness" - Managers Tend to Stay in Their State

Discovery: There's a strong polarization - managers in low and high states tend to remain there

Sensitivity Analysis: How KPIs Affect Transitions

Team vs. Human (TH: -30% to +30% improvement)

  • Low-Willingness: 71.90% → 67.59% staying low (4.31% decrease)
  • High-Willingness: 55.62% → 63.40% staying high (7.78% increase)
  • Conclusion: Better team performance reinforces high-willingness, slightly weakens low-willingness

Team vs. AI (TA: -30% to +30%)

  • Low-Willingness: 68.08% → 71.46% staying low (when team outperforms AI)
  • High-Willingness: 65.34% → 53.29% staying high (drops 12.05% when team outperforms AI!)
  • Conclusion: High-willingness managers are highly sensitive to AI's independent performance

Team vs. Team (TT: -30% to +30%) - The Attribution Effect!

  • Low-Willingness: Positive coefficient (0.2567***) → When team wins, attribute to own effort, reinforces low-willingness
  • High-Willingness: Negative coefficient (-0.4460***) → When team wins, attribute to AI contribution, reinforces high-willingness
  • Theory: IBLT explains this - managers draw on past experiences for attribution. High-willingness managers have more AI collaboration experience, so they credit AI. Low-willingness managers have less AI experience, so they credit themselves.

Finding 5: Qualitative Evidence - Override Reasoning Quality

Method: Word cloud analysis of top 30 assessment reports (reasons for overriding AI)

High-Willingness Managers - Sound Reasoning:

  • Override due to holidays (AI lacks training data for rare events)
  • Override due to sudden changes in customer flows (store location effects)
  • Override due to special store inventory conditions
  • Override due to rare operational environments AI cannot capture
  • Pattern: Thoughtful, context-aware, recognizing AI's limitations in specific situations

Low-Willingness Managers - Poor Reasoning:

  • Repetition of words (nonserious nature)
  • Meaningless or facile reasons
  • Failure to evaluate AI advantages
  • Pattern: Irrational, unconstructive, superficial feedback showing lack of interest in AI

Insight: High-willingness managers have a better sense of AI's incapability under rare conditions. They understand when to trust AI and when not to - this is true collaborative intelligence!

💡 6. Theoretical Contributions

Contribution 1: Operationalizing ADF and IBLT with HMM

First study to use HMM to model AI delegation dynamics in a longitudinal setting. HMM provides a distinct approach to:

  • Leverage human-AI interaction data to identify unobservable delegation willingness
  • Track how willingness evolves through feedback loops
  • Uncover dynamic behavioral aspects previously unseen in IS research

Contribution 2: Polarization Phenomenon

Revealed the importance of initial delegation willingness and status quo that inherently affect subsequent decisions regardless of perceived AI performance:

  • Managers don't converge to single state - they polarize into low vs. high
  • Initial attitudes lead to entrenched behaviors
  • Extends ADF/IBLT by highlighting role of initial attitudes (not just performance feedback)
  • Resonates with LMX theory's "in-group" and "out-group" relationships

Contribution 3: Process-Oriented Perspective

Goes beyond static IS resistance literature (e.g., Lapointe & Rivard 2005) by:

  • Capturing dynamic interaction process in delegation feedback loops
  • Highlighting behavioral aspects of human-AI collaboration over time
  • Unveiling new insights into this increasingly crucial relationship

🏢 7. Practical Implications for Organizations

1. Manage Early Experiences Carefully

Since initial willingness creates sticky states, organizations must:

  • Ensure positive early AI interactions
  • Provide training to overcome initial resistance
  • Identify low-willingness employees early and intervene

2. Transparency and Explainability

Aligns with Explainable AI (XAI) movement:

  • Clearly illustrate how AI system was designed and trained
  • Explain AI's capabilities AND weaknesses
  • Make attribution clearer when team performance is assessed

3. Targeted Policies for Different Groups

Solely focusing on transparent decision-making is insufficient:

  • Use interaction data to identify potential misalignment
  • Develop targeted policies combining education and incentives
  • Nurture positive interactions with AI for resistant groups

4. Design Effective KPIs

Different KPIs have different effects:

  • Team vs. Human: Shows overall value of AI collaboration
  • Team vs. AI: Highlights AI's independent capability (most influential for high-willingness)
  • Team vs. Team: Can backfire due to attribution differences - use carefully

⚠️ 8. Limitations & Future Research Directions

Limitations

  • HMM Assumptions: Model allowed estimating unobserved willingness but couldn't pinpoint exact reasons for attitudes
  • Limited KPIs: Only three performance indicators - more comprehensive feedback system could reveal finer dynamics
  • Environmental Conditions: Only captured weather and holidays - other operational complexities may matter
  • Underlying Mechanisms: Personal traits not examined (risk perceptions, learning goal orientations)

Future Research Opportunities

  • Field Interventions: Design experiments to conduct causal inference on delegation willingness
  • Individual Differences: Study role of personality traits, risk perceptions, learning orientations
  • Fine-Grained Feedback: Develop comprehensive performance evaluation systems
  • Social Dynamics: Apply HMM to study unobservable attitudes in social aspects of human-AI collaboration
  • Intervention Effectiveness: Test which interventions can shift low-willingness managers to higher states

AI Governance & Trust

  • Trust Dynamics: Trust doesn't just increase linearly - it polarizes! This challenges simple trust-building strategies
  • Trust Maintenance: The "stickiness" shows trust (or distrust) is self-reinforcing through selective attention to feedback

Human-in-the-Loop Systems

  • Collaborative Intelligence: High-willingness managers demonstrate true collaboration - knowing when to delegate and when to override

Accountability Mechanisms

Extensions for Your Theory Paper

  • Polarization as Risk: The polarization finding could inform governance structures that prevent entrenchment

📝 10. Summary: The Core Story

The Big Picture

"Find the Good, Seek the Unity" - This title captures the essence:

  • "Find the Good": High-willingness managers actively seek to understand AI's strengths and weaknesses
  • "Seek the Unity": They work toward human-AI unity through collaborative intelligence

The Core Findings in One Slide

  1. Three States Exist: Managers have Low, Medium, or High willingness to delegate to AI
  2. States are Sticky: 63.5% (Low) and 66.5% (High) stay in their state - polarization
  3. KPIs Shape Transitions: Performance feedback influences willingness, but differently for each state
  4. Attribution Differs: Same outcome, different credit assignment (self vs. AI)
  5. Performance Differs: High-willingness managers outperform by 10.92%

The Theoretical Innovation

Used HMM to model the unobservable (willingness) from the observable (decisions, outcomes), revealing dynamic patterns that static models miss.

The Practical Takeaway

Organizations deploying AI must:

  1. Create positive early experiences (prevent low-willingness polarization)
  2. Use targeted interventions for different willingness groups
  3. Design KPIs carefully (they shape attribution and willingness)
  4. Foster transparency and explainability (build understanding, not just compliance)

📝 11. Article Critique: My Analysis

Summary and Theoretical Contribution

This paper studies how managers decide to delegate tasks to AI systems over time. The authors used data from 875 store managers who worked with an AI replenishment system for bakery and dairy products. The interesting finding is that managers don't just accept or reject AI once, they develop different "willingness states" that change based on how well the AI performs.

The paper tests and extends the Agentic Delegation Framework (ADF) that Baird and Maruping proposed in 2021. ADF said delegation should be dynamic but nobody had proven it with real data. Liu and colleagues showed that yes, willingness changes over time, but they also found something new which is polarization. Some managers get stuck in low willingness (63% stay there) while others get stuck in high willingness (66% stay there). This wasn't predicted by ADF.

The main theoretical contribution is combining ADF with Instance-Based Learning Theory (IBLT). The idea is that managers learn from experience. When they delegate more they learn what AI can and can't do. When they delegate less, they never learn enough to trust it. This creates a "delegation feedback loop" where past decisions shape future willingness. The polarization happens because managers with different initial attitudes accumulate different experiences which then reinforces their original stance. This is the new insight the paper adds to existing theory.

Theory Classification

Using Gregor's framework this is Type IV: Explanation and Prediction. The paper explains what causes changes in delegation willingness (the three performance indicators) and predicts future behavior using the HMM model. It's not just descriptive because it provides evidence for the proposed mechanisms. It's not purely explanatory because the model has predictive power. And it's not design science because they didn't create an artifact or intervention.

Constructs and Variables

The independent variables are three Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) that managers see in weekly meetings. Team vs. Human measures the sales improvement after AI compared to pre-AI baseline. Team vs. AI shows how the human-AI team performs versus what pure AI alone would have achieved. Team vs. Team compares how the manager's store compares to regional average. There are also control variables like implementation age, bad weather days and holidays.

The dependent variable is Delegation Decision which is the percentage of tasks actually delegated to AI each week. On average managers delegated 76% of tasks.

The key mediator is Willingness to Delegate, this is a latent state (not directly observed) that the HMM infers from patterns of delegation decisions. The model identifies three states: Low, Medium and High. The paper reports that high willingness managers delegate around 94% of tasks on average.

The model shows KPIs influence willingness state which then influences delegation decisions. But it's also a feedback loop because delegation decisions affect what managers learn about AI, which feeds back into willingness.

Comparison with Petter et al. (2013)

When I compare this to Petter's paper on IS Success there are interesting connections. Petter found that user characteristics like Trust and Enjoyment predict Use and Satisfaction. Liu's "willingness to delegate" is similar to trust, both are attitudes toward the system. Both papers show user attitudes really matter.

Another connection is task characteristics. Petter identified task compatibility as important. Liu controls for task complexity through bad weather and holidays which are situations where AI might not be compatible with the task.

But there's a big difference in how they think about performance. In Petter's framework organizational impact is a dependent variable, an outcome of IS success. In Liu's framework, performance (sales improvement) is an independent variable that drives future delegation through the feedback loop. Performance outcomes loop back to influence future use. I think this is more realistic for understanding AI systems where people need to learn the system's capabilities.

Operationalization

Team vs. Human is calculated as percentage change in sales compared to the 30-day pre-AI baseline. Team vs. AI is trickier, they estimate what "uncensored sales" would be (best possible with zero stockout and waste) and compare actual team sales to what AI's original recommendations would have produced. Team vs. Team compares the manager's improvement to regional average.

Delegation decision is straightforward, tasks delegated divided by total tasks. Willingness state can't be measured directly so the HMM infers it from patterns over time.

Method and Analysis

The method is a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) which is perfect because willingness to delegate is unobservable but generates observable delegation decisions. HMM models how managers transition between low, medium and high willingness states based on performance feedback.

The HMM has three parts. The initial state distribution describes starting probabilities. The transition matrix shows how managers move between states, the probability depends on the KPIs through an ordered logit function. The emission probability describes the likelihood of observing a certain delegation percentage given the state.

They estimated parameters using maximum likelihood with the BFGS algorithm. They tested different numbers of states (1-5) and found three states fit best based on BIC and AIC.

The results show managers in different states respond very differently to same performance feedback. The state-dependent coefficients show how performance indicators affect transitions differently based on current willingness. This is IBLT in action, managers interpret performance based on their accumulated experience. Low willingness managers tend to attribute good outcomes to their own override decisions while high willingness managers attribute success to effective AI collaboration.

The performance analysis is striking. High willingness managers achieved 10.92% higher sales than low willingness managers. They also did qualitative analysis showing high willingness managers had sensible reasons for overriding AI (holidays, unusual events) while low willingness managers gave facile reasons or just submitted nonsense in their reports.

Reflections

What I find most interesting is the polarization finding. It suggests some managers may remain entrenched in low delegation patterns for long periods. This has big implications for training and feedback design. Organizations can't just implement AI and hope everyone learns, they need to identify low willingness employees early and intervene.

The HMM method is also clever for studying unobservable attitudes. Most IS research uses surveys which are one-time snapshots. Inferring attitudes from behavior patterns over time might give more valid measures.

The connection to my research interests in trust and AI governance is clear. Willingness to delegate is basically about trust. The paper shows trust changes over time based on experience. For AI governance the key point is that feedback mechanisms matter a lot, different performance indicators might create different learning patterns and trust levels.

Practical Implications

📋 For Platform Designers

  1. Use Bots for Immediate Engagement: Helpful to boost visibility of good content
  2. Focus on Quality: Bot comments must be attractive and relevant
  3. Target Strategically: Most effective for
    • New users who need encouragement
    • Users with small follower bases
    • Shorter, digestible posts
  4. Don't Expect Behavioral Change: Bots won't make users post more or differently

For Users:

  • Be aware that some engagement may be bot-generated
  • Evaluate comment quality, not just quantity

Implications for IS Research

💡 Key Takeaways for Your Own Research

1. Multiple Perspectives Matter

  • Rational (system performance) + Affective (emotions) + Cognitive (mental processes)
  • Don't study just one - they're interrelated

2. Look for Mechanisms, Not Just Correlations

  • Hu et al.: Navigability → Cognitive Load → Performance → Satisfaction
  • Zhang: Different types of affect have different pathways
  • Liu et al.: Performance → Learning → Willingness changes

3. Dynamics Over Time

  • Liu et al. shows attitudes evolve and polarize
  • Gao et al. shows short-term ≠ long-term effects
  • Static snapshots miss important processes

4. Context & Contingencies

  • User characteristics moderate effects
  • One-size-fits-all solutions rarely work
  • Need to understand when and for whom effects occur

5. Measurement Matters

  • Hu et al.: Data-driven navigability metrics vs. subjective ratings
  • Liu et al.: HMM to infer latent states from observable behaviors
  • Zhang: Precise definitions avoid conceptual confusion
