PhD Seminar | Spring 2026 | Dr. Anna Sidorova | University of North Texas

Course Overview

This interactive study guide accompanies the BCIS 6670 PhD seminar focused on Artificial Intelligence in IS Research. The seminar covers different research paradigms, theoretical frameworks, and practical skills for conducting high-quality IS research.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand different theories used in IS research
  • Know how to plan and execute an IS research project
  • Learn about important IS papers, especially ones about AI
  • Be able to read and evaluate other people's research
  • Write about research problems clearly
  • Get ready for doing real IS research in your career

Weekly Sessions

Week 0: Course Introduction

Syllabus, learning objectives, grading, semester schedule, research paradigms, AIS Basket of 11 Premier Journals, and course policies.

Week 1: Introduction to IS Research

How to write and review IS research papers. Covers Baird (2021), Sarker et al. (2023), and Sun et al. (2025). Also includes the AIS Basket of 11 Premier Journals.

Week 2: AI in IS Research

The sociotechnical axis, digital objects, and agentic IS artifacts. Covers Sarker et al. (2019), Faulkner & Runde (2019), Baird & Maruping (2021), and Gartner 2026 trends.

Key Concepts from This Course

  • Theoretical Tension: The problem or contradiction your research addresses
  • Gap Spotting: Finding what's missing in literature (weak approach)
  • Assumption Challenging: Questioning what everyone takes for granted (strong approach)
  • q-r Framework: Quality of idea (q) vs. quality of execution (r)
  • Sociotechnical: Studying both social and technical aspects together
  • Agentic IS: AI systems that can perceive, act, and learn autonomously

Note-Taking Guide

For each paper you read, take notes on the following aspects. This helps you engage deeply with the material and prepare for class discussions.

Strategic Reading Framework

Aspect What to Note
Content What is the main message? What are the key findings?
Methodology How are arguments made? What methods are used?
Structure How is the paper organized? What is the sentence-level craft?
Gaps What is missing? What questions remain unanswered?
Citations What interesting references should you explore further?

Professor's Advice

"Buy a physical notebook and pens. I will check your handwritten notes on papers. Notes show engagement with readings."
