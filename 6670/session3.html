📄 MY PART 1 ESSAY - Ready for Submission

Summary of Burton-Jones et al. (2021): How to Analyze and Construct Theory

Burton-Jones et al. (2021) wrote an introduction to MISQ's special issue on "next-generation theorizing" in IS. The paper is not a traditional theory paper itself. Instead, it provides a meta-level framework for how we should think about theorizing in our field.

The main argument is simple but important: some researchers cannot do "next-generation" theoretical work because they are held back by outdated views on what theory is. To fix this, the authors propose four critical shifts:

The 4 Shifts in Theorizing:

1. From Product to Conversation: Instead of seeing theory as a fixed set of statements, we should see it as an evolving conversation among scholars. A theory in one paper is just a "version stabilized" at one point in time.

2. From Truth to Lens: Instead of asking "does this theory mirror reality?", we should ask "what does this lens help us see?" Different theories are like different lenses - each brings different things into focus.

3. From Desk Work to Reflective Engagement: Theory does not only come from abstract thinking at a desk. It can also come from engaging with practitioners, doing design research, or learning through action.

4. From Representational to Performative: Theories do not just describe the world - they can shape it. When practitioners adopt a theory, it becomes "enrolled into world-making activities."

How to Use This Framework to Analyze Existing Theory:

When I look at an existing theory (like Trust Theory or TAM), I can ask: What scholarly conversation is this theory part of? What does it help us see, and what does it miss? Did it come from desk work or engagement with practice? How has it shaped the practice it describes?

How to Use This Framework to Construct New Theory:

When building new theory, I should: position my theory in the ongoing scholarly conversation, be explicit about what my lens illuminates and misses, consider learning from practice not just abstract reasoning, and think about how my theory might shape the world if practitioners adopt it.

The authors also introduce three criteria: Motivate (reveal problems with existing theory), Create (advance or create new theory), and Mobilize (inspire others to research differently).

In short, Burton-Jones gives us a way to think about theory building that goes beyond "what constructs should I have?" (that is Whetten). It asks us to think about the nature of theorizing itself.

Analyzing Trust Theory Using Burton-Jones Framework

I chose to analyze Trust Theory because it is central to my research on delegation in human-AI systems. 1. Theory as Conversation: Trust Theory has a rich scholarly conversation. It started in psychology and organizational behavior (Mayer et al., 1995) and was brought into IS by McKnight et al. The theory keeps evolving as scholars add new dimensions. This fits Burton-Jones's view of theory as an ongoing dialogue rather than a finished product. 2. Theory as Lens: Trust Theory provides a lens to see relationships between users and systems. The main constructs are ability, benevolence, and integrity (from Mayer et al.) and trusting beliefs and trusting intentions (from McKnight et al.). This lens helps us see why users accept or reject technology. What might this lens miss? It focuses on individual-level trust and may miss organizational or societal factors. For AI systems, the lens might not fully capture concepts like explainability, fairness, or algorithm transparency. A next-generation approach could expand the lens to include these new dimensions that matter for AI trust.

3. Theory as Reflective Engagement: Most Trust Theory development came from desk work. Mayer et al. (1995) synthesized literature and proposed their model. McKnight et al. (2002) adapted it for IS through logical reasoning. There has been much empirical testing, but less practitioner engagement. According to Burton-Jones, stronger theorizing might come from working with UX designers, AI developers, or policy makers who deal with trust issues in practice. Action research or design science approaches could help Trust Theory become more practically grounded.

4. Theory as Performative: Trust Theory has already been performative. It shapes how organizations design websites (trust signals like seals, reviews), how they communicate privacy policies, and how they try to build user confidence. For AI, the theory is starting to shape how explainable AI is designed and how transparency is communicated. When we use concepts like "competence-based trust," we create practices around demonstrating AI competence. This is exactly what Burton-Jones means by theories being "enrolled into world-making activities."

  • Motivate: ✅ Strong historically, but needs updating - Original motivation was e-commerce, but now AI presents new challenges like opacity and emergent behavior
  • Create: ✅ Moderate - Has created useful constructs, but needs new ones for AI context (like calibrated trust, appropriate reliance)
  • Mobilize: ✅ Strong - Has influenced practice in web design and e-commerce, starting to influence AI design

Opportunities for Next-Generation Trust Theory:

  • Move from trust as a static belief to trust as a dynamic process that calibrates over time
  • Add constructs for AI-specific factors like transparency, explainability, and appropriate reliance
  • Engage more with practitioners to understand real-world trust challenges in AI deployment
  • Consider how trust theory can be performative in shaping responsible AI guidelines

Conclusion: Trust Theory has been a very useful theory in IS research for over 20 years. Through the Burton-Jones lens, we can see it has strong roots as a scholarly conversation and has been performative in practice. However, to be a truly next-generation theory for the AI era, it needs more reflective engagement with practitioners and new constructs that address AI-specific trust challenges like delegation, calibration, and appropriate reliance. 4. Conceptual Latitude

Different research approaches have different degrees of "conceptual latitude" - flexibility in how concepts can be defined and applied.

  • High latitude: Concepts are flexibly defined, adapt to context
  • Low latitude: Concepts are precisely defined, universal application

Trade-off

Higher precision enables comparison but may miss context-specific nuances. Higher flexibility captures nuance but limits comparability.

5. Fit Between Theory and Research Question

Key insight: Different research questions call for different types of theory.

If your question is about... Consider using...
Categories and classification Type I (Analyzing) - taxonomies, frameworks
Mechanisms and understanding Type II (Explaining) - process theories
Outcomes and predictions Type III or IV - variance theories
Action and design Type V - design science

Burton-Jones expands on what constitutes a theoretical contribution:

  • Conceptual contribution: New concepts or reconceptualizations

  • Boundary contribution: New scope conditions
  • Integrative contribution: Synthesizing multiple theories

Paper 2: What Constitutes a Theoretical Contribution?

Whetten, D. A. (1989). What constitutes a theoretical contribution? Academy of Management Review, 14(4), 490-495.
0. Citation

Seminal AMR paper that remains the gold standard for evaluating theoretical contributions in management and IS research.

1. Goal

To provide clear criteria for evaluating what makes a theoretical contribution legitimate and how to distinguish theoretical development from other types of academic contributions.

2. Audience

Researchers developing theory and reviewers evaluating theoretical contributions.

3. The Four Essential Elements of Theory

🎯 The Core Framework: What, How, Why, When/Where

Whetten argues that a complete theory must address four questions. The first three provide the initial theoretical statement, while the fourth defines the boundaries.

Element Question Answered Description Evaluation Criteria
WHAT Which factors? The constructs, variables, or concepts that are part of the explanation Comprehensiveness (all relevant?) + Parsimony (too many?)
HOW How are they related? The relationships and connections between the constructs Usually shown as arrows in models, boxes and arrows diagrams
WHY Why do these relationships exist? The underlying logic, mechanisms, and theoretical reasoning ⚠️ MOST CRITICAL! This is where real contribution happens
WHEN/WHERE/WHO Under what conditions? Boundary conditions, scope, and generalizability limits Discovered through replications and extensions

⚠️ The Most Important Element: WHY

Whetten emphasizes that "WHY" is the key to theoretical contribution.

"Merely identifying new concepts or relationships is often insufficient; a true contribution comes from developing new and compelling 'whys' that alter the prior understanding of how relationships operate."

The "why" provides:

  • Logical explanations for why the relationships exist
  • Underlying mechanisms that make the connections plausible
  • Theoretical reasoning that can be tested and debated

The "What" Element in Detail

When identifying constructs, ask two questions:

  • Comprehensiveness: Are all relevant factors included?
  • Parsimony: Should some factors be deleted for adding little value?

Tension exists between these two - you need enough constructs to explain the phenomenon, but not so many that the model becomes unwieldy.

The "How" Element in Detail

This is typically represented visually as:

  • Boxes and arrows diagrams
  • Research models with hypotheses
  • Path models showing causal connections

The "how" adds order to conceptualization by explicitly delineating patterns.

The "When/Where/Who" Element in Detail

These are the bounding conditions that define:

  • WHO: Which populations does this apply to?
  • WHERE: In what settings or contexts?
  • WHEN: Under what temporal conditions?

Often discovered through subsequent tests and replications, not in the initial theorizing.

4. What Makes a Theoretical CONTRIBUTION?

Whetten says adding to theory can happen through:

Type of Addition Description Contribution Level
New WHATs Adding new constructs or variables Modest (unless with new WHY)
New HOWs Proposing new relationships Modest (unless with new WHY)
New WHYs New underlying logic/mechanism HIGHEST! This is the real contribution
New WHEN/WHERE New boundary conditions Moderate (extends generalizability)

💡 Key Insight for Your Research

Just saying "nobody has studied X in context Y" (gap-spotting) is not enough. You need to explain WHY studying X in context Y would change our theoretical understanding.

5. Incremental vs. Radical Contributions

Whetten distinguishes between:

  • Incremental improvements: Minor adjustments to existing theory
  • Radical thought shifts: Challenging fundamental assumptions (like Kuhn's paradigm shifts)

Both can be valuable, but the criteria are different:

  • Incremental: Needs to demonstrate clear improvement over existing understanding
  • Radical: Needs to explain why the new perspective is more valuable than the old
5b. 🎯 Whetten's 7 Key Questions for Judging Papers (EXAM CRITICAL!)

📝 MEMORIZE THESE - Used by Reviewers!

These are the 7 questions reviewers ask when evaluating any theory paper:

# Question What Reviewers Look For
1 What's new? Significant value-added contribution. Scope (how much field is impacted) vs. Degree (how different from current thinking)
2 So what? Will this change research practice? Are linkages to research evident? Are solutions proposed for deficiencies?
3 Why so? Is the underlying logic compelling? Are assumptions explicit? Are views believable? Built on sound foundation?
4 Well done? Seasoned thinking? Completeness? Multiple elements covered (What, How, Why, When)? Proper use of propositions?
5 Done well? Well written? Flows logically? Central ideas easily accessed? Enjoyable to read? Professional standards?
6 Why now? Contemporary interest? Will advance/stimulate/revitalize discussions? Not redundant/unconnected/antiquated?
7 Who cares? What % of readers interested? Narrow topics held to higher standards. Should link to core management concepts.

💡 Key Insight from Paper

Whetten emphasizes: "Critiques should focus on multiple elements" - just attacking one element is not enough. Also, "critiques should marshal compelling evidence" that is logical, empirical, OR epistemological. And "critics should propose remedies" - not just point out problems.

6. How to Use Whetten's Framework

When Reading a Paper:

  1. Identify the WHATs: What constructs are introduced?
  2. Map the HOWs: What relationships are proposed?
  3. Evaluate the WHY: Is the underlying logic compelling? Is this the real contribution?
  4. Check the boundaries: What are the scope conditions?

When Writing Your Paper:

  1. Clearly define your constructs (WHAT)
  2. Clearly show your relationships (HOW) - typically in a model
  3. Spend significant effort on explaining WHY (this is where reviewers focus)
  4. Be explicit about boundary conditions (WHEN/WHERE/WHO)

Paper 3: The Nature of Theory in Information Systems

Gregor, S. (2006). The nature of theory in information systems. MIS Quarterly, 30(3), 611-642.
0. Citation

Foundational MISQ paper that provides a taxonomy for classifying theories in IS research.

1. Goal

To provide a classification system for different types of theories based on what goals they serve, helping researchers understand what kind of theory they're building.

2. The Four Goals of Theory

Gregor identifies four fundamental goals that theories can serve:

Goal Question Answered Focus
Analysis What is it? Describing, categorizing, classifying phenomena
Explanation Why does it happen? Understanding causes and reasons
Prediction What will happen? Forecasting outcomes, testable propositions
Prescription How should it be done? Guiding action, design principles
2b. 🎯 Table 3: Gregor's 7 Theory Components (EXAM CRITICAL!)

📝 Table 3 from Gregor (2006): Structural Components of Theory

Gregor identifies 7 structural components that theories can have. Different theory types emphasize different components.

# Component Description Example
1 Means of Representation How theory is expressed Words, diagrams, tables, mathematical formulas
2 Primary Constructs Key concepts/variables Trust, perceived usefulness, delegation
3 Statements of Relationship How constructs relate X → Y, taxonomic hierarchies
4 Scope Boundaries of the theory Applies to organizations, individuals, etc.
5 Causal Explanations WHY relationships exist Mechanisms, logic, underlying reasons
6 Testable Propositions Falsifiable predictions Hypotheses that can be tested empirically
7 Prescriptive Statements Design guidance "Should" statements, recommendations

💡 Which Components for Which Type?

  • Type I (Analyzing): Components 1-4 only (no causal, no predictions, no prescriptions)
  • Type II (Explaining): Components 1-5 (no testable predictions)
  • Type III (Predicting): Components 1-4, 6 (no causal explanation!)
  • Type IV (EP): Components 1-6 (all except prescription) - GOLD STANDARD
  • Type V (Design): All 7 components possible
2c. Variance vs. Process Theories (From Gregor & Markus-Robey)

🔑 Critical Distinction in IS Research

Gregor discusses this alongside Markus & Robey. This is a KEY exam topic!

Aspect Variance Theory Process Theory
Core Question How much variance in Y is explained by X? What sequence of events leads to outcome?
Logic More X → More Y A happens → B happens → C happens
Conditions Necessary AND sufficient Necessary but NOT sufficient
Time Assumed (X before Y) Explicit (sequence matters)
Data Cross-sectional often OK Longitudinal typically needed
Quote from Gregor "Some authors argue that process and variance should be kept separate... However, an alternative view sees them as interrelated, with both necessary for developing satisfying theory."

🎯 Table 2: A Taxonomy of Theory Types in IS Research (EXAM CRITICAL!)

Different theories emphasize different combinations of goals, leading to five distinct types. This is Gregor's Table 2 from the PDF.

Type Name Goals Addressed Distinguishing Attributes Example
I Theory for Analyzing Analysis Describes "what is" - no causal relationships or predictions Taxonomies, classification schemes, frameworks
II Theory for Explaining Explanation Says how/why without testable predictions Process theories, historical analyses
III Theory for Predicting Prediction Predicts outcomes but doesn't explain why Statistical models, machine learning
IV Theory for Explaining and Predicting (EP) Explanation + Prediction Both explains WHY and predicts WHAT - most valued in IS TAM, UTAUT, most behavioral theories
V Theory for Design and Action Prescription (+ often others) Provides principles for how things should be done Design science, method papers

⚠️ Type IV (EP) is the "Gold Standard" in Behavioral IS

Most positivist behavioral research in IS aims for Type IV: Explaining and Predicting. These theories:

  • Explain WHY relationships exist (causal mechanisms)
  • Predict WHAT outcomes will occur (testable hypotheses)
  • Are falsifiable through empirical testing

Example: Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) explains WHY people adopt technology (perceived usefulness, ease of use) AND predicts WHAT will happen (adoption intention, actual use).

4. Type I: Theory for Analyzing

Characteristics:

  • Describes "what is" but not "why" or "what will happen"
  • No causal relationships
  • Focus on classification and conceptualization

Examples in IS:

  • Sarker et al.'s 6 Types: Classifies IS research types without predicting which is "better"
  • Orlikowski & Iacono's 5 Views: Classifies views of IT artifact
  • Taxonomies of system features

Value of Type I

Type I theories are foundational - they help us "see" and organize the world before we can explain or predict. They answer: "What are the relevant categories?"

5. Type II: Theory for Explaining

Characteristics:

  • Explains how and why things happen
  • No testable predictions
  • Often process-oriented

Examples in IS:

  • Process theories: How technology adoption unfolds over time
  • Historical analyses: Why certain implementations failed
  • Interpretive studies: Understanding meaning-making

Value of Type II

Type II theories provide deep understanding of mechanisms and processes. They answer: "How does this work?"

6. Type III: Theory for Predicting

Characteristics:

  • Predicts outcomes
  • Does NOT explain WHY (black box)
  • Statistical associations without mechanisms

Examples in IS:

  • Machine learning models: Predict churn without explaining why
  • Empirical correlations: "X is associated with Y"
  • Forecasting models: Predict adoption rates

Limitation of Type III

Type III theories are often criticized in academic research because they lack explanatory power. Prediction without explanation limits our ability to intervene or generalize.

7. Type IV: Theory for Explaining and Predicting (EP)

Characteristics:

  • Both explains mechanisms AND predicts outcomes
  • Testable, falsifiable propositions
  • The ideal for most behavioral research

Examples in IS:

  • Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)
  • UTAUT
  • Task-Technology Fit
  • Most variance theories in IS

💡 Why Type IV is Preferred

Type IV theories enable both understanding (why) and application (what will happen). This combination makes them scientifically valuable and practically useful.

8. Type V: Theory for Design and Action

Characteristics:

  • Prescribes HOW things should be done
  • Provides design principles
  • Guides practice

Examples in IS:

  • Design Science Research: Principles for building IS artifacts
  • Method papers: How to conduct research a certain way
  • Best practice frameworks: COBIT, ITIL

Value of Type V

Type V theories are practical - they guide action. They answer: "How should we design/build this?"

Often implicitly contain Type IV elements (predictions about what designs will work).

9. Application Example: Raisch & Fomina (2024)

Applying Gregor's framework to the hybrid decision-making paper:

Question Analysis
What type of theory? Primarily Type IV (Explaining and Predicting) with elements of Type V (Design)
Does it explain? Yes - explains WHY certain human-AI configurations work better (complementary capabilities)
Does it predict? Yes - predicts WHICH configurations lead to better decision outcomes
Does it prescribe? Implicitly - suggests how to design human-AI teams

Paper 4: Information Technology and Organizational Change

Markus, M. L., & Robey, D. (1988). Information technology and organizational change: Causal structure in theory and research. Management Science, 34(5), 583-598.
0. Citation

Foundational paper on how IS theories conceptualize causality. Provides three dimensions for analyzing causal structure in IS theories.

1. Goal

To provide a framework for understanding the causal structure of theories about IT and organizational change, helping researchers be explicit about their causal assumptions.

2. The Three Dimensions of Causal Structure

🎯 Three Fundamental Questions About Causality

When theorizing about IT and organizations, you must make choices along three dimensions:

Dimension Question Options
1. Causal Agency WHO or WHAT causes the outcomes? Technology? Organization? Emergent process?
2. Logical Structure WHAT KIND of causal logic? Variance (correlation) or Process (sequence)?
3. Level of Analysis AT WHAT LEVEL do effects occur? Individual? Group? Organization? Society?
3. Dimension 1: Causal Agency

Three Views of What Causes Organizational Change:

View Causal Agent Key Assumption Example Research Question
Technological Imperative Technology IT causes organizational change (deterministic) "How does AI change work structure?"
Organizational Imperative Organization Organizations shape IT to meet their needs "How do organizations customize IT?"
Emergent Perspective Process of interaction Effects emerge from unpredictable social dynamics "How does IT adoption unfold over time?"

⚠️ Connection to Sarker's Types!

Notice how this connects to Week 2:

  • Type V (Technical Imperative): Technology → Social outcomes
  • Type II (Social Imperative): Social → Technology outcomes
  • Type IV (Interplay): Emergent perspective
4. Dimension 2: Logical Structure

Variance Theories vs. Process Theories:

Variance Theory Process Theory
More X → More Y (correlation) Event A → Event B → Event C (sequence)
Cross-sectional snapshots Longitudinal evolution
Necessary and sufficient conditions Necessary but not sufficient conditions
Time ordering assumed (X before Y) Time ordering explicit (sequence matters)
Example: "Higher perceived usefulness → Higher adoption" Example: "Unfreezing → Change → Refreezing"

Key Difference

Variance theories: Variables co-vary. If X increases, Y increases.

Process theories: Events unfold in sequence. A must happen before B can happen.

5. Dimension 3: Level of Analysis

At What Level Do Effects Occur?

Level Focus Example Constructs
Individual Single person Trust, perceived usefulness, anxiety
Group Teams, units Team cohesion, group performance
Organization Firm level Firm performance, competitive advantage
Society/Market Industry, economy Market structure, industry adoption

⚠️ Multi-Level Complexity

Many interesting IS phenomena involve cross-level effects:

  • Individual AI acceptance → Team productivity
  • Organizational culture → Individual trust in AI

Be explicit about your level of analysis and any cross-level mechanisms.

6. Application Example: Raisch & Fomina (2024)
Dimension In Raisch & Fomina
Causal Agency Emergent Perspective - outcomes emerge from human-AI interaction, not from technology alone
Logical Structure Mix of variance (configurations → outcomes) and process (how combinations evolve)
Level of Analysis Primarily organizational/team level (decision-making units)

Application Paper: Combining Human and Artificial Intelligence

Raisch, S., & Fomina, E. (2024). Combining human and artificial intelligence: Hybrid problem-solving in organizations. Academy of Management Review.
0. Overview

This is the application paper that all students must analyze using their assigned framework. It develops theory for how human and AI agents combine for organizational problem-solving.

1. 🎯 The 3 Types of Hybrid Search (CORE FRAMEWORK)

📝 Table 1 from Raisch & Fomina (2024): Types of Hybrid Problem-Solving

The paper identifies 3 fundamentally different ways humans and AI can combine. This table shows the processes and outcomes for each type.

Type Description Problem Definition Solution Search Solution Selection
1. AUTONOMOUS Search AI acts independently; human only selects from AI outputs AI forms latent representations (opaque, complex) AI explores latent space autonomously AI's anticipatory quantification
2. SEQUENTIAL Search AI first → Human refines; OR Human first → AI refines Creates refined representations (explainable) Human conducts privileged search using AI predictions Human heuristic selection
3. INTERACTIVE Search Human and AI iterate, learn from each other Creates shifting representations that evolve Interactive experimentation between agents Interactive selection combining AI and human
2. 🎯 The 8 New Mechanisms (EXAM CRITICAL!)

Key Contribution of the Paper

The paper identifies 8 NEW AI-enabled search mechanisms that cannot be derived from human-centric behavioral search models. These are numbered in the paper's Table 1.

Problem Definition Mechanisms (HOW problems are represented):

# Mechanism Description Search Type
1 Latent Representations AI forms highly complex, opaque representations Autonomous
2 Refined Representations Human + AI create moderately complex, explainable representations Sequential
3 Shifting Representations Representations evolve through human-AI iteration Interactive

Solution Search Mechanisms (HOW solutions are found):

# Mechanism Description Search Type
4 Latent Space Exploration AI explores solution space independently (generative AI) Autonomous
5 Privileged Search Human conducts search benefiting from AI's superior problem definition Sequential
6 Interactive Experimentation Human and AI jointly experiment, combining complementary capabilities Interactive

Solution Selection Mechanisms (HOW solutions are chosen):

# Mechanism Description Search Type
7 Anticipatory Quantification AI substitutes for human's heuristic selection, reducing local bias Autonomous
8 Interactive Selection AI complements human selection, combining benefits of both Interactive
3. 🎯 The 5 Main Propositions (EXAM CRITICAL!)
Prop Type Effect on Search SCOPE Effect on Search DEPTH
P1 Autonomous POSITIVE (+) - Broader exploration NEGATIVE (-) - Less focused refinement
P2 Sequential NEGATIVE (-) - More narrow due to refined representation POSITIVE (+) - Deeper local search
P3 Interactive POSITIVE (+) - Gradual broadening over time No effect relative to collective human

Moderating Propositions:

Prop Moderator Effect
P4a-c Time Scarcity REINFORCES autonomous (P1), CONSTRAINS sequential (P2) and interactive (P3)
P5a-c Lack of Expertise REINFORCES autonomous (P1), CONSTRAINS sequential (P2) and interactive (P3)

💡 Key Insight: Trade-offs

Autonomous search: Wider but shallower - good when time is scarce or expertise is low

Sequential search: Narrower but deeper - good for refinement but requires expertise

Interactive search: Gradually widens - best for learning but requires time

4. Summary Through All Four Frameworks

Whetten's Framework Analysis

WHAT Human agents, AI agents, representations (latent/refined/shifting), search scope/depth, time scarcity, expertise
HOW 3 search types × 8 mechanisms → affect scope and depth (moderated by time and expertise)
WHY Complementarity: Humans bring intuition, context; AI brings pattern recognition, speed. Different combinations yield different trade-offs.
WHEN/WHERE Varies by time scarcity, expertise level, task complexity

Gregor's Framework Analysis

Theory Type: Type IV (Explaining and Predicting)

  • Explains WHY different search types lead to different outcomes
  • Predicts WHAT will happen to scope/depth under each type
  • 5 testable propositions with moderators

Markus & Robey's Framework Analysis

Causal Agency Emergent - outcomes emerge from human-AI interaction patterns
Logical Structure Primarily variance (propositions predict relationships) with process elements (how search unfolds)
Level of Analysis Organizational level (problem-solving units)

Burton-Jones Framework Analysis

Shift Applied: Theory as Lens - provides a way to SEE human-AI combinations differently

Contribution Type: Primarily CONCEPTUAL (new mechanisms) + RELATIONAL (new propositions)

Motivation/Creation/Mobilization: Strong on all three - motivates need for new theory, creates 8 new mechanisms, mobilizes research on hybrid search

WHAT Human intelligence, AI capabilities, task complexity, decision quality, hybrid configurations HOW Human + AI → Configuration → Outcomes (moderated by task characteristics) WHY Complementarity: Humans bring intuition, context, ethics; AI brings speed, consistency, pattern recognition WHEN/WHERE Varies by task complexity, uncertainty, stakes, organizational context

Gregor's Framework Analysis

Theory Type: Primarily Type IV (Explaining and Predicting)

  • Explains WHY certain configurations outperform others (complementary capabilities)
  • Predicts WHAT configurations will succeed in which contexts
  • Also has Type V elements (prescriptive implications for design)

🎯 Practical Integration: How to Use All Four Frameworks

1. When Reading/Reviewing Papers

Ask these questions in sequence:

Framework Questions to Ask
Whetten What are the constructs? How are they related? WHY (contribution)? What boundaries?
Gregor What type of theory? Does it explain? Predict? Prescribe?
Markus & Robey What's the causal agent? Variance or process? What level?
Burton-Jones What metaphor fits? What's the contribution type? Good fit for the question?
2. When Developing Your Own Theory
  1. Whetten: Be explicit about your WHATs, HOWs, WHYs, and boundaries
  2. Gregor: Know what type of theory you're building (usually Type IV)
  3. Markus & Robey: Be explicit about causal assumptions, logical structure, and level
  4. Burton-Jones: Consider fit between your question and approach
3. For Your Theory Presentation

Analyze your chosen theory (e.g., Trust, Agency Theory, Affordances) through ALL FOUR lenses:

Presentation Checklist

  • ☐ Whetten: What, How, Why, When/Where of your theory
  • ☐ Gregor: What type of theory is it?
  • ☐ Markus & Robey: Causal agency, logical structure, level of analysis
  • ☐ Burton-Jones: Metaphor, latitude, contribution type

📝 خلاصه فارسی - Persian Summary

🎯 این هفته چی یاد می‌گیریم؟

پنج پیپر درباره چیستی تئوری، چگونگی ساختن تئوری، و تئوری‌سازی نسل بعد

1. Whetten (1989) - چهار عنصر تئوری 🏗️

  • WHAT: چه Construct هایی دارید؟ (مثلاً Trust, Delegation, AI Autonomy)
  • HOW: چه رابطه‌ای بین آنها؟ (فلش‌ها و مدل مفهومی)
  • WHY: چرا این رابطه وجود دارد؟ این مهم‌ترین عنصر است! بدون WHY خوب، Contribution ندارید
  • WHEN/WHERE: محدودیت‌ها، شرایط مرزی، Generalizability

۷ سوال کلیدی برای ارزیابی پیپر:

  1. آیا چیز جدیدی اضافه شده؟ (WHAT جدید)
  2. آیا چیز اضافی حذف شده؟ (Parsimony)
  3. آیا منطق قانع‌کننده است؟ (WHY قوی)
  4. آیا محدودیت‌ها مشخص است؟ (WHEN/WHERE)
  5. آیا زمان مناسب است؟
  6. چرا این موضوع مهم است؟
  7. آیا استدلال جالب است؟

نکته مهم: Gap-spotting کافی نیست! باید Assumption-challenging داشته باشید.

2. Gregor (2006) - پنج نوع تئوری 📊

  1. Type I (Analyzing): دسته‌بندی و طبقه‌بندی - بدون توضیح یا پیش‌بینی (مثل Taxonomy)
  2. Type II (Explaining): توضیح چرایی بدون پیش‌بینی - "چرا این اتفاق افتاد؟"
  3. Type III (Predicting): پیش‌بینی بدون توضیح - مثل Machine Learning (What will happen)
  4. Type IV (EP): هم توضیح هم پیش‌بینی استاندارد طلایی! Gold Standard - بیشتر پیپرهای IS این نوع هستند
  5. Type V (Design): تجویزی - چگونگی ساختن artifact

۷ جزء تئوری: Constructs, Relationships, Scope, Causal Explanations, Testable Propositions, Prescriptive Statements, Representation

تفاوت Variance vs Process:

  • Variance Theory: X باعث Y می‌شود (همبستگی، رگرسیون)
  • Process Theory: توالی رویدادها در زمان (A → B → C)

3. Markus & Robey (1988) - سه بُعد ساختار علّی ⚙️

بُعد گزینه‌ها
Causal Agency تکنولوژی (Technological Imperative) | سازمان (Organizational Imperative) | تعامل (Emergent)
Logical Structure Variance (X→Y) | Process (A→B→C)
Level of Analysis Micro (فرد) | Meso (گروه/سازمان) | Macro (جامعه)

نکته: وقتی تحقیق می‌کنید، باید بدانید کجای این ماتریس قرار دارید!

4. Burton-Jones et al. (2021) - تئوری‌سازی نسل بعد 🚀

چهار تغییر پارادایمی:

از به

Truth (تئوری به عنوان حقیقت) Lens (تئوری به عنوان عینک برای دیدن)

متافورهای تئوری:

  • Lens (عینک): برای دیدن به شکل خاص
  • Map (نقشه): برای راهنمایی
  • Story (داستان): برای فهم روایی

سه معیار Contribution:

  • Motivate: چرا این تئوری لازم است؟
  • Create: چه چیز جدیدی ساخته شده؟
  • Mobilize: چگونه تحقیقات بعدی را فعال می‌کند؟

🚨 چهار استراتژی تئوری‌سازی نسل بعد (جدول مهم برای امتحان!):

پدیده موجود پدیده جدید

5. Raisch & Fomina (2024) - تئوری همکاری انسان-هوش مصنوعی 🤖

این پیپر Application Paper است که همه با چارچوب Burton-Jones تحلیل می‌کنند.

سه نوع جستجوی ترکیبی (Hybrid Search):

  1. Autonomous Search: انسان و AI جداگانه کار می‌کنند → عمق بیشتر، گستره کمتر
  2. Sequential Search: AI اول، انسان بعد (یا برعکس) → تعادل متوسط
  3. Interactive Search: همکاری مداوم → گستره بیشتر، عمق کمتر

۸ مکانیزم جدید:

مرحله مکانیزم‌ها
Problem Definition Cognitive Anchoring, Problem Reframing
Solution Search Information Filtering, Creative Stimulation, Temporal Pacing
Solution Selection Evaluation Criteria, Confidence Calibration, Final Authority

۵ گزاره اصلی:

  • P1: جستجوی ترکیبی بهتر از جستجوی فقط انسان یا فقط AI است
  • P2: تعاملی بودن → گستره بیشتر (Breadth)
  • P3: تعاملی بودن → عمق کمتر (Depth)
  • P4: کمبود زمان → مزیت بیشتر برای Interactive
  • P5: کمبود تخصص → مزیت بیشتر برای Interactive
