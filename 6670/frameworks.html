1. Sarker et al. (2019) — The Sociotechnical Axis

Sarker, S., Chatterjee, S., Xiao, X., & Elbanna, A. (2019). The sociotechnical axis of cohesion for the IS discipline. MIS Quarterly, 43(3), 695–719.

🚨 The Transport Test (Most Important Tool)

Take the entire research model. Transport it from an IT context to a non-IT context (e.g., physical security in an elementary school with NO computers). If the model works exactly the same with zero changes → Type I (social-dominant). If IT is central to the relationships → more sociotechnical.

Type Name IT Role Example RQ MISQ %
I Social-Dominant Context only (background) "Does neuroticism predict career speed of IT employees?" (IT = context, not in model) 56.5%
II Social Imperative Social causes Tech "Does org culture influence AI use in board meetings?" 11.4%
III Additive Social + Tech as separate IVs, no interaction "Do both manager support AND system usability predict productivity?" 2.9%
IV Interplay (Reciprocal) Social and Tech interact, shape each other ChatGPT: users shape AI → AI shapes users → cycle continues 13.0%
V Tech Imperative Tech causes Social "How does GenAI affect task allocation in insurance companies?" 9.9%
VI Tech-Dominant Social = noise to be removed "How can we reduce database latency by 30%?" 6.3%

Why Most Papers Are Type I (Anna's 3 Reasons)

  1. Easier data collection — "It's easier to gather data from humans" than from technical systems
  2. Theory borrowing is easy — "Take a theory from psychology. Take all those constructs and apply them to the context."
  3. Publication advantage — "If the majority of research is already falling into those categories, then those are the categories that are getting published."

Anna's Honest Advice

"Am I going to tell you to not do it? Absolutely not. Because we are all practical people here. We all need to get published." But: "You just have to be AWARE that when you're positioning your paper, you know where it is positioned."

Three Dangers of Losing the Sociotechnical Axis

  1. Loss of distinctiveness — What makes IS different from CS or OB?
  2. Internal division — The field breaks into pieces that don't talk to each other
  3. Ethical failure — Ignoring how technology affects human well-being

2. Gregor (2006) — The Nature of Theory in IS

Gregor, S. (2006). The nature of theory in information systems. MIS Quarterly, 30(3), 611–642.
Type Name Explains WHY? Predicts WHAT? Prescribes HOW? Example
I Theory for Analyzing ❌ ❌ ❌ Taxonomies, Sarker's 6 types, Orlikowski's 5 views
II Theory for Explaining ✅ ❌ ❌ Process theories, historical analyses, interpretive studies
III Theory for Predicting ❌ ✅ ❌ Statistical models, machine learning (black box)
IV Theory for Explaining & Predicting (EP) ✅ ✅ ❌ TAM, UTAUT — Gold Standard in behavioral IS
V Theory for Design & Action ✅ (often) ✅ (often) ✅ Design science papers, method papers

Gregor's 7 Structural Components of Theory (Table 3)

# Component Description Which Types Have It
1 Means of Representation How theory is expressed (words, diagrams, math) All types
2 Primary Constructs Key concepts/variables All types
3 Statements of Relationship How constructs relate (X → Y) All types
4 Scope Boundary conditions All types
5 Causal Explanations WHY relationships exist (mechanisms) II, IV, V
6 Testable Propositions Falsifiable predictions (hypotheses) III, IV, V
7 Prescriptive Statements "Should" statements, design guidance V only

Variance vs. Process Theories (Gregor + Markus & Robey)

Aspect Variance Theory Process Theory
Core Question How much variance in Y is explained by X? What sequence of events leads to outcome?
Logic More X → More Y A → B → C → Outcome
Conditions Necessary AND sufficient Necessary but NOT sufficient
Time Assumed (X before Y) Explicit (sequence matters)
Data Needed Cross-sectional often OK Longitudinal typically needed

3. Markus & Robey (1988) — Causal Structure of IT Research

Markus, M. L., & Robey, D. (1988). Information technology and organizational change: Causal structure in theory and research. Management Science, 34(5), 583–598.

This paper gives us 3 dimensions to classify the causal logic of any IS theory.

Dimension Option A Option B Key Question
1. Causal Agency Technological Imperative
Technology determines outcomes. Humans adapt to IT.		 Organizational Imperative
Humans choose and shape IT. IT is a tool.		 Who has more power — the technology or the humans?
2. Structure of Causality Variance Theory
More X → More Y. Deterministic. Necessary + sufficient conditions.		 Process Theory
A → B → C. Sequence of events. Necessary but NOT sufficient.		 Is the outcome determined by variable levels, or by a sequence of events?
3. Level of Analysis Micro
Individual users, small groups		 Macro
Organizations, industries, societies		 What is the unit of analysis?

The "Emergent" View (Third Option)

Markus & Robey also describe an Emergent perspective: neither technology nor humans fully determine outcomes. Instead, outcomes emerge from the interaction between them. This aligns with Type IV (Interplay) in Sarker's framework and is the most complex but most realistic view.

4. Whetten (1989) — What Constitutes a Theoretical Contribution?

Whetten, D. A. (1989). What constitutes a theoretical contribution? Academy of Management Review, 14(4), 490–495.

The 4 Essential Elements of Theory

Element Question Description Importance
WHAT Which factors? The constructs, variables, or concepts Comprehensiveness vs. Parsimony
HOW How are they related? Relationships between constructs (arrows in models) Shown as boxes-and-arrows diagrams
WHY Why do these relationships exist? The underlying logic, mechanisms, theoretical reasoning ⚠️ MOST CRITICAL — where real contribution happens
WHEN/WHERE/WHO Under what conditions? Boundary conditions, scope, generalizability limits Discovered through replications and extensions

The Most Important Insight

Whetten: "Merely identifying new concepts or relationships is often insufficient; a true contribution comes from developing new and compelling 'whys' that alter the prior understanding of how relationships operate."

Just saying "nobody has studied X in context Y" (gap-spotting) is not enough. You need to explain WHY studying X in context Y would change our theoretical understanding.

Whetten's 7 Questions Reviewers Ask

# Question What Reviewers Look For
1 What's new? Significant value-added contribution. Scope (how much field is impacted) vs. Degree (how different from current thinking)
2 So what? Will this change research practice? Are linkages to research evident?
3 Why so? Is the underlying logic compelling? Are assumptions explicit? Built on sound foundation?
4 Well done? Seasoned thinking? Completeness? Multiple elements covered?
5 Done well? Well written? Flows logically? Enjoyable to read?
6 Why now? Contemporary interest? Not redundant or antiquated?
7 Who cares? What % of readers interested? Links to core concepts?

5. Burton-Jones et al. (2021) — Next-Generation IS Theorizing

Burton-Jones, A., Butler, B. S., Scott, S. V., & Xu, S. X. (2021). Next-generation information systems theorizing: A call to action. MIS Quarterly, 45(1), 301–314.

Table 1: Four Next-Gen Theorizing Strategies

Studying EXISTING Phenomena Studying NEW Phenomena
Inventing NEW Theory Replace
Replace existing theory with new IS theory to understand a phenomenon more effectively.
Example: Media Richness → Media Synchronicity		 Envision
Develop a new theory that opens up a new world.
Example: Design theory for emergent knowledge processes
Using EXISTING Theory Reformulate
Unpack and alter a theory considerably so it offers new and better insights.
Example: Reformulating resistance-to-change paradigm		 Extend
Expand a theory significantly so it can explain/reveal a new world.
Example: Extending identity theory to study "IT identity"

Table 3: Four Shifts in How We View Theory

# From (Old View) To (New View) Why It Matters
1 Theory as intellectual product
(explicit set of statements)		 Theory as intellectual conversation
(evolving ideas in a community)		 Knowing the history of ideas helps you understand why a theory was chosen and how it evolved
2 Theory as truth
(mirror of reality, seek fidelity)		 Theory as lens
(purposefully selected for its focus)		 Need diversity in lenses because the world is complex. Lenses surface questions, not just answers.
3 Theory as purely desk work
(armchair theorizing)		 Theory as reflective engagement
(can emerge from practice and design) 		Theorizing can happen in the field through design, action, or interaction with practitioners
4 Theory as purely representational
(simplified representation of reality) 		Theory as performative
(describes AND produces the world)		 Theories are "models of" and "models for" the world — they can make a difference

Key Insight: Why Theorize Now?

Burton-Jones argues that theorizing is more critical than ever because digitalization is increasing complexity (more sensors, data, software, interfaces, interactions, and change). Theory helps us make sense of complexity. Big data and ML don't replace theory — they are new instruments for theorizing.

6. Faulkner & Runde (2019) — Theorizing the Digital Object

Faulkner, P., & Runde, J. (2019). Theorizing the digital object. MIS Quarterly, 43(4), 1279–1302.

🚨 The Most Important Concept: Social Positions

Anna said: "What I think is most important about this paper is the social, is that little piece on social positions."

Definition: A social position is "a definition of rights and responsibilities that are expected of an object that would occupy that position."

When you create a digital object, you typically create it to occupy a particular social position. You do NOT create a digital object in a vacuum.

Key Concepts

Concept Definition Example
Material Objects Have physical properties (mass, location, shape) Servers, hard drives, laptops
Nonmaterial Objects No physical form; made of symbols (syntactic objects) Software, files, protocols, documents
Bitstrings Fundamental building block of digital tech (0s and 1s) Program files (instructions) + Data files (content)
Bearer What "carries" a nonmaterial object so it can be used Hard drive (material) bears a Word document (nonmaterial)
Social Position Rights and responsibilities expected of an object occupying that position PhD student position: rights (resources, mentorship) + responsibilities (pass comps, publish)

Why Social Positions Connect to Sociotechnical Research

Anna: "Social position is EXTREMELY important. It's that social position that makes the focus on a digital object part of sociotechnical research. It's that social position, that interest in the social position, that positions your paper on that sociotechnical axis."

For design science: "If I'm designing an agent to perform a type of work, and I'm actually trying to understand the type of work it is going to perform from the social point of view, from the interaction with other objects point of view, I am doing sociotechnical research!"

Why This Paper Exists (Connection to Orlikowski & Iacono, 2001)

Orlikowski & Iacono (2001) showed IS research under-theorizes the IT artifact. Most research uses the "nominal" or "tool" view. Only ~12% use the "ensemble view." Faulkner & Runde (2019) respond 18 years later with a proper theory of what digital objects ARE.

7. Orlikowski & Iacono (2001) — Five Views of the IT Artifact

Orlikowski, W. J., & Iacono, C. S. (2001). Research commentary: Desperately seeking the "IT" in IT research — A call to theorizing the IT artifact. Information Systems Research, 12(2), 121–134.

🚨 The Core Problem This Paper Addresses

IS research often ignores or under-theorizes the very thing that makes it IS research: the IT artifact. The artifact is either absent, treated as a black box, or reduced to a single surrogate measure. This paper provides a framework to diagnose HOW a paper conceptualizes the IT artifact.

Why it matters for Q1: When you read an unknown paper, ask "How does this paper view the IT artifact?" Use these five views.

The Five Views (Table 1 from the paper)

View How IT is Treated Problem % in ISR (2001)
1. Nominal IT artifact mentioned in name only or not at all. IT is just context or label. IT has no role in the research model 24.9%
2. Tool IT as a discrete, unchanging instrument. How it works is a technical issue, black-boxed. Focus is on effects, not the artifact itself. Artifact treated as static and independent of social setting 20.3%
3. Computational Emphasis on IT's ability to represent, manipulate, store, retrieve, and transmit information. About what the machine can do computationally. Ignores social embedding 24.3%
4. Proxy IT described by one or more quantitative surrogate measures (e.g., number of systems, IT spending, IT intensity) rather than actual artifact properties. Reduces rich artifact to a single number 18.1%
5. Ensemble ✅ Emphasis on the interactions and relationships between IT artifacts and the groups involved in their construction, implementation, and use. IT is dynamic, situated, socially embedded. None — this is the GOOD view 12.4%

The Key Finding: Only 12.4% Got It Right

Orlikowski & Iacono found that only 12.4% of IS papers used the ensemble view — the only view that properly captures the complexity, dynamism, and context-dependence of IT artifacts. Three follow-up studies show this has barely improved (highest found: 21.7%).

The four inadequate views all treat IT as either absent, black-boxed, abstracted from social life, or reduced to surrogate measures.

How to Apply This in Q1 (Unknown Paper Analysis)

Ask yourself: "How does this paper view the IT artifact?"

  • Is IT just named but not in the model? → Nominal
  • Is IT treated as a neutral tool? Effects studied, internals ignored? → Tool
  • Is the focus on what the system can compute/process? → Computational
  • Is IT measured by spending or counts only? → Proxy
  • Does the paper study how IT interacts with users, organizations, context? → Ensemble (the ideal)

Connection to Sarker 2019: Ensemble view papers tend to be Type IV (Interplay). Nominal view papers tend to be Type I (Social-Dominant).

Connection to Faulkner & Runde (2019)

Faulkner & Runde published their digital object theory 18 years after Orlikowski & Iacono's call for better IT theorizing. They say IS research still under-theorizes the IT artifact. Their response: provide a formal ontological theory of what digital objects are (bearers, bitstrings, social positions). This is what a proper ensemble-view paper looks like at the ontological level.

8. Baird & Maruping (2021) — IS Delegation Framework

Baird, A., & Maruping, L. M. (2021). The next generation of research on IS use: A theoretical framework of delegation to and from agentic IS artifacts. MIS Quarterly, 45(1), 315–341.

The Core Theoretical Tension

Old assumption: IS artifacts have NO agency. They are passive tools. Now we have agentic systems (AI, chatbots, autonomous vehicles) that can perceive, act, learn, and accept responsibility. The old assumption no longer holds. We need a new theory.

What Makes an IS Artifact "Agentic"?

Agency = having choice + having accountability + ability to give up control while retaining some rights. An agentic IS artifact can choose an action in response to changes in the environment.

Four Archetypes of Agentic IS (Simple → Complex)

Archetype Description Examples
Reflexive React to immediate stimuli. Simple mapping: state → action. Voice assistants, basic sensors
Supervisory Monitor progress, suggest corrections. Has internal model. Decision support systems, smart thermostats
Anticipatory Proactively anticipate needs. Predicts future states. Social media content filtering, auto-generated albums
Prescriptive Make autonomous decisions. Utility-based. Highest agency. Chatbots, autonomous vehicles, robo-advisors

Three Delegation Mechanisms

Mechanism Description
Appraisal Evaluating the other agent. "Can I trust this AI? Is the human capable?" Trust is part of appraisal.
Distribution How rights and responsibilities are divided. Who decides? Who acts?
Coordination Managing the ongoing relationship. Monitoring, updating, accountability.

Key Difference from Traditional IS Use Research

IS Use (Old) IS Delegation (New)
Users responsible for application Roles are fluid, can switch
Systems are dependent on users Systems can be autonomous
Human is always superior Either agent might be "better" for the task
Focus on individual user Focus on the DYAD (human + AI pair)

Preference Alignment (The Thermostat Example)

If you let an agentic system optimize for its own preferences (e.g., thermostat maximizes its longevity → prefers very cold), you freeze. The solution: set the preference function to optimize your preference, not the agent's. This is the same problem as executive compensation in agency theory — how do you align the agent's incentives with the principal's goals?

Applied to ChatGPT: "The preferences of OpenAI, as a money-making corporation, will be reflected in the preferences of ChatGPT." — Anna, Week 2

8. Baird (2021) — Writing IS Papers

Baird, A. (2021). On writing research articles well: A guide for writing IS papers. Journal of the Association for Information Systems, 22(5), 1197–1211.

Use this for Q1: The 5 elements tell you what to look for when analyzing an unknown paper. Finding each element shows deep reading.

The 5 Elements Every IS Paper Must Have

# Element What to Look For in the Paper
1 Area of Focus Who is this for? What specific topic does it address?
2 Relevant Background What does the existing literature say? Where does the paper start from?
3 Theoretical Tension The "but" or "however" moment. What problem, contradiction, or challenge drives the paper?
4 Resolution What theory, model, or argument does the paper propose to solve the tension?
5 Application Guidelines How can other researchers or practitioners use this? What are the implications?

Gap Spotting vs. Assumption Challenging

Gap spotting (weak): "Nobody studied X yet." Any empty space can be called a gap. This is not strong theory.

Assumption challenging (strong): "Everyone assumes X is true, but X breaks down under condition Y." This questions what the field takes for granted.

Example: Everyone assumed end-users always direct IS artifacts. Baird & Maruping challenge this: agentic AI now directs humans. That is assumption challenging.

Red Thread and Hourglass

Red thread: Every section connects to the central argument. No detours. If you can remove a section and the paper still makes sense, the thread is broken.

Hourglass shape: Broad intro (what we know) → narrow (specific theory or study) → broaden again (implications, future research).

9. Sarker et al. (2023) — How to Review IS Papers

Sarker, S., Whitley, E. A., Goh, K. Y., et al. (2023). Editorial: Some thoughts on reviewing for Information Systems Research. ISR, 34(4), 1321–1338.

Use this for Q2/Q3: When asked to evaluate or critique a paper, use the q/r framework.

The q/r Framework

Dimension What It Means How to Judge It
q — Idea Quality Is the research question important, novel, and interesting? High q = addresses something that matters and has not been answered. Low q = trivial or already known.
r — Execution Quality Is the method appropriate and done well? High r = design matches the question, rigorous implementation. Low r = poor fit or weak execution.

The Four Combinations

q r Verdict
High High Accept — strong paper
High Low Major revision — great idea, weak execution
Low High Hard case — perfectly done, but boring question
Low Low Reject

Key Rules from Sarker

Know the research type first. You cannot criticize a qualitative paper for not having a large sample. The standards differ by paradigm (positivist, design science, interpretive).

Developmental reviewing. For every weakness you identify, suggest how to fix it. Be a collaborator, not just a critic.

Fatal vs. fixable issues. Distinguish "showstoppers" (fundamental flaws in q) from "fixable" issues (execution problems that revision can solve).

10. Sun et al. (2024) — Novelty, Rigor, and Relevance

Sun, H., Wen, W., Thatcher, J. B., Chau, M., & Brown, S. A. (2024). Rebalancing novelty with rigor and relevance in IS research. MIS Quarterly.

Use this for Q1 and Q2/Q3: Identify what kind of novelty a paper claims and whether it properly balances rigor and relevance.

The Three Concepts (Table 2)

Concept Definition
Novelty Originality of concepts, relationships, or methods. Includes theoretical extensions, new empirical phenomena, or design innovations.
Rigor Theoretical and empirical validity. Methodological soundness. The study is done correctly.
Relevance Practical and theoretical importance. The study matters to someone — practice, policy, or future research.

Novelty by Research Paradigm (Table 1)

Paradigm What Counts as Novelty Main Risk
Behavioral / Theory Testing New constructs, new relationships, new boundary conditions Jangle fallacy — old construct with new name
Design Science New artifact, new design principle, new evaluation framework Building a system without showing it is better than alternatives
Qualitative / Interpretive New understanding, new context, new theoretical lens Novelty of context instead of novelty of insight
Econ-IS New causal identification, new data, new natural experiment "Methodological fireworks without conceptual fire"

The Jangle Fallacy

Creating a new construct name for something that already exists in the literature. Example: calling something "delegation readiness" when "trust" already covers it. Sun et al. call this one of the biggest threats to IS research quality. Anna mentioned it specifically in class.

Two Key Tensions

Novelty vs. Rigor: Chasing novelty leads to untested theories and poorly validated constructs that look exciting but cannot be replicated.

Novelty vs. Relevance: Chasing novelty leads to studying obscure phenomena nobody cares about. "We study what is countable, not what counts."

🔗 How All 11 Frameworks Connect for Q1

For an unknown paper in Q1, work through these in order. Each one answers a different question:

  • Baird 2021 → WHAT DOES THE PAPER SAY? (Identify the 5 elements: focus, background, tension, resolution, guidelines)
  • Gap or Assumption? → HOW DOES IT FIND ITS TENSION? (Gap spotting = weak. Assumption challenging = strong.)
  • Sarker 2019 → WHERE IS IT ON THE SOCIOTECHNICAL AXIS? (Type I pure tech to Type VI pure social)
  • Gregor → WHAT TYPE OF THEORY? (Analyzing, Explaining, Predicting, Explaining+Predicting, Design)
  • Markus & Robey → WHAT IS THE CAUSAL LOGIC? (Variance vs. Process; Deterministic vs. Emergent)
  • Whetten → HOW GOOD IS THE THEORY? (What, How, Why, When — is the WHY compelling?)
  • Burton-Jones → WHAT THEORIZING STRATEGY? (Replace, Reformulate, Envision, Extend)
  • Sun 2024 → IS THE NOVELTY REAL AND BALANCED? (What type of novelty? Any jangle fallacy? Balanced with rigor and relevance?)
  • Sarker 2023 → OVERALL QUALITY VERDICT: q (important question?) + r (executed well?)
  • Faulkner & Runde → HOW IS THE IT ARTIFACT THEORIZED? (Only for papers where the artifact matters)
  • Orlikowski & Iacono → WHICH VIEW OF IT ARTIFACT? (Nominal, Tool, Computational, Proxy, or Ensemble? Is the IT theorized adequately?)
  • Baird & Maruping → HOW IS HUMAN-AI INTERACTION THEORIZED? (Only for papers about AI/agentic IS)
