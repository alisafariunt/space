# Paper 1: Sarker et al. (2019)
## The Sociotechnical Axis of Cohesion for the IS Discipline
*MIS Quarterly, 43(3), 695-719*

### What is this paper about?
The authors are worried that IS research has lost its sociotechnical character, the core identity that makes IS a unique field. They reviewed 991 papers from MISQ and ISR between 2000-2016 and found that most research now focuses on either the social side (treating technology as background) or only the technical side without considering social effects.
w
### Key Problem
IS was built on the idea that both social and technical parts matter equally and should be studied together. But this is no longer happening. About 56% of research is mostly social (Type I), only 7% is mostly technical (Type VI), and only 13% actually examines how social and technical interact (Type IV). Also, 91% of studies focus only on instrumental outcomes (efficiency) and ignore humanistic outcomes (ethics, well-being).

### The Framework
The authors use Abbott's concept of "axis of cohesion" to explain why fields need a shared foundation. They argue the sociotechnical perspective has been this axis for IS. When fields lose their axis, they face three dangers:
1. **Loss of distinctiveness** - what makes IS different from computer science or organizational behavior?
2. **Internal division** - the field breaks into pieces that don't talk to each other
3. **Ethical failure** - ignoring how technology affects human well-being

### Three Recommendations
1. **The continuum idea** - Think of IS research on a social-technical line. Not every study needs to be in the middle, but researchers should know where their work sits.
2. **Accept different connections** - Social and technical can relate in many ways, not just "fit" or "balance."
3. **Link outcomes together** - Connect efficiency goals with ethics and well-being goals. They can support each other.

# Paper 2: Faulkner & Runde (2019)
## Theorizing the Digital Object
*MIS Quarterly, 43(4), 1279-1302*

### What is this paper about?
The authors argue that IS research lacks good theories about what digital objects actually ARE. We talk about digital technology all the time, but we don't really understand the basic nature of these objects. They propose a theory that explains both the complexity of digital objects and how their identity depends on social context.

### The Core Problem
Orlikowski and Iacono (2001) showed that IS research under-theorizes the IT artifact. Follow-up studies (up to 2015) show this hasn't changed much. Most IS research either:
- **Ignores** what IT artifacts really are (nominal view)
- **Treats them as simple tools** (tool view)
- **Reduces them to proxy measures** (proxy view)
- Only about 12-21% use the "ensemble view" that properly considers technology complexity

### Key Concepts

**Objects vs. Occurrents**
- **Objects** = things that endure through time (like a smartphone)
- **Occurrents** = things that happen (like a process or event)

**Material vs. Nonmaterial Objects**
- **Material objects** = have physical properties (mass, location, shape) - examples: servers, hard drives
- **Nonmaterial objects** = no physical form - examples: software, files, protocols
- **Hybrid objects** = both together - examples: laptop running an app

**Syntactic Objects**
These are nonmaterial objects made of symbols arranged according to rules. Examples: text, code, contracts.

**Bitstrings**
The fundamental building block of digital technology. Bitstrings are syntactic objects made of 0s and 1s. They come in two types:
- **Program files** = instructions (software, apps)
- **Data files** = content (documents, images, videos)

### The Bearer Theory (Very Important!)
This is the paper's main innovation. Nonmaterial objects need to be "borne by" something to be used.

**Material bearers** = physical things that carry nonmaterial objects
- Example: A hard drive (material) "bears" a Word document (nonmaterial)
- The same document can exist on many different material bearers (your phone, cloud server, USB drive)

**Nonmaterial bearers** = other nonmaterial objects that carry nonmaterial objects
- Example: A software testing protocol (nonmaterial object) can be expressed in a text document (nonmaterial bearer), which is then saved as a DOCX file (another nonmaterial bearer), which sits on a hard drive (material bearer)
- This creates LAYERS of bearers

**Why this matters:**
- Nonmaterial objects don't degrade with use (unlike physical things)
- They can be copied infinitely (non-rivalry)
- The same file can exist in many places at once
- This creates legal problems (can you "sell" a used digital file?)

### Social Positioning
Digital objects get their identity from social positions. An MRI scanner hardware becomes "an MRI scanner" because it occupies a specific position in a hospital system with specific expectations (system functions) and rights/responsibilities. Just like how a person becomes "a surgeon" by occupying that position.

This means:
- Digital object identity is not just about technical properties
- It depends on community context
- It can change as social practices change

# Paper 3: Baird & Maruping (2021)
## The Next Generation of Research on IS Use: A Theoretical Framework of Delegation to and from Agentic IS Artifacts
*MIS Quarterly, 45(1), 315-341*

### What is this paper about?
IS use research has always assumed humans are in control and technology is a passive tool. But now we have "agentic IS artifacts" like AI, chatbots, and autonomous vehicles that can make decisions, learn, and act on their own. This paper introduces DELEGATION as a new lens to understand human-AI relationships.

### Why Current IS Use Theory is Not Enough
Three problems with current IS use thinking:

1. **Human-centric language** - We say "users use systems." But what happens when the AI uses the human? Current theory doesn't account for this flip.

2. **Focus on perceptions** - UTAUT and similar models care about what users think (perceived usefulness, ease of use). They don't care about what the artifact can actually do.

3. **Wrong unit of analysis** - Current research looks at individual users or groups. But with agentic IS, the DYAD (human + AI pair) is what matters.

### What are Agentic IS Artifacts?
Software agents that can:
- **Perceive** their environment
- **Act** autonomously
- **Learn** and adapt
- **Accept rights and responsibilities** for outcomes

**Four Archetypes (from simple to complex):**

| Archetype | Description | Examples |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Reflexive** | React to immediate stimuli | Voice assistants responding to queries, basic sensors |
| **Supervisory** | Monitor progress, suggest corrections | Decision support systems, smart home thermostats |
| **Anticipatory** | Proactively anticipate needs | Social media content filtering, auto-generated albums |
| **Prescriptive** | Make autonomous decisions | Chatbots, autonomous vehicles, robo-advisors |

### The IS Delegation Framework

**Key Idea:** Delegation = transferring rights and responsibilities for task execution from one agent to another.

**Agent Attributes:**
1. **Endowments** - Resources and capabilities each agent has
   - Human: knowledge, experience, abstract thinking
   - Agentic IS: data storage, processing power, speed
2. **Preferences** - Goals and decision models
   - Human: what they want to achieve
   - Agentic IS: what they're designed to optimize
3. **Roles** - Delegator or Proxy (and these can switch!)
   - Traditional: human delegator, AI proxy
   - New reality: AI can delegate TO humans too

**Delegation Mechanisms:**
1. **Appraisal** - Evaluating the other agent (Can I trust this AI? Is the human capable?)
2. **Distribution** - How rights and responsibilities are divided (Who decides? Who acts?)
3. **Coordination** - Managing the ongoing relationship (Monitoring, updating, accountability)

**Task Attributes:**
- **Action requirements** - Cognitive, digital, or physical?
- **Complexity** - How uncertain, dynamic, and interdependent?
- **Decomposability** - Can it be split into subtasks?

**Situation Attributes:**
- **Incentives** - Economic or social motivations
- **Complexity** - How stable, observable, and controllable is the situation?

### Key Differences from IS Use

| IS Use | IS Delegation |
|--------|---------------|
| Users responsible for application | Roles are fluid, can switch |
| Systems are dependent on users | Systems can be autonomous |
| Tasks are well-defined | Tasks can be ambiguous, uncertain |
| Human is always superior | Either agent might be "better" for the task |

### Guidelines for Researchers
1. Start with the task or desired outcome
2. Identify relevant delegation mechanisms
3. Consider agent attributes (asymmetries between human and AI)
4. Consider situational factors
5. Look for feedback loops that change future delegation

### Two Examples from the Paper

**Example 1: Surgical Support AI**
- Task: AI helps surgeon with procedures (augmented reality glasses)
- Mechanism: Appraisal is key (surgeon evaluates if AI is accurate enough)
- Agent attribute: Human preferences matter (trust builds over repeated interactions)
- Situation: Very high stakes, so delegation is gradual

**Example 2: Open Source Code Review**
- Task: Bot assigns code review work to developers
- Mechanism: Distribution is key (how are tasks assigned?)
- Twist: The bot delegates TO humans, not the other way around
- Feedback: Bot learns from past review outcomes

# Paper 4: Gartner (2026)

Gartner's annual report on technology trends that business leaders should pay attention to. Organized into three themes:

*Orchestrating diverse technologies for value*

*Elevating trust, governance, and security*

What: Using AI to anticipate and stop attacks BEFORE they happen, not just respond after.

**8. Digital Provenance**
What: Verifying the origin and integrity of software, data, and media. Uses watermarking and "bills of materials" to track where things come from.

Why trending: Rising risks from code tampering, abandoned open-source projects, and deepfakes.

**9. AI Security Platforms**
What: Consolidated controls to secure both third-party AI services and custom AI applications. Addresses AI-specific risks like prompt injection and rogue agent actions.

**10. Geopatriation**
What: Moving data and processing back to specific geographic locations for regulatory compliance and sovereignty.

# Paper 5: Orlikowski & Iacono (2001)
## Desperately Seeking the 'IT' in IT Research
*Information Systems Research, 12(2), 121-134*

**Note:** This is an older seminal paper, listed as optional reading.

### What is this paper about?
This famous paper criticized the IS field for not paying enough attention to the IT artifact itself. The authors reviewed 188 articles published in Information Systems Research during the 1990s and found that most research treats IT in superficial ways.

### The Five Views of IT Artifacts

| View | What it means | Problem |
|------|---------------|---------|
| **Nominal** | IT mentioned in name only or not at all | IT is absent |
| **Tool** | IT as instrument; black-boxed, unchanging | Ignores what IT actually is |
| **Computational** | Focus on computing power | Too narrow |
| **Proxy** | IT measured by surrogates (IT spending) | Reduces IT to a number |
| **Ensemble** | IT in context with users and organizations | Most complete but rarely used |

### Key Finding
Only about 12% of articles took the "ensemble view" that really engaged with what IT artifacts are. The rest treated IT as "commonplace and received notions" without deep analysis.

### Why This Still Matters
- Faulkner & Runde (2019) directly responds to this paper
- Follow-up studies show the problem hasn't been fixed
- With AI and agentic IS, understanding the artifact is more important than ever

### Connection to Other Week 2 Readings
- Sarker et al. (2019) worries about the same problem: IS research losing its focus on technology
- Faulkner & Runde (2019) tries to solve this by providing a theory of digital objects
- Baird & Maruping (2021) shows why we need to understand agentic artifacts, not just treat them as tools

| Orlikowski & Iacono | IT artifact | We've been ignoring the core of our field | **ENSEMBLE VIEW** is rare |

### Three Papers, One Story

Professor showed how the papers build on each other:

1. **Orlikowski & Iacono (2001):** "You're ignoring the IT artifact!"
2. **Faulkner & Runde (2019):** "Here's a theory of what IT artifacts actually ARE" (response 18 years later)
3. **Sarker et al. (2019):** "You're STILL ignoring it! Most research is Type I (social only)"
4. **Baird & Maruping (2021):** "With agentic AI, understanding the artifact is MORE important than ever"

### Most Important Concepts to Memorize

#### 1. The Transport Test 🚨
> Can you take your entire model from IT context to non-IT context with ZERO changes? Then it's Type I research.

**Use this on EVERY paper you read!**

#### 2. Social Positions 🚨  
> Digital objects are created to occupy specific social positions with rights and responsibilities.

**This connects the technical to the social!**

#### 3. Preference Alignment 🚨
> When agents become utility-based, we need to align their preferences with ours (just like executive compensation).

**ChatGPT has OpenAI's profit motive embedded!**

#### 4. Awareness Over Perfection 🚨
> You don't have to do Type IV research to publish. But you MUST know where your paper sits on the continuum.

**Be strategic, be aware, be honest.**

### For Your Dissertation on Trust & Delegation in Agentic IS

**From Sarker:**
- Position as Type IV (Interplay) research - justify the complexity
- Link humanistic outcomes (fairness, trust) with instrumental (efficiency)
- Be AWARE you're doing sociotechnical research

**From Faulkner & Runde:**
- Focus on the SOCIAL POSITION AI occupies
- What rights/responsibilities do users expect from "AI assistant" position?
- Don't get lost in bearer complexity

**From Baird & Maruping:**
- Unit of analysis: Human-AI DYAD (not just human)
- Trust is part of Appraisal mechanism
- Understand AI's embedded preferences (OpenAI's profit motive)
- Different archetypes need different trust considerations

**From Gartner:**
- Multiagent systems: Trust between MULTIPLE AI agents
- Domain-specific models: May be more trusted
- Physical AI: Mistakes have real consequences

### The Writing Lesson (Apply to ALL Papers)

**Structure consistency:**
1. Problematization: What's the tension?
2. Resolution: How do you solve it?
3. Literature review: Support your resolution
4. Observations: Abstract your findings
5. Recommendations: Mirror your observations

**DON'T:**
- Start with idea A
- Get excited about idea B in lit review
- Focus on part C in theory
- Discuss implications for D

**DO:**
- Maintain thread from beginning to end
- Tell them what you'll tell them
- Tell them
- Tell them what you told them
- Along the SAME dimensions
