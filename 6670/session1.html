Paper 1: On Writing Research Articles Well

Baird, A. (2021). On writing research articles well: A guide for writing IS papers. Journal of the Association for Information Systems, 22(5), 1197-1211.
0. Citation

JAIS Editorial/Tutorial paper by Aaron Baird, focused on practical guidance for IS researchers.

1. Goal

To provide a practical guide for IS researchers who want to write better papers, especially theory papers. The paper teaches the essential elements that every IS paper needs to communicate clearly.

The core objective is to produce an article that is enjoyable to read, changes what the reader knows, and is not cognitively taxing. This is achieved through clear structural principles and a methodical writing process.

Foundational Principles: Two Key Analogies

The Red Thread

A paper must follow a "focused and continuously connected set of ideas from start to finish." The logical arguments should not fray, ensuring the reader is not confused by a disjointed narrative.

What this means: Every paragraph should connect logically to the previous one. If you cut a paragraph and the paper still makes sense, that paragraph probably should not be there.

The Hourglass

A paper should start broad (what the target audience knows), narrow to the specifics (what was done and why), and then broaden again (application, generalizations, and future opportunities).

Visual:

    BROAD: What audience already knows
           ↓
    NARROW: Your specific contribution
           ↓
    BROAD: Applications and implications
2. Audience

IS researchers, especially doctoral students and early-career scholars who are learning how to write publishable research papers.

3. Problem

Many IS papers fail in the review process because they don't clearly communicate certain essential elements. Authors often don't know what these elements are or how to present them effectively.

"A good IS paper... needs to communicate five essential things clearly. These five elements are: the area of focus, the relevant background, the theoretical tension, the resolution of that tension, and guidelines for future application. If any of these pieces is missing or unclear, the paper will struggle in the review process."
4. Research Questions

This is a tutorial paper, so there are no formal research questions. The implicit question is: What makes an IS paper successful in the review process?

5. Resolution

Baird provides a five-element framework that every IS paper must communicate:

Element What It Means Key Questions
Area of Focus Identify your target audience and why they will care Who will find this interesting?
Relevant Background What is already known (current consensus) What does your audience already believe?
Theoretical Tension Why new theory is needed Which assumptions need revisiting?
Resolution Your theory building approach + new theory What is your specific objective?
Guidelines Roadmap for future researchers How should others use your theory?
6. Contribution

Recommendations: Practical guidance and templates for writing. Also introduces the important distinction between gap spotting (weak) and assumption challenging (strong) approaches.

Gap Spotting vs. Assumption Challenging

Gap Spotting: "Nobody has studied X in context Y." This is weak because just because something is unstudied doesn't mean it's important.

Assumption Challenging: "The field assumes users are homogeneous, but actually there is heterogeneity based on Z." This is strong because it directly addresses why prior work may be incomplete.

7. Guidelines
  • Write "middle out": Draft Background and Theory first, then Conclusion and Abstract, save Introduction for last
  • Use planning worksheets: Fill in blanks like "This paper focuses on the _____ area"
  • Challenge assumptions: Look for homogeneity assumed where heterogeneity exists
  • Combine existing ideas: Theoretical innovation comes from unique combinations, not entirely new ideas
8. Paper Structure
  • Introduction: Defines the problem and previews the approach
  • The five elements: Detailed explanation of each element
  • Writing process tips: Practical advice on workflow
  • Appendices: Planning worksheets and outline templates
9. Inspired Research Question

How do researchers in the AI/agentic IS area identify and challenge assumptions about human-AI relationships that no longer hold in the era of increasingly autonomous systems?

"New ideas are not always entirely new from the ground up. Rather, theoretical innovation almost always comes from unique combinations of existing theory, concepts, constructs, contemporary IS artifact designs, and observations." — Baird (2021)
Planning Worksheet Template (Appendix A)

Use this worksheet to plan your paper before writing:

Item Description Fill in the Blank
Area of Focus What is the context and why is it interesting? "This paper focuses on the _____ area. This area is interesting because _____."
Background What is already known and assumed? "From prior literature, we know that _____. In general, this literature assumes _____."
Tension Why must this paper be read? "However, _____." OR "It is surprising that _____."
Resolution What is your objective and approach? "Given this background, the primary objective of this work is _____. The approach taken is _____."
Contribution What are you delivering? "The expected contribution of this paper is _____."
Guidelines How should others apply your work? "Future researchers should consider _____. This work is limited by _____."
"Middle-Out" Writing Order

Baird recommends writing in this order (NOT the order sections appear in the paper):

Order Section Why This Order?
1. First Background and Theory sections The substance of your contribution
2. Second Guidelines and Conclusion Now you know what you're contributing
3. Third Abstract Summarize what you wrote
4. Last Introduction Hardest and most-read section; easier once you know what paper says

Why Introduction Last?

The Introduction is the hardest section to write and also the most read. It's easier to write once you know what the rest of your paper actually says. Don't waste hours perfecting an intro for a paper whose direction may still change.

The 5-Step Structured Writing Process (Detailed)

A disciplined, step-by-step process can demystify the writing of both theoretical and empirical papers:

Step 1: Plan and Outline

Begin by using a planning worksheet to draft sentences for each of the five core communication areas. Expand these notes into a detailed manuscript outline, paying attention to the target journal's formatting requirements from the outset.

Step 2: Draft from the Middle Out

For the first draft, it is most effective to write the core sections first: Background, Theory Building/Methods, and New Theory Development/Results. The Introduction, typically the most difficult section, should be written last, along with the Conclusion and Abstract.

Step 3: Utilize Synthesis Tables

In the literature review section, create a synthesis table to organize prior research thematically. This is a valuable exercise for clarifying the paper's perspective on existing literature, even if the table is removed from the final version.

Literature Review Best Practice

A literature review should be an "evidence-based editorial", not just a list of facts.

  • Use synthesis tables with columns: research area, description, citations
  • Use "string citations" to save space (e.g., Author1, 2018; Author2, 2019)
  • Demonstrate common ground with your target subcommunity
  • Include both classic and recent works

Step 4: Create Results Tables and Figures First

Before writing the full paper, create the tables and figures for the Results section. This ensures the primary findings are finalized, preventing extensive rewriting later.

Results Section Rules

  • Stick to facts - reserve interpretation for the Discussion
  • "Rookie mistake" = introducing new constructs or relationships for the first time in the Results section
  • Create tables and figures BEFORE writing the prose around them
  • Emulate model papers that use similar methods

Step 5: Iterate and Revise Consistently

Academic writing is an iterative process. An "agile" approach, involving frequent sharing and collective revision with co-authors, is preferable to a "waterfall" method where a "finished" paper is presented.

"Revise, revise, revise... Be obsessed with incrementally improving." — Baird (2021)

After the full draft is complete, the paper must be reread and "tightened" at least three times to remove extraneous text and clarify the "red thread."

Empirical Paper Structure (Appendix C)
Section Content
Abstract Area of focus, Background, Tension, Resolution (objective, theory, design), Contribution
Introduction One paragraph for each element. Optional: structure paragraph at end
Background Table of research areas → Describe each → Summarize what is known and what is assumed
Research Design and Methods Study design, data sources, analysis methods
Results Stick to facts. Emulate model papers using similar methods
Discussion Remind reader of focus/tension. Contributions to research (one paragraph per finding). Contributions to practice. Limitations and future research
Conclusion Synthesize what paper accomplished. Emphasize what is novel and why important
Example: Agentic IS Artifacts (Baird & Maruping, 2021)

Baird uses his own paper as an example of assumption challenging:

"The traditional assumption was that end-users are always the primary agents when interacting with IS artifacts. But with AI and automation, this assumption no longer holds. Now, agentic IS artifacts (like routing systems telling delivery drivers where to go) can direct human agents."

If we do not challenge this assumption, we risk not understanding how human-technology relationships are evolving. This example shows how good theory comes from questioning what everyone takes for granted.

The Theorizing Process is Iterative
"Our theorizing process was in a word, iterative. We individually iterated through cycles of reading articles, developing ideas, rereading, and rethinking. We jointly iterated through outlines, tables, figures, drafts, presentations, and feedback." — Baird (2021)

Practical reading strategy: Print papers, read on the train, reread the next morning, then write a brief synthesis. Over weeks and months, this builds a stack of relevant literature and valuable notes.

Paper 2: Some Thoughts on Reviewing for ISR

Sarker, S., Whitley, E. A., Goh, K. Y., et al. (2023). Some thoughts on reviewing for Information Systems Research and other leading information systems journals. Information Systems Research, Articles in Advance.
0. Citation

Editorial from the ISR editorial team, one of only two IS journals on the FT50 list.

1. Goal

To explain how the peer review process works and give advice to researchers who want to become good reviewers.

2. Audience

Current and aspiring reviewers for top IS journals, as well as authors who want to understand the review process from the inside.

3. Problem

Peer review can be mystifying to authors, and reviewers often don't have clear guidance on how to evaluate papers fairly and constructively.

"Understanding this paper is important because it shows you the 'behind the scenes' of how your papers will be evaluated when you submit them to top journals."
4. Research Questions

Not applicable (editorial paper). Implicit questions: What makes a good review? How should reviewers evaluate papers from different paradigms?

5. Resolution

The authors introduce the q-r framework for thinking about paper quality:

Dimension Meaning Key Questions
q (Quality of Idea) How important is the research question? How valuable is the contribution? Is this question worth asking?
r (Quality of Execution) Are methods appropriate and rigorous? Is presentation clear? Are the methods sound?

Combinations of q and r

Scenario Meaning Outcome
High q, High r Great idea + excellent execution Accept (ideal paper)
High q, Low r Great idea + poor execution Help improve, may accept after revisions
Low q, High r Trivial idea + excellent methods Reject (methods can't save weak question)
Low q, Low r Trivial idea + poor execution Reject
6. Contribution

Recommendations: Guidelines for reviewing and metaphors for reviewer roles (Diamond Cutter, Coach, Champion, Developmental Gatekeeper).

7. Guidelines
  • Be developmental: Help authors improve, not just reject
  • Be paradigm-aware: Don't apply criteria from one paradigm to another
  • Be civil: IS is a small community; treat authors with respect
  • Know the process: EIC → SE → AE → Reviewers (double-blind)
8. Paper Structure
  • Introduction: Purpose of peer review
  • The q-r framework: Two dimensions of paper quality
  • Review process: How papers move through the system
  • Paradigm awareness: Different standards for different research types
  • Benefits of reviewing: Why you should volunteer
9. Inspired Research Question

How can the review process for AI-related IS papers be adapted to fairly evaluate contributions that may challenge established paradigm boundaries?

Reviewer Role Metaphors

  • Diamond Cutter: Polish manuscripts so the gem can "surface and shine"
  • Coach: Manage and mentor authors to produce winning research
  • Champion: Advocate for work, especially from marginalized traditions
  • Developmental Gatekeeper: Balance quality control with helping authors improve
  • Steward: Create value for society, scholars, practitioners, and the academic community
Type I and Type II Errors in Review
Error Type Meaning How to Minimize
Type I Error Accepting papers that should have been rejected Rigorous assessment of both q and r dimensions
Type II Error Rejecting papers that should have been accepted Developmental feedback, paradigm awareness, generosity
ISR Review Process Flow
Step Who What Happens
1. Submission Authors Submit manuscript to system
2. Initial Screening EIC + ME Check basic requirements, scope, quality
3. SE Assignment EIC Assign to Senior Editor with relevant expertise
4. AE Assignment SE Senior Editor assigns to Associate Editor
5. Reviewer Selection AE Invites 2-3 reviewers with topic expertise
6. Review Reviewers Double-blind review. Reviewers assess q and r
7. Decision AE → SE AE recommends, SE makes final decision
8. Revision Authors Revise based on feedback, resubmit

Fatal Flaws vs. Fixable Issues

Reviewers must distinguish between issues that are "fatal showstoppers" and those that are "not necessarily fatal."

  • Fatal flaws: Fundamental problems with the research question, invalid methods, or lack of contribution. Cannot be fixed in revision.
  • Fixable issues: Presentation problems, missing literature, unclear explanations. Can be addressed with developmental feedback.

Expert "diamond cutters" can help camouflage some errors, but most fatal flaws are impossible to conceal.

Goes (2014) describes reviewing as achieving two objectives:

  • Love: Appreciation from authors who benefit from constructive feedback, and from editors who rely on your evaluations
  • Glory: Building academic standing, Best Reviewer awards, editorial board invitations, support letters for promotion
Reviewer Ethics (CRITICAL for Exam)

High-quality reviewing is underpinned by a commitment to ethical conduct:

Ethical Principle What It Means What to Do
Manage Biases Be aware of your own theoretical or paradigmatic biases Strive to transcend your own preferences when evaluating work
Disclose Conflicts Any relationship with authors must be disclosed Tell the editor about any competitive, collaborative, or other relationship
Maintain Confidentiality Manuscript content is confidential Do not share or discuss the paper with others
NO AI/LLMs Using generative AI in review is inappropriate Confidentiality risks + AI cannot grasp specialist norms
Report Misconduct Plagiarism, fabrication, ethical lapses Report confidentially to EDITORS, NOT in the review itself
Professional Approach to Reviewing
Principle What It Means Practical Tip
"Us-with-them" mentality NOT "us-versus-them" You are collaborating with authors, not competing against them
Constructive and Kind Critical comments offered in collegial spirit Reread your review 24 hours later to check tone
Focus on Paper Not on authors' training or character Avoid judging the authors personally
No Sweeping Statements Dismissive statements are unprofessional Be specific about what is wrong and how to fix it
Paradigm-Aware Evaluate using standards of paper's own tradition Don't apply positivist criteria to interpretive research
How to Structure a Review (4 Parts)

An effective review provides clear, specific, and actionable feedback:

Part Content Purpose
1. Initial Overview Brief summary of the paper from your perspective Shows you understood the paper's core argument
2. Broad Reactions High-level impressions of strengths and weaknesses Gives authors big-picture feedback first
3. Detailed Critiques Specific, organized comments on major issues (theory, method, contribution) Organize by ISSUE, not page-by-page
4. Concluding Summary Final synthesis of key points Helps authors prioritize what to address

Review Technique Tips

  • Engage deeply: Skimming leads to inaccurate and unhelpful feedback
  • Be specific: "The contribution is insufficient" is NOT useful. Explain WHY and suggest alternatives
  • Substantiate critiques: Claims like "Hypothesis 1 seems trivial" must be justified with reasoning
  • Point to solutions: Suggest specific literature or alternative analytical approaches
How to Become a Reviewer
  1. Create an account in the journal's manuscript management system (e.g., mc.manuscriptcentral.com/isr)
  2. List your expertise accurately - AEs search by keywords, so listing your expertise correctly increases your chances of being invited
  3. Build your publication record - ISR is selective; invitations based on publications and conference interactions
  4. Network at conferences - AEs often invite reviewers they have met

Note: Because ISR is a prestigious journal, AEs are very selective about who they invite as reviewers.

Paper 3: Rebalancing Novelty with Rigor and Relevance

Sun, H., Wen, W., Thatcher, J. B., Chau, M., & Brown, S. A. (2025). Editor's comments: Rebalancing novelty with rigor and relevance. MIS Quarterly, 49(3), iii-xi.
0. Citation

Editorial from MIS Quarterly, often considered the top journal in IS research.

1. Goal

To address confusion about what "novelty" actually means and how it should be balanced with rigor and relevance.

2. Audience

Authors, reviewers, and editors in the IS community who need a clearer understanding of how to evaluate and demonstrate novelty.

3. Problem

The IS field often talks about the importance of making novel contributions, but this term is poorly defined and inconsistently applied. Different reviewers have different opinions, and authors struggle to satisfy everyone.

"Novelty should not be evaluated in isolation. Instead, it needs to be balanced with two other values: rigor (doing the research correctly) and relevance (making the research matter to someone)."
4. Research Questions

Not applicable (editorial). Implicit question: How should novelty be defined and evaluated across different research paradigms?

5. Resolution

The editors propose a triadic framework showing three interconnected values:

          NOVELTY
            /\
           /  \
          /    \
         /      \
        /        \
    RIGOR ------- RELEVANCE

All three values must be present, and tensions exist between each pair.

Novelty Across Research Paradigms

Paradigm Forms of Novelty
Behavioral IS New constructs, new relationships, new boundary conditions
Economics of IS Novel causal identification, new phenomena, new policy implications
Design Science Novel artifacts, new design principles, superior performance
Interpretive Fresh conceptual framings, new process theories, deeper understanding
6. Contribution

Constructs and Relationships: The triadic framework itself is a conceptual contribution. Also identifies four risks of novelty-seeking.

Four Risks of Novelty-Seeking

  1. Old Wine in New Bottles: Repackaging existing theories with new labels
  2. Methodological Fireworks: Using complex methods to appear sophisticated
  3. Overcontextualization: Findings too specific to generalize
  4. Fleeting Novelty: Technology-specific findings that will become obsolete
7. Guidelines

For Authors:

  • Triangulate findings to strengthen novel claims
  • Share methods and data for replication
  • Conduct a "contribution audit" to distinguish genuine novelty from hype
  • Focus on enduring knowledge, not fleeting novelty

For Reviewers:

  • Articulate what is novel and why it matters
  • Be paradigm-aware when applying standards
  • Balance skepticism with generosity
  • Ask "Can this work?" not "Have I seen this before?"
8. Paper Structure
  • The problem: Confusion about novelty
  • The triadic framework: Novelty, Rigor, Relevance
  • Novelty across paradigms: Different forms for different research types
  • Risks and tensions: What can go wrong
  • Strategies: Practical advice for authors and reviewers

Key Evaluative Question for Novelty

"Is this novelty substantively necessary for understanding the phenomenon?"

If the issue can be adequately explained using existing theories, the marginal gain of a novel framework may be limited. Novelty should arise from genuine need, not the desire to appear innovative.

Complete Definitions of Novelty, Rigor, Relevance (Exam Level)
Dimension Full Definition
Novelty The originality of concepts, relationships, or methods in a study, including theoretical extensions, new empirical phenomena, or design innovations
Rigor The theoretical and empirical validity of a study and its methodological soundness, involving coherence between theory, operationalization, data collection, measurement, and analysis
Relevance The practical or societal significance of the research, including salience to digital practice, stakeholder utility, or public policy implications
The Three Tensions (Detailed for Exam)

1. Novelty-Rigor Tension

The pursuit of novelty can undermine rigor in these ways:

  • Speculative theorizing without sufficient validation
  • Post-hoc theorizing ("theory laundering") - discovering patterns, then retroactively "explaining" them as if predicted
  • Jangle fallacies - introducing constructs only marginally distinct from existing ones (e.g., "digital trust" vs. plain "trust")
  • "Methodological fireworks without conceptual fire" - complex methods that don't add insight

2. Novelty-Relevance Tension

Novel findings may lack practical or theoretical significance:

  • "Old wine in new bottles" - repackaging existing theories with new labels
  • Studying marginal phenomena with little practical value
  • Overcontextualization - findings tied to local idiosyncrasies that don't transfer
  • Fleeting trends - focus on technology-specific findings with no enduring value

3. Rigor-Relevance Tension

Methodologically precise research may become unusable:

  • Research that is technically "correct" but inaccessible to practitioners
  • Findings that are valid but irrelevant to real-world decisions
  • Over-emphasis on internal validity at the cost of external validity

Research Paradigms and Their Criteria (for Review)
Paradigm Goal What Counts as Good Research
Positivist / Theory Testing Test causal relationships Rigorous methods, valid measures, statistical significance, replicability
Economics of IS Establish causal effects Clean identification strategy, natural experiments, robust econometrics
Design Science Create useful artifacts Novel design, demonstrated utility, clear design principles, rigorous evaluation
Interpretive / Qualitative Understand meaning Rich description, theoretical sensitivity, transferability (not generalizability)
Critical Research Expose power structures Reflexivity, emancipatory intent, challenge of status quo

Paradigm Awareness Warning

Do NOT apply criteria from one paradigm to evaluate papers from another.

Example: Do not criticize interpretive research for lacking statistical generalizability - that is not the goal of interpretive research.

What Makes a Sustainable Contribution?

Insights that provide lasting value can be:

Quality What It Means Example
REUSED Applied in new settings TAM being used for mobile apps, not just PCs
EXTENDED Built upon with additional data Adding moderators to existing relationships
CHALLENGED Tested with alternative explanations Finding boundary conditions where theory fails

Fleeting novelty = research focused on a specific technology that will become obsolete. Such work has limited lasting value.

Q1: What are the five elements every IS paper must communicate according to Baird?

According to Baird (2021), every IS theory paper must communicate five essential elements to be effective. First, Focus establishes the phenomena of interest and the theoretical lens through which it will be examined. Second, Background provides the theoretical and empirical context, positioning the paper within existing literature and establishing what "commonsense" knowledge exists.

Third, and most critically, Tension identifies the problem the paper addresses. Baird (2021) argues that the strongest tensions come from "assumption challenging" rather than mere "gap spotting." This means questioning fundamental assumptions in existing literature rather than simply noting what has not been studied. Fourth, Resolution presents the theoretical contribution that addresses the identified tension. This is the core value proposition of the paper.

Finally, Guidelines describe how the theory should be applied in future research or practice. These elements work together as a coherent narrative. Baird (2021) specifically recommends writing the body first (Background and Resolution) before crafting the introduction (Focus and Tension), as this "inside-out" approach helps authors develop clearer tensions after fully understanding their contribution.

Reference: Baird, A. (2021). How to write IS papers. Working paper, UNT.

Q2: Why is assumption challenging better than gap spotting?

Assessment challenging produces stronger theoretical contributions than gap spotting because it questions fundamental premises rather than merely identifying unexplored areas. According to Baird (2021), gap spotting follows the weak logic of "X has been studied, but not in context Y, so let us study X in Y." This approach often leads to incremental contributions that add little to theoretical understanding.

In contrast, assumption challenging identifies and questions the taken-for-granted beliefs underlying existing theory. Baird (2021) explains that assumptions operate quietly in the background, and by challenging them, researchers can reveal fundamental flaws in current thinking. For example, rather than studying "technology adoption in healthcare" (gap spotting), assumption challenging would question whether the adoption paradigm itself is appropriate when AI systems operate autonomously.

This distinction matters because journals increasingly reject papers with weak contributions. Sun et al. (2024) similarly argue that genuine novelty comes from generating new theoretical perspectives, not from applying existing frameworks to new contexts. They warn that "construct proliferation" and "data mining masquerading as theory" often result from gap spotting approaches. Assumption challenging is more difficult but yields contributions that fundamentally advance the field.

References: Baird, A. (2021). How to write IS papers; Sun, Y., et al. (2024). Understanding novelty, rigor, and relevance. MISQ.

Q3: What is the q-r framework, and why is a high-q, low-r paper more salvageable than a low-q, high-r paper?

The q-r framework, introduced by Sarker et al. (2023), evaluates manuscripts along two dimensions: the quality of the underlying idea (q) and the quality of execution or presentation (r). This framework helps reviewers and authors understand that these dimensions are independent and require different types of attention.

A high-q, low-r paper has a strong theoretical contribution but poor execution, such as unclear writing, incomplete analysis, or presentation issues. Such papers are more salvageable because the core value proposition exists and can be improved through revision. The authors can work on clarifying their arguments, strengthening their methodology, or improving the writing quality (Sarker et al., 2023).

Conversely, a low-q, high-r paper is beautifully written and methodologically sound but lacks a meaningful contribution. No amount of polishing can fix a fundamentally weak idea. As Sarker et al. (2023) note, such papers may appear professional but add little to theoretical or empirical knowledge. This is why senior scholars often advise focusing first on having something important to say before worrying about how to say it. The q-r framework reminds us that substance matters more than style, though both are ultimately necessary for publication.

Reference: Sarker, S., et al. (2023). How to review papers for ISR. ISR.

Q4: Why must reviewers be "paradigm-aware" when evaluating papers?

Reviewers must be paradigm-aware because different research paradigms operate under fundamentally different assumptions about knowledge, reality, and valid evidence. Sarker et al. (2023) emphasize that applying evaluation criteria from one paradigm to research conducted in another leads to unfair and inappropriate reviews.

For example, positivist research seeks statistical generalizability and hypothesis testing, while interpretive research aims for deep contextual understanding without claiming universal applicability. A reviewer trained only in positivist methods might incorrectly criticize an interpretive study for lacking statistical power or random sampling, when those criteria are irrelevant to the interpretive paradigm (Sarker et al., 2023).

Sun et al. (2024) reinforce this by showing how novelty manifests differently across paradigms. In behavioral research, novelty means new constructs or relationships. In design science, it means new artifacts or design principles. In interpretive research, it means new understanding of situated phenomena. A paradigm-unaware reviewer might fail to recognize legitimate contributions simply because they differ from familiar forms.

This awareness is particularly critical given that IS is a pluralistic field embracing multiple research traditions. Paradigm blindness can lead to Type I errors, where good papers are rejected, or Type II errors, where flawed papers are accepted because they superficially match familiar patterns (Sarker et al., 2023).

References: Sarker, S., et al. (2023). How to review papers for ISR; Sun, Y., et al. (2024). Understanding novelty, rigor, and relevance. MISQ.

Q5: What are the four risks of novelty-seeking according to Sun et al.?

Sun et al. (2024) identify four significant risks associated with overemphasizing novelty in IS research. First, construct proliferation occurs when researchers create new constructs that are conceptually redundant with existing ones, adding to theoretical confusion rather than clarity. The desire to appear novel drives scholars to relabel established concepts rather than building on prior work.

Second, data mining masquerading as theory happens when researchers analyze data extensively, find statistically significant but theoretically unexplained relationships, and then construct post-hoc theoretical justifications. This reverses the proper relationship between theory and data, making findings appear more theoretically grounded than they actually are.

Third, studying things that do not matter results from prioritizing novelty over relevance. Researchers may study esoteric phenomena that no one has examined precisely because those phenomena lack practical or theoretical importance. Novelty alone does not equal value.

Fourth, ignoring prior research inappropriately occurs when authors dismiss relevant literature to make their contribution appear more novel. This intellectual dishonesty harms cumulative knowledge building and often leads to "reinventing the wheel" without acknowledgment.

These risks highlight why novelty must be balanced with rigor and relevance, not pursued in isolation (Sun et al., 2024).

Reference: Sun, Y., et al. (2024). Understanding novelty, rigor, and relevance. MISQ.

Q6: How does novelty manifest differently across research paradigms?

Sun et al. (2024) explain that novelty takes different forms depending on the research paradigm, and scholars must evaluate novelty using paradigm-appropriate criteria.

In behavioral research (positivist paradigm), novelty typically manifests as new constructs, new relationships between existing constructs, new theoretical mechanisms explaining known relationships, or new boundary conditions that specify when relationships hold. The emphasis is on advancing testable, generalizable theory.

In design science research, novelty appears as new artifacts (tools, systems, methods), new design principles or patterns, or new evaluation frameworks. The contribution lies in demonstrating that an artifact works and generates prescriptive knowledge about how to create effective solutions.

In interpretive research, novelty emerges through new understanding of particular phenomena in their context, new conceptual frameworks for making sense of complex situations, or new insights into meaning-making processes. The focus is on illuminating rather than predicting.

This paradigm-specific understanding of novelty is essential for both authors and reviewers. Authors must demonstrate novelty in ways appropriate to their chosen approach, while reviewers must recognize legitimate novelty even when it differs from familiar forms (Sun et al., 2024). Applying behavioral novelty expectations to design science or interpretive work leads to inappropriate evaluations.

Reference: Sun, Y., et al. (2024). Understanding novelty, rigor, and relevance. MISQ.

Q7: What practical strategies can authors use to demonstrate genuine novelty?

Sun et al. (2024) and Baird (2021) offer several practical strategies for demonstrating genuine novelty in IS research.

First, authors should engage deeply with existing theory rather than superficially citing prior work. This means understanding the theoretical assumptions, boundary conditions, and limitations of previous research, which enables meaningful extension rather than mere replication in new contexts.

Second, challenge assumptions explicitly. Identify what existing literature takes for granted and question whether those assumptions hold. As Baird (2021) notes, assumptions operate quietly and rarely get examined, making them fertile ground for theoretical contribution.

Third, borrow from adjacent disciplines thoughtfully. Importing concepts from sociology, psychology, economics, or philosophy can generate new perspectives, but this requires genuine integration rather than superficial application (Sun et al., 2024).

Fourth, attend to phenomena carefully. Sun et al. (2024) argue that deep engagement with empirical phenomena often reveals theoretical inadequacies. New technologies like generative AI create genuinely novel situations that existing theories may not adequately explain.

Fifth, position contributions clearly. Authors must articulate exactly how their work differs from and advances prior literature, making the novelty explicit rather than leaving readers to infer it. This involves both showing what is similar (to establish legitimacy) and what is different (to establish novelty).

References: Baird, A. (2021). How to write IS papers; Sun, Y., et al. (2024). Understanding novelty, rigor, and relevance. MISQ.

SC1: Define Novelty, Rigor, Relevance and their tensions

Novelty refers to generating new theoretical perspectives, concepts, or relationships that advance understanding beyond existing knowledge. Rigor involves demonstrating that ideas were developed and tested using appropriate methods for the research paradigm. Relevance means the research matters to practitioners, policymakers, or society (Sun et al., 2024).

These three desiderata exist in tension. The novelty-rigor tension arises because novel ideas may lack established methodological templates. The novelty-relevance tension occurs when novel theoretical contributions seem abstract and disconnected from practice. The rigor-relevance tension emerges when rigorous methods create findings too narrow or technical for practical application (Sun et al., 2024). Balancing these tensions requires conscious tradeoffs that authors must navigate thoughtfully.

Reference: Sun, Y., et al. (2024). Understanding novelty, rigor, and relevance. MISQ.

SC2: Conference vs Journal for career

Conferences and journals serve different purposes in an academic career. Conferences like ICIS, AMCIS, and ECIS allow researchers to present work-in-progress, receive feedback, and build networks. They offer faster turnaround and visibility but carry less weight for tenure and promotion (Sun et al., 2024).

Journals, particularly those in the AIS Basket of 11 (MISQ, ISR, JAIS, JMIS, ISJ, EJIS, I&M, JIT, JSIS, I&O, DSS), are essential for tenure at research universities. Journal publications undergo rigorous peer review, often taking 2-3 years from submission to publication. For PhD students and early-career faculty, building a pipeline of journal publications is critical (Sarker et al., 2023). The strategy is: present at conferences for feedback, then develop into journal submissions.

References: Sun, Y., et al. (2024); Sarker, S., et al. (2023).

SC3: Which journals are on FT50

The Financial Times Top 50 (FT50) is a list of academic journals used for ranking business schools based on faculty publications. Only two IS journals are included: MIS Quarterly (MISQ) and Information Systems Research (ISR).

This limited representation has implications for IS scholars at business schools where FT50 publications are emphasized for tenure. Other highly regarded IS journals like JAIS, JMIS, ISJ, and EJIS are not on the FT50, despite being in the AIS Basket of 11 senior scholars' list. This creates pressure for IS researchers to publish in MISQ or ISR specifically, or to target journals in adjacent fields like Management Science or Organization Science that are on the FT50 (Sun et al., 2024). Understanding your institution's publication expectations is essential for career planning.

Reference: Sun, Y., et al. (2024).

SC4: Level of Analysis (individual, organizational, multi-level)

Level of analysis refers to the unit at which phenomena are studied and theorized. Individual level examines how people adopt, use, or are affected by technology, such as TAM or UTAUT studies. Organizational level studies how firms implement, manage, or benefit from IS, including IT governance and strategic alignment research (Sarker et al., 2023).

Multi-level research recognizes that phenomena operate across levels simultaneously. For example, individual technology use affects team performance, which influences organizational outcomes. Multi-level theorizing requires careful attention to cross-level effects and appropriate aggregation or disaggregation of constructs. Reviewers should evaluate whether authors have clearly specified their level of analysis and avoided inappropriately mixing levels without theoretical justification (Sarker et al., 2023). Misspecified levels of analysis is a common methodological problem.

Reference: Sarker, S., et al. (2023). How to review papers for ISR.

SC5: Why deep engagement with theory is important

Deep engagement with theory is essential because it enables genuine theoretical contribution rather than superficial application. Baird (2021) argues that researchers must understand not just what prior theories say, but their underlying assumptions, boundary conditions, and limitations. This deep understanding allows researchers to identify meaningful tensions worth addressing.

Without deep engagement, researchers risk "construct proliferation," creating new constructs that are conceptually identical to existing ones (Sun et al., 2024). They may also fail to recognize when their findings contradict or extend existing theory in important ways. Deep theoretical engagement also helps position contributions appropriately, showing how new work relates to and advances prior literature. Surface-level citation of theories without genuine understanding leads to weak papers that add little to cumulative knowledge building.

References: Baird, A. (2021); Sun, Y., et al. (2024).

SC6: Four risks of novelty-seeking

Sun et al. (2024) identify four risks when researchers overemphasize novelty:

1. Construct proliferation: Creating new constructs conceptually redundant with existing ones, adding confusion rather than clarity.

2. Data mining masquerading as theory: Finding statistically significant relationships first, then constructing post-hoc theoretical justifications.

3. Studying things that do not matter: Investigating esoteric phenomena precisely because no one has studied them, ignoring that their obscurity reflects irrelevance.

4. Ignoring prior research inappropriately: Dismissing relevant literature to make contributions appear more novel, harming cumulative knowledge building.

These risks highlight why novelty must be balanced with rigor and relevance rather than pursued in isolation.

Reference: Sun, Y., et al. (2024). Understanding novelty, rigor, and relevance. MISQ.

SC7: Reviewer role metaphors

Sarker et al. (2023) describe several metaphors for the reviewer role, each with different implications:

Gatekeeper: Protecting the field from low-quality work. Risk: may be too conservative, rejecting innovative but unconventional papers.

Judge: Rendering verdicts on manuscript quality. Risk: may focus on flaws rather than potential.

Mentor/Coach: Helping authors improve their work. This is the recommended approach, providing constructive feedback that guides revision even for papers that cannot be accepted.

Collaborator: Working with authors to advance knowledge. Appropriate but must maintain objectivity.

The mentor metaphor is preferred because it balances rigor with developmental support, helping the field grow while maintaining standards (Sarker et al., 2023).

Reference: Sarker, S., et al. (2023). How to review papers for ISR.

SC8: Type I and Type II errors in review

Sarker et al. (2023) apply statistical error terminology to the review process:

Type I Error (False Positive): Accepting a paper that should have been rejected. This allows flawed or trivial research into the literature, potentially misleading future researchers and practitioners.

Type II Error (False Negative): Rejecting a paper that should have been accepted. This prevents valuable contributions from reaching the field, potentially discouraging innovative researchers.

Both errors are costly to the field. Type I errors pollute the literature; Type II errors impede progress. Reviewers tend toward risk aversion, making Type II errors more common, especially for paradigm-challenging work. Good reviewing requires balancing these risks, being neither too lenient (risking Type I) nor too harsh (risking Type II). The q-r framework helps: high-q papers deserve multiple chances even with execution problems (Sarker et al., 2023).

Reference: Sarker, S., et al. (2023). How to review papers for ISR.

پنج عنصر یک مقاله (Baird)

هر مقاله IS باید پنج چیز را واضح بیان کند:

  1. حوزه تمرکز (Focus): مخاطب شما کیست و چرا باید اهمیت دهند. مثال: "این مقاله برای محققانی است که روی اعتماد به AI کار می‌کنند."
  2. پیشینه (Background): آنچه در حال حاضر شناخته شده است. خواننده را از دانسته‌ها به نادانسته‌ها هدایت کنید.
  3. تنش نظری (Tension): چرا به تحقیق جدید نیاز است. این مهم‌ترین بخش است!
  4. راه حل (Resolution): رویکرد شما برای حل مشکل + تئوری جدیدی که ارائه می‌دهید.
  5. راهنما (Guidelines): چگونه دیگران می‌توانند از کار شما استفاده کنند + محدودیت‌ها + تحقیقات آینده.

نخ قرمز و ساعت شنی (Red Thread & Hourglass)

نخ قرمز: مقاله باید یک خط منطقی پیوسته داشته باشد. هر پاراگراف باید به قبلی وصل باشد. اگر یک پاراگراف را حذف کنید و مقاله هنوز معنی‌دار باشد، آن پاراگراف نباید وجود داشته باشد!

ساعت شنی:

  • شروع: پهن (آنچه مخاطب می‌داند)
  • وسط: باریک (کار خاص شما)
  • پایان: پهن (کاربردها و تعمیم‌ها)

چارچوب q-r (Sarker)

کیفیت مقاله دو بعد دارد:

  • q = کیفیت ایده: آیا سوال تحقیق مهم است؟ آیا مشارکت ارزشمند است؟
  • r = کیفیت اجرا: آیا روش‌ها صحیح هستند؟ آیا ارائه واضح است؟
q بالا، r پایین → قابل نجات! ایده خوب، اجرای ضعیف. داور کمک می‌کند.
q پایین، r بالا → رد! ایده ضعیف، روش عالی. روش نمی‌تواند ایده بد را نجات دهد.

اخلاق داوری (مهم!)

  • محرمانگی: محتوای مقاله محرمانه است. با کسی بحث نکنید!
  • هوش مصنوعی ممنوع: استفاده از ChatGPT یا مشابه برای داوری نامناسب است.
  • گزارش تخلف: اگر plagiarism دیدید، به ویراستار بگویید، نه در review بنویسید.
  • ما با آنها: ذهنیت "us-with-them" داشته باشید، نه "us-versus-them".
  • ۲۴ ساعت صبر کنید: review را بنویسید، ۲۴ ساعت صبر کنید، دوباره بخوانید تا لحن مناسب باشد.

سه‌گانه نوآوری-دقت-ارتباط (Sun)

همه تحقیقات خوب باید سه چیز را متعادل کنند:

  • نوآوری (Novelty): چه چیز جدیدی ارائه می‌دهید؟ (مفاهیم، روابط، روش‌ها)
  • دقت (Rigor): آیا روش‌های شما صحیح و قابل اعتماد هستند؟
  • ارتباط (Relevance): چه کسی اهمیت می‌دهد؟ چرا مهم است؟

خطرات:

  • نوآوری بدون دقت = ادعاهای بی‌اساس
  • دقت بدون ارتباط = تحقیق بی‌فایده
  • نوآوری بدون ارتباط = "شراب کهنه در بطری نو"

چهار خطر نوآوری‌طلبی

  1. شراب کهنه در بطری نو: تغییر نام تئوری قدیمی بدون اضافه کردن چیز جدید
  2. آتش‌بازی روش‌شناختی: استفاده از روش پیچیده فقط برای جلب توجه
  3. زمینه‌محوری بیش از حد: نتایج فقط در یک شرکت خاص کار می‌کند
  4. نوآوری زودگذر: مطالعه یک اپ خاص که ۲ سال دیگر منسوخ می‌شود

شکاف‌یابی در مقابل چالش مفروضات

شکاف‌یابی (Gap Spotting) - ضعیف:

"هیچ‌کس X را در زمینه Y مطالعه نکرده است."

مشکل: صرف اینکه چیزی مطالعه نشده به این معنی نیست که مهم است!

چالش مفروضات (Assumption Challenging) - قوی:

"این حوزه فرض می‌کند کاربران همگن هستند، اما در واقع ناهمگنی وجود دارد."

قوی است چون مستقیماً نشان می‌دهد چرا کار قبلی ممکن است ناکامل باشد.

F.I.R.S.T. چارچوب ارزیابی

  • F = Fit: آیا به ژورنال IS می‌خورد؟
  • I = Interestingness: چرا جامعه علمی باید اهمیت دهد؟
  • R = Rigor: آیا روش‌ها مناسب و شفاف هستند؟
  • S = Story: آیا داستان منسجم و قانع‌کننده است؟
  • T = Theory: آیا "چرا" و "چگونه" را توضیح می‌دهد؟

  1. اول: Background و Theory (جوهره کار شما)
  2. دوم: Guidelines و Conclusion
  3. سوم: Abstract
  4. آخر: Introduction (سخت‌ترین بخش! وقتی می‌دانید مقاله چیست راحت‌تر است)
